Chapter Six

Grace, Not a Shortcut

✦ ✦ ✦

≈ 12 min read

I PRAYED FOR THE same thing for ten years. Those years taught me that delay need not be empty time. Grace can form the person who waits even while the desired door stays shut, and the road toward an answer may become part of what God gives.

As a young man I became convinced that my future lay in a country on the other side of the world, because the work I believed I was made to do seemed to require crossing an ocean. I had no practical means of getting there. More than once I asked a travel agent for the fare, and it was more than I could have saved in years.

The agent was kind in the way people are kind when they expect never to see you again. So I made another plan. I would cross by ship and work for my passage, perhaps washing dishes or sweeping floors during the two‑month journey. I had no idea whether such arrangements still existed; it was the only process I could imagine.

For ten years I asked God to open that door, and I arranged my life around the hope. I avoided courtship, though I wanted marriage, because I believed I had to cross the ocean first. My friends laughed. One said, “You know why it is called a dream, don’t you? Because it only happens when you are asleep.” The line stayed funny for several years. Around the seventh, I stopped laughing. Through most of that decade, heaven gave no answer I could recognize.

I often prayed at four in the morning while eating my first meal at a food stall on a bridge. The river below was filthy because the neighborhood used it as a dump, and chickens shrieked as they were slaughtered for the morning market. That bridge became my chapel. In the dark, my prayer narrowed to one sentence borrowed from the story of a boy in the Gospels: “Lord, all I have are five loaves and two fish.”

Nothing about the place looked sacred. The smell of the river, the noise from the market, and the cheap meal all belonged to ordinary poverty. Yet prayer does not wait for ideal surroundings. God met me within the life I actually had, and the bridge became holy through repeated attention to Him.

The hour also mattered. At four in the morning the future was invisible; I could not see the far country, and sometimes I could barely see the water below. Prayer took place before evidence. Each dawn trained the body to confess what the mind often doubted, that God was worthy of the first attention of the day.

Andrew had found the only food among the crowd: “There is a lad here who has five barley loaves and two fish; but what are they among so many?” (John 6:9). His arithmetic matched mine. What could one poor student’s prayer do against an ocean and a fare he could never pay?

The boy still placed the food in Jesus’ hands, and my prayer did the same with the little I possessed. God already knew the inventory; I was learning to offer what I had instead of living only from what I lacked. Each morning, before light reached the dirty water, I gave Him the small life in my hands.

I used to think of those ten years as a corridor, with real life waiting in the room at the end. The years turned out to be part of the room. God was not withholding grace until the answer arrived. Grace was at work the whole time, in the jobs I took, the limits I lived under, the friendships, the prayer, and the endurance itself. Delay became a school of providence, though I did not recognize the lessons while taking them.

But we must be careful here. We cannot say that every delayed answer means a person is unready, because that explanation can blame sufferers for circumstances they did not create. What we can say is that no time entrusted to God is beyond His power to redeem, and that formation may occur during a delay without explaining why the delay was permitted.

ISRAEL’S WILDERNESS GIVES FORMATION a biblical shape. God brought the people through the sea and then led them into the desert, where freedom exposed fears that slavery had concealed. When they were hungry, they complained, and when they were thirsty, they turned bitter. Danger made them homesick for Egypt. Slavery had been terrible, but it was predictable, and their bodies had left Egypt while Egypt still lived in their instincts. Deliverance happened in a night; learning to live as a free people took much longer.

This pattern appears throughout the spiritual life. God can forgive in an instant while habits formed by sin require long healing, and a person may leave an oppressive situation and still carry its fears. The gift is real from the beginning, yet learning to live inside it takes time.

Israel’s complaints were therefore more than bad manners; they revealed a people who had not learned to trust daily provision. Egypt had offered stored grain under a tyrant, and the wilderness offered manna that could not be hoarded, because God was teaching them to receive life each day rather than secure it apart from Him.

Freedom without trust soon reaches backward for bondage, and the desert exposed that danger before the people entered a land where prosperity would create new temptations. Their waiting was tied to a covenant vocation, for they were being formed to live as God’s people among the nations.

At the end of the forty years, Moses told them plainly what the desert had been for:

And you shall remember all the way which the Lord your God has led you these forty years in the wilderness, that he might humble you, testing you to know what was in your heart, whether you would keep his commandments, or not. And he humbled you and let you hunger and fed you with manna, which you did not know, nor did your fathers know; that he might make you know that man does not live by bread alone, but that man lives by everything that proceeds out of the mouth of the Lord. (Deuteronomy 8:2‑3)

Moses names the wilderness as God’s leading, humbling, testing, and feeding. God already knew the people’s hearts; it was the people who needed to know themselves. Hunger revealed their dependence, manna taught that life rests upon God’s word, and the wilderness exposed what slavery had formed while beginning to form a people capable of covenant freedom.

Readiness is deeper than a confident feeling. At conferences someone asks, “Are you ready?” and every hand goes up. I have never heard a room answer no. The sincerity is real. The definition may be thin.

Kingdom readiness means the capacity to receive a gift without being mastered by it. Leadership requires endurance, humility, and freedom from applause; influence can damage a person who cannot bear criticism, and responsibility exposes every weakness it enlarges. These capacities usually develop through ordinary obedience, through correction and failure and hidden work.

Yet readiness does not purchase God’s gifts, for grace cannot be earned. Formation removes some of the obstacles that keep us from receiving it well. God gives Himself first, and our capacity to live from that gift grows over time.

Christian growth always keeps that order. God acts, and we respond, and His action awakens and sustains the response. Discipline matters, but discipline does not manufacture divine life. Prayer, work, and endurance dispose us to receive what grace alone can give.

The order protects formation from pride. The person who has waited ten years cannot present the decade as payment, for time itself has no merit. If waiting produces patience, the patience is fruit of grace working through consent, failure, repentance, and renewed consent, and we arrive able to carry more while knowing that the strength was received.

It also guards those who feel little growth. Formation is often hidden, roots deepening before the branches show change, and a person may notice mainly fatigue while others see greater gentleness, steadiness, and compassion. God is a better judge of the work than the person being formed.

Delay changed me in ways I could name only afterward. I learned to live on little and waste nothing; I completed my studies, accepted available work, and kept my hands occupied. Faithful waiting is active, tending present duties while leaving the future with God.

The decade also broke my confidence in the script I had written. I felt certain that God would send me, certain of what I would accomplish, and certain of the story’s final shape. Some of those convictions proved true and some did not, and waiting separated trust in God from trust in my plan for God.

The two can feel identical, since we use God’s name while defending our preferred future. Delay exposes the difference. Circumstances revise the script, and faith must decide whether it was attached to God or to a religious forecast. By the seventh year my own certainty could not take correction anymore, and I am not sure certainty like that is trust at all.

I did not pray nobly every morning. Often I wanted a shortcut: ten years compressed into ten weeks, the bridge and the filthy river and the shrieking chickens all removed from the story. Much later, I found myself praying the words I wish I had known then:

Give me grace, O Lord, not a shortcut.

The prayer asks for grace within the actual road. A shortcut promises relief from the process that forms endurance, humility, and wisdom, yet difficulty has no sanctifying power by itself; hardship can also injure and embitter. Grace makes the difference by joining the person to Christ and enabling a free response of faith and love.

Grace is God’s self‑gift rather than a kind of spiritual toughness, and it heals and renews us while circumstances stay difficult. We respond by continuing in prayer, work, repentance, and love, and even that response is sustained by the gift already given, for God works in us both to will and to act (Philippians 2:13).

Christ accepted the full road of obedience and refused to use divine power as an escape from the Father’s saving purpose. His Cross is unique; our delays do not reproduce it. But they can be united to His obedience, and His grace can keep us faithful when we cannot shorten the road.

Waiting therefore differs from passivity. Noah built the ark nail by nail with the flood nowhere in sight. Israel gathered manna day by day. The Christian attends to the obedience in front of him without demanding control over the result, and grace often arrives as strength for the next faithful act.

THE DOOR FINALLY OPENED through a route I had never imagined. My studies were complete, and nothing had changed when a friend studying abroad got in touch after years of silence. We began a small project together by letters and telephone calls, and eventually he said that he wanted to visit me.

“Why?” I asked him. “We can finish this the way we started it, by mail and by phone.”

“I just want to complete it alongside you,” he said.

I joked, “Instead of you coming all this way, why don’t I come to you?”

He took the joke seriously. He knew about the ten years, and he told me to apply properly and state my purpose. If permission came, he would support my stay and buy the ticket.

Permission came. He bought the ticket. After years of imagining ships, dishwater, and a two‑month passage, I crossed the ocean in one day without paying the fare. The answer came through a friendship I had never thought to include in my prayer, and none of my imagined mechanics mattered.

The friendship had developed through ordinary work: letters, telephone calls, a project completed across the distance. I had treated those tasks as something to do while waiting for real life, and Providence used them to prepare the very relationship through which the door opened.

The way the door opened changed how I understood answers. God often works through created means and human generosity, and the gift is still His without making the friend less free or the practical process less real. Providence does not compete with ordinary causes. It gathers them into a purpose none of us could see in advance.

My friend’s offer also made the answer personal. I had imagined money appearing or a ship accepting my labor. Instead God sent help through someone who knew the story and chose to stand behind me, so the answer came as communion, not anonymous provision.

I had behaved like the man at Bethesda who knew only one route into the healing water, while God’s providence was never confined to the process I understood. The answer did not vindicate my plan. If anything, it showed me how little I had been able to imagine.

The ten years of that waiting have their verse in Isaiah, the one people embroider on cushions, and I understand why, since it is beautiful. What is less noticed is how precise it is:

but they who wait for the Lord
shall renew their strength,
they shall mount up with wings like eagles,
they shall run and not be weary,
they shall walk and not faint. (Isaiah 40:31)

Isaiah places renewed strength within the waiting, and the waiting is directed toward the LORD Himself, His character and word. Waiting only for an outcome wears the soul down, because attention stays fixed on what is not there. Waiting upon God is different. He is there.

The promised strength has several forms. Sometimes faith mounts up. Sometimes it runs. Often it walks without fainting. The final image may be the most useful, because long obedience is usually less dramatic than flight, and grace supplies enough strength for the pace the present road requires.

Boarding the airplane felt quieter than I had expected. There were no trumpets, only the recognition of a key entering the lock for which it had been cut. On the other side of the ocean I knew one person. Everything learned on the bridge became useful: thrift, discipline, prayer in darkness, and trust that had survived laughter. None of those gifts earned the journey, but they helped me inhabit it once it came.

This story ended in a visible Yes. Many waits do not. Some readers have waited longer than ten years for healing, for reconciliation, for a child, and a lifelong refusal cannot be explained by saying the person never became ready. Chapter 8 will face that different sorrow. Here I can offer Paul’s words: “And let us not grow weary in well‑doing, for in due season we shall reap, if we do not lose heart” (Galatians 6:9).

Paul promises a harvest while leaving the season with God. The harvest includes every fruit of grace produced by faithful love, not merely the particular outcome we requested. Some of that fruit appears within history, and its fullness belongs to the Kingdom, where no act of love offered in Christ is lost.

Hope is wider than the outcome we asked for, but it is not vague. A person may still ask for the particular healing or reconciliation or opportunity, yet Christian hope also looks beyond the limits of the present outcome. Resurrection means that God can gather every faithful act into a future larger than death.

The promise of harvest therefore calls us to continue doing good, for waiting does not suspend vocation. We can love the person before us, keep a promise, complete honest work, and pray today’s prayer. These acts may appear unrelated to the answer we seek, but in God’s Kingdom no faithfulness is wasted.

Due season belongs to God because the harvest belongs to Him; we are neither its owners nor its engineers. We sow and water within the time given to us, and God gives the growth according to a wisdom that embraces the individual soul, the community, history, and eternity.

The ten years also contained mornings when prayer itself felt heavier than anything else I carried onto the bridge, and no book had warned me about them. The plain name for that weight is disappointment.
