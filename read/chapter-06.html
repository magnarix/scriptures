Chapter Six

Grace, Not a Shortcut

✦ ✦ ✦

≈ 12 min read

I PRAYED FOR THE same thing for ten years. Those years taught me that delay need not be empty time. Grace can form the person who waits, even while the desired door remains closed. The road toward an answer may become part of what God gives.

As a young man, I became convinced that my future lay in a country on the other side of the world. The work I believed I was made to do seemed to require crossing an ocean. I had no practical means of getting there. More than once I asked a travel agent for the fare. The amount exceeded what I could have saved in years.

The agent was kind in the way people are kind when they expect never to see you again. I made another plan. I would cross by ship and work for my passage, perhaps washing dishes or sweeping floors during the two‑month journey. I had no idea whether such arrangements still existed. It was the only process I could imagine.

For ten years I asked God to open that door. I arranged my life around the hope. I avoided courtship, though I wanted marriage, because I believed I had to cross the ocean first. My friends laughed. One said, “You know why it is called a dream, don’t you? Because it only happens when you are asleep.” The line remained funny for several years. Around the seventh, I stopped laughing. Through most of that decade, heaven gave no answer I could recognize.

I often prayed at four in the morning while eating my first meal at a food stall on a bridge. The river below was filthy because the neighborhood used it as a dump. Chickens shrieked as they were slaughtered for the morning market. That bridge became my chapel. In the dark, my prayer narrowed to one sentence borrowed from a boy in the Gospels: “Lord, all I have are five loaves and two fish.”

Nothing about the place looked sacred. The smell of the river, the noise from the market, and the cheap meal belonged to ordinary poverty. Yet prayer does not wait for ideal surroundings. God met me within the life I actually possessed. The bridge became holy through repeated attention to Him.

The hour also mattered. At four in the morning, the future remained invisible. I could not see the far country, and sometimes I could barely see the water below. Prayer took place before evidence. Each dawn trained the body to confess what the mind often doubted: God remained worthy of the first attention of the day.

Andrew had found the only food among the crowd: “There is a lad here who has five barley loaves and two small fish, but what are they among so many?” (John 6:9 NKJV). His arithmetic matched mine. What could one poor student’s prayer do against an ocean and a fare he could never pay?

The boy still placed the food in Jesus’ hands. My prayer did the same with the little I possessed. God already knew the inventory. I was learning to offer what I had instead of living only from what I lacked. Each morning, before light reached the dirty water, I gave Him the small life in my hands.

I once treated those ten years as a corridor outside the room where life would begin. I now see them as part of the room itself. God was not withholding grace until the answer arrived. Grace was already active in work, limits, friendships, prayer, and endurance. Delay became a school of providence, though I did not recognize the lessons while taking them.

This claim needs restraint. We cannot say that every delayed answer means a person is unready. Such an explanation can blame sufferers for circumstances they did not create. We can say that no time entrusted to God is beyond His power to redeem. Formation may occur during delay without explaining why the delay was permitted.

Israel’s wilderness gives formation a biblical shape. God brought the people through the sea and then led them into the desert. Freedom exposed fears that slavery had concealed. Hunger produced complaint, thirst produced bitterness, and danger awakened nostalgia for Egypt. Slavery had been terrible, but it was predictable. Their bodies had left Egypt while Egypt remained in their instincts. Deliverance happened in a night. Learning to live as a free people took much longer.

This pattern appears throughout the spiritual life. God can forgive in an instant while habits formed by sin require long healing. A person may leave an oppressive situation and still carry its fears. The gift is real from the beginning, yet learning to live inside the gift takes time.

Israel’s complaints were therefore more than bad manners. They revealed a people who had not learned to trust daily provision. Egypt offered stored grain under a tyrant. The wilderness offered manna that could not be hoarded. God was teaching them to receive life each day rather than secure it apart from Him.

Freedom without trust soon reaches backward for bondage. The desert exposed that danger before the people entered a land where prosperity would create new temptations. Their waiting was tied to a covenant vocation: they were being formed to live as God’s people among the nations.

At the end of the forty years, Moses told them plainly what the desert had been for:

And you shall remember that the LORD your God led you all the way these forty years in the wilderness, to humble you and test you, to know what was in your heart, whether you would keep His commandments or not. So He humbled you, allowed you to hunger, and fed you with manna which you did not know nor did your fathers know, that He might make you know that man shall not live by bread alone; but man lives by every word that proceeds from the mouth of the LORD. (Deuteronomy 8:2‑3)

Moses names the wilderness as God’s leading, humbling, testing, and feeding. God already knew the people’s hearts. The people needed to know themselves. Hunger revealed their dependence, and manna taught that life rests upon God’s word. The wilderness exposed what slavery had formed and began forming a people capable of covenant freedom.

Readiness is deeper than a confident feeling. At conferences, someone asks, “Are you ready?” and every hand goes up. I have never heard a room answer no. The sincerity is real. The definition may be thin.

Kingdom readiness means the capacity to receive a gift without being mastered by it. Leadership requires endurance, humility, and freedom from applause. Influence can damage a person who cannot bear criticism. Responsibility exposes every weakness it enlarges. These capacities usually develop through ordinary obedience, correction, failure, and hidden work.

Still, readiness does not purchase God’s gifts. Grace remains unearned. Formation removes some of the obstacles that keep us from receiving grace well. God gives Himself first; our capacity to live from that gift grows over time.

Christian growth always keeps this order. God acts, and we respond. His action awakens and sustains the response. Discipline matters, but discipline does not manufacture divine life. Prayer, work, and endurance dispose us to receive what grace alone can give.

This protects formation from pride. The person who has waited ten years cannot present the decade as payment. Time itself has no merit. If waiting produces patience, the patience is fruit of grace working through consent, failure, repentance, and renewed consent. We arrive able to carry more while knowing that the strength was received.

It also protects those who feel little growth. Formation is often hidden. Roots deepen before branches show change. A person may notice mainly fatigue while others see greater gentleness, steadiness, and compassion. God is a better judge of the work than the person being formed.

Delay changed me in ways I could name only afterward. I learned to live on little and waste nothing. I completed my studies, accepted available work, and kept my hands occupied. Faithful waiting is active. It tends present duties while leaving the future with God.

The decade also broke my confidence in the script I had written. I felt certain that God would send me, certain of what I would accomplish, and certain of the story’s final shape. Some convictions proved true. Others did not. Waiting separated trust in God from trust in my plan for God.

The two can feel identical. We use God’s name while defending our preferred future. Delay exposes the difference because circumstances revise the script. Faith must then decide whether it was attached to God or to a religious forecast. A certainty too complete to receive correction has stopped behaving like trust.

I did not pray nobly every morning. Often I wanted a shortcut. I wanted ten years compressed into ten weeks and the bridge, filthy river, and shrieking chickens removed from the story. Much later, I found myself praying the words I wish I had known then:

Give me grace, O Lord, not a shortcut.

The prayer asks for grace within the actual road. A shortcut promises relief from the process that forms endurance, humility, and wisdom. Yet difficulty has no sanctifying power by itself. Hardship can also injure and embitter. Grace makes the difference by joining the person to Christ and enabling a free response of faith and love.

Grace is God’s self‑gift, not spiritual toughness. It heals and elevates our nature while circumstances remain difficult. We cooperate by continuing in prayer, work, repentance, and love. Even that cooperation is sustained by the gift already given.

Christ accepted the full road of obedience. He refused to use divine power as an escape from the Father’s saving purpose. His Cross remains unique; our delays do not reproduce it. They can be united to His obedience, and His grace can keep us faithful when we cannot shorten the road.

Waiting therefore differs from passivity. Noah built while the flood remained unseen. Israel gathered manna day by day. The Christian attends to today’s obedience without demanding control over tomorrow’s result. Grace often arrives as strength for the next faithful act.

The door finally opened through a route I had never imagined. My studies were complete, and nothing had changed. Meanwhile, a friend studying abroad contacted me after years of silence. We began a small project together by letters and telephone calls. Eventually he said that he wanted to visit me.

“Why?” I asked him. “We can finish this the way we started it, by mail and by phone.”

“I just want to complete it alongside you,” he said.

I joked, “Instead of you coming all this way, why don’t I come to you?”

He took the joke seriously. He knew about the ten years. He told me to apply properly and state my purpose. He would support my stay and buy the ticket if permission came.

Permission came. He bought the ticket. After years of imagining ships, dishwater, and a two‑month passage, I crossed the ocean in one day without paying the fare. The answer came through a friendship I had never thought to include in my prayer. None of my imagined mechanics mattered.

The friendship had developed through ordinary work. We exchanged letters, made telephone calls, and completed a project across the distance. I had treated those tasks as something to do while waiting for real life. Providence used them to prepare the very relationship through which the door opened.

This changed how I understood answers. God often works through created means and human generosity. The gift remains His without making the friend less free or the practical process less real. Providence does not compete with ordinary causes. It gathers them into a purpose no single participant can see in advance.

My friend’s offer also made the answer personal. I had imagined money appearing or a ship accepting my labor. God sent help through someone who knew the story and chose to stand behind me. The answer carried the form of communion rather than anonymous provision.

I had behaved like the man at Bethesda who knew only one route into the healing water. God’s providence was never confined to the process I understood. The answer did not validate my plan. It revealed how little of the possible I had been able to see.

The ten years of that waiting have their verse in Isaiah, one people embroider on cushions, and I understand why, since it is beautiful. What is less noticed is how precise it is:

But those who wait on the LORD
Shall renew their strength;
They shall mount up with wings like eagles,
They shall run and not be weary,
They shall walk and not faint. (Isaiah 40:31)

Isaiah places renewed strength within the waiting. The waiting is directed toward the LORD Himself, His character and word. Waiting only for an outcome drains the soul because attention remains fixed on what is absent. Waiting upon God keeps returning attention to the One who is present.

The promised strength has several forms. Sometimes faith mounts up. Sometimes it runs. Often it walks without fainting. The final image may be the most useful because long obedience is usually less dramatic than flight. Grace supplies enough strength for the pace the present road requires.

Boarding the airplane felt quieter than I had expected. There were no trumpets. I felt the recognition of a key entering the lock for which it had been cut. On the other side of the ocean, I knew one person. Everything learned on the bridge became useful: thrift, discipline, prayer in darkness, and trust that had survived laughter. None of those gifts earned the journey. They helped me inhabit it once it came.

This story ended in a visible Yes. Many waits do not. Some readers have waited longer than ten years for healing, reconciliation, or a child. A lifelong refusal cannot be explained by saying the person never became ready. Chapter 8 will face that different sorrow. Here I can offer Paul’s words: “And let us not grow weary while doing good, for in due season we shall reap if we do not lose heart” (Galatians 6:9).

Paul promises a harvest while leaving the season with God. The harvest includes every fruit of grace produced by faithful love, not merely the particular outcome we requested. Some fruit appears within history. Its fullness belongs to the Kingdom, where no act of love offered in Christ is lost.

This widens hope without making it vague. A person may still ask for the particular healing, reconciliation, or opportunity. Christian hope also looks beyond the limits of the present outcome. Resurrection means that God can gather every faithful act into a future larger than death.

The promise of harvest therefore calls us to continue doing good. Waiting does not suspend vocation. We can love the person before us, keep a promise, complete honest work, and pray today’s prayer. These acts may appear unrelated to the answer we seek. In God’s Kingdom, faithfulness is never wasted.

Due season belongs to God because the harvest belongs to Him. We are neither its owners nor its engineers. We sow and water within the time given to us. God gives growth according to wisdom that embraces the individual soul, the community, history, and eternity.

The ten years also contained mornings when prayer itself felt heavier than the burden carried up the bridge. No book had warned me about them. Their weight has a plain name: disappointment.
