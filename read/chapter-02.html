Chapter Two

The Courage of a Child

✦ ✦ ✦

≈ 14 min read

BOLD PETITION IS AN act of trust. God receives it that way. His refusal never grades the quality of our faith.

My father taught me this before I could read. I was five or six when he took me to have a baby tooth pulled. I remember the smell of disinfectant, the enormous chair, and a tray of bright instruments. I had been told to ignore the tray, so naturally I studied every instrument. I climbed into the chair and held still. Whether I cried has mercifully disappeared from memory.

I remember what happened afterward. My father drove me to a store and bought the costume of the superhero I loved. I had wanted it for months. He said it was for my bravery.

The tooth had earned nothing. It was already loose, and submitting to the inevitable was hardly heroic. My father saw a frightened boy climb into the chair anyway. My courage pleased him, and his pleasure took the form of a gift I could understand. Scripture tells us that our Father in heaven also delights in the trust of His children.

A child asks without knowing everything. He has no access to the family accounts and no seat in his parents’ private discussions. He names the toy, the dog, the later bedtime, or the second helping. Each request may receive a No. The lip trembles. Tears come. By breakfast the child is asking again.

The child survives refusal because his confidence rests in his parents. He believes they can provide, want his good, and will let him weep safely when disappointed. A No concerns the request. It does not decide the child’s worth or place in the family.

Christian tradition calls this filial trust. We ask from within a relationship already given. We do not negotiate for the right to belong. Good parents worry when a child stops asking altogether. A child who voices only desires certain to be approved has begun to manage the relationship. Strategy has replaced trust.

This confidence comes through Christ. The Son does not merely show us how a child speaks to the Father. He brings us into His own relation of trust and obedience. In Him, strangers receive adoption and servants learn the freedom of sons and daughters. The Holy Spirit does more than give us suitable words. He bears witness that we belong to God and moves the heart to address Him as Father. Boldness in prayer is therefore a work of grace before it becomes an act of courage.

Our weakness does not cancel that gift. A frightened child remains a child. A confused prayer still rises from within the covenant God established. We may approach with mixed motives, poor judgment, and desires that need healing. The Father receives the person before He judges the request. His answer will serve the truth of that relationship, including when love requires Him to refuse what the child cannot yet see clearly.

Jesus built His case for bold petition on this picture, from the parents’ side. In the Sermon on the Mount, right after commanding us to ask, to seek, and to knock, He said:

Or what man is there among you who, if his son asks for bread, will give him a stone? Or if he asks for a fish, will he give him a serpent? If you then, being evil, know how to give good gifts to your children, how much more will your Father who is in heaven give good things to those who ask Him! (Matthew 7:9‑11)

Jesus assumes that the son will name his hunger. He asks for bread, a particular food. Jesus treats this as the ordinary conduct of a child. Then His argument rises from imperfect human parents to the Father: “how much more.” If flawed parents can recognize a good gift, the Father cannot be less generous than they are.

Our boldness rests in God’s character. The child brings only hunger and nearness. He does not first prove that he deserves bread. We also come because God has made us His children in Christ. Grace gives us the standing that our merit never could.

Scripture therefore teaches concrete petition. Jesus places a loaf in the prayer He gives us: “Give us this day our daily bread” (Matthew 6:11). He also teaches us to name our danger: “deliver us from the evil one” (Matthew 6:13). The sick who approached Him named diseases, daughters, and dying servants. James traces some of our poverty to our silence: “you do not have because you do not ask” (James 4:2).

I take that permission personally. For years I asked God for a good musical instrument with which to praise Him at home. No one needs such an instrument as urgently as he needs bread, and I felt embarrassed whenever I mentioned it. Still, I asked. After several years it came through the kindness of friends. It had been built for a church, and its sound is comically large for a living room. It still fills the house.

I now regret the embarrassment more than the request. I had assumed that God should be approached only with disasters. The Father of Matthew 7 keeps no office hours for serious needs. He has numbered the hairs of our heads. No detail of a child’s life is too small for His attention, though His attention does not guarantee that He will grant every desire.

Refusal can quietly kill petition when we mistake it for rejection. I have heard this history in many conversations. One prayer failed, then several more. The person concluded that God was disappointed in a faith too weak or a life too impure. Another request would invite another humiliation. So the person stopped asking.

Such believers may continue to worship and serve. Their public prayers still sound warm. Yet they hide what they actually want. They resemble a child who believes his father is ashamed of him and therefore keeps every wish to himself. The whole sorrow rests on a false reading. A good father may refuse the request. He does not despise the child for asking.

We continue to ask because God commands us. Each request is therefore an act of obedience. We ask without controlling the result, so petition also becomes an act of faith. In asking, we surrender the illusion of self‑sufficiency. We admit that our life is received.

God sees the whole life while we see one corner of it. I have watched some refusals unfold over decades and reveal a hidden mercy. Others remain painful and obscure. Filial trust does not require us to explain them. It allows us to grieve the No, remain with the Father, and ask again.

Hebrews calls faith “the substance of things hoped for” (Hebrews 11:1). The word translated “substance” suggests a solid ground beneath a claim. It was also used for the standing represented by a title deed. A deed does not create the field. It testifies that the holder has a rightful claim even before he walks upon the land.

Faith rests its weight upon God’s reality and character. It does not give us title to every object we desire. God Himself is the promised inheritance, and every promise finds its Yes in Christ. The child at the Father’s knee possesses this assurance: whatever the answer, he remains a child and the Father remains good.

ONE MAN IN THE Gospels asked with a child’s courage. He was a leper. The law drew a wide circle around him and kept everyone he loved beyond it. He lived banished from ordinary life and had to warn others when he approached. Such a life teaches a person to stop asking. This man still asked. When Jesus came down from the mountain, the leper crossed every social boundary and came near.

And behold, a leper came and worshiped Him, saying, “Lord, if You are willing, You can make me clean.” (Matthew 8:2)

The two clauses hold the proper posture of petition. “If You are willing” entrusts the outcome to Christ. The leper claims no knowledge of what Jesus intends. “You can make me clean” states the desire plainly. Clean was the word his whole existence seemed to forbid. He did not wait to discover Christ’s will before naming it. Trust and boldness belonged to the same prayer.

Matthew continues: “Then Jesus put out His hand and touched him, saying, ‘I am willing; be cleansed’” (Matthew 8:3). Jesus touched the man everyone else avoided. He could have healed by a word, as He does elsewhere in the chapter. The touch restored more than skin. It met the isolation that disease and exclusion had imposed.

The order also matters. Jesus touched him while the disease still marked him as unclean. Divine mercy did not wait for the man to become safe. Christ’s holiness moved toward what others feared and overcame the uncleanness without being mastered by it. The man had asked for cleansing. Jesus answered the bodily need and the deeper wound of exclusion in one gesture.

Every answer of Christ reveals something about Christ Himself. The gift is never detached from the Giver. Here His power appears as compassion willing to draw near. This keeps bold petition personal. We seek help from One whose authority is joined to mercy, whose power bears the shape of self‑giving love.

Both clauses must remain together. Confidence in God’s power gives petition its courage. Submission to God’s wisdom keeps courage from becoming presumption. The fact that God can heal does not reveal what He will permit in each life. Preaching that confuses divine power with a guaranteed outcome leaves wounded people searching for the defect that prevented their miracle.

God may restore a sick body. He may allow the illness to remain while drawing the sufferer into deeper union with Christ and bringing good to others through that freely offered suffering. We may not understand His providence in this life. We may still ask without reserve for healing. The leper’s prayer gives us language for precisely this uncertainty.

Timidity damages petition in the opposite direction. I have prayed long and devout prayers that contained no actual request. I was waiting to become certain of God’s will before risking a desire in His presence. The leper possessed no such certainty. He knew Christ’s power, admitted that Christ’s intention remained free, and asked to be clean.

I once confused Christ’s power with the invulnerability of a superhero. I saw my first superhero film when I was about seven, and it captured me. Afterward I pictured the Gospel stories in the same terms: Jesus fed crowds, healed villages, and always arrived in time. I omitted the cape and flying out of reverence, but the basic figure remained. Someone invincible was on duty. That comforts a frightened child.

The image took decades to die. Our fantasies of rescue usually demand a savior who cannot be wounded. We want evil defeated without cost and the world repaired without rejection. Jesus reveals another kind of power. He enters cities that misunderstand Him. He touches the excluded and accepts the cost of drawing near. He prays with tears. He gives Himself on the cross and defeats violence by bearing it in love. The real Christ bleeds.

When that childhood image failed, I nearly abandoned bold petition with it. I assumed that mature faith should have outgrown the desire for rescue. I had confused two different things. My image of the Giver was too small, but the child’s confidence in asking was true. The costume from the dentist’s chair eventually went into a box. The courage was meant to remain.

The cross deepens our permission to ask. We bring our desires to One who knows human need from within. Christ has entered hunger, grief, pain, and loneliness. He understands the cost of hope in a world where love suffers. His wounds make our prayer more honest and more hopeful.

YEARS AGO, A YOUNG person in our family had to choose between two excellent schools. Each had offered to pay half the cost, and each led toward a different future. One was known for the field this young person loved. The other was known by nearly everyone. We discussed the choice for weeks. Each evening we rearranged the same three arguments and reached the same uncertainty.

Finally, we stopped debating and prayed. We named both schools before God and asked Him to settle what we could not. The request was as plain as a child asking for bread.

The answer came on an ordinary afternoon while the young person was away at a competition. One school offered to cover the entire cost. It was more than we had requested and more than we had imagined. When I read the news, I said aloud, “God settles it, case closed.” I have repeated those words in gratitude many times.

That story records a real provision. It does not establish a method. I have stood at other crossroads, prayed just as plainly, and received no clear direction. We chose in the dark and later discovered that God had remained present in the choosing. Some answers brought consequences so difficult that we wondered whether we had understood them correctly. Scripture itself refuses to make providence predictable. Abraham received the promised son in old age. Moses walked toward the promised land for forty years and never entered it.

The Father sometimes delights His children with an answer whose generosity is unmistakable. He has promised to remain their Father in every circumstance. He has never promised to become predictable.

The scholarship story therefore gives us permission to ask. Naming the desire gathered an arguing family into one act of dependence. We placed the decision in God’s hands, where it belonged. Once a request has been entrusted to Him, constant inspection only takes it back. We can watch for His guidance without auditing His faithfulness each morning. Faith keeps attention on God while the outcome remains uncertain.

Hannah asked with far more at stake than a choice of schools. She wanted a child. Scripture says plainly that the LORD had closed her womb. The other wife in the household provoked her year after year, especially during the annual feast. Hannah wept and could not eat. Her husband tried to reason her out of the desire: “Am I not better to you than ten sons?” (1 Samuel 1:8). Loving and baffled husbands have been trying similar arguments ever since.

Hannah did not answer him. She went to the house of the LORD and poured her grief before God. She wept in anguish while her lips moved without sound. She asked for a son and made vows concerning his future before he existed. Eli watched from his seat by the doorpost and decided that such unrestrained sorrow must be drunkenness. He told her to put away her wine.

Her reply is one of the Old Testament’s great descriptions of what praying actually is:

But Hannah answered and said, “No, my lord, I am a woman of sorrowful spirit. I have drunk neither wine nor intoxicating drink, but have poured out my soul before the LORD. Do not consider your maidservant a wicked woman, for out of the abundance of my complaint and grief I have spoken until now.” Then Eli answered and said, “Go in peace, and the God of Israel grant your petition which you have asked of Him.” (1 Samuel 1:15‑17)

Hannah called prayer a pouring out of the soul. She did not reduce the desire to a polite size. Complaint and grief came before God in their abundance. Religious eyes may find such prayer improper. We often censor ourselves for the same reason, as though God needed protection from the truth He already sees.

Eli recovered from his mistake and blessed her petition without knowing its content. Hannah never told him what she had requested. He recognized that a soul poured out before the LORD stood on holy ground. The pouring itself revealed faith because Hannah carried her unmanageable sorrow toward God.

Then Scripture gives a quiet sign that prayer has already acted: “So the woman went her way and ate, and her face was no longer sad” (1 Samuel 1:18). Samuel had not been conceived. The other wife remained in the household, and Hannah’s womb remained closed. Nothing visible had changed. Yet Hannah could eat, and her face was different.

The change did not mean that she had stopped wanting a child. Her petition remained precise. She had entrusted the desire to God, and that surrender released her from carrying it alone. Grace did not erase the ache. It changed the place from which she bore it.

Entrustment is a real spiritual act. Hannah placed in God’s care something she could neither produce nor forget. She remained responsible for her life, yet she no longer stood as the sole guardian of its future. The burden had entered a relationship with the LORD. That is why she could eat before she conceived. Her renewed strength came from God’s presence within the unresolved desire.

This also explains why lament and faith can occupy the same prayer. Hannah’s grief did not accuse her of unbelief. It became the material of trust when she poured it before God. Faith is sometimes imagined as the ability to feel certain about a favorable result. Hannah shows a deeper certainty: God is the One to whom the whole sorrow may be given. Her face changed because the sorrow now had an address.

Our usual accounting recognizes only two events: the request and the answer. Hannah shows us a third. God meets the person within the asking itself. Before circumstances move, prayer brings the hidden self into His presence. Desire loses its secrecy. Grief finds a listener. The person praying becomes exposed to God and therefore open to His action.

This exposure costs more than religious speech. We can say many prayers while keeping the heart protected. Hannah gave God the abundance of her complaint and grief. Her courage leads us to the next question: what happens when prayer removes our defenses and leaves us fully known before God?
