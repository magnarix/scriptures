Chapter Twelve

When the Answer Is You

✦ ✦ ✦

≈ 13 min read

GOD OFTEN ANSWERS PRAYER through people willing to be sent. Providence works through people and things, and the answer is no less God’s for coming through them. Human freedom, practical action, institutions, and ordinary materials can all become instruments of mercy. Sometimes the available hands are ours.

I have seen this from both sides: the night a telephone call sent me back out into the dark, and the year creditors became unexpected agents of mercy in my own life.

Years ago I returned near midnight from a long flight, having reserved the following holiday for rest. Then the telephone rang. Before the second ring I had assembled a small theology explaining why the caller could wait.

The caller described a woman who cleaned houses for a living; her employer was putting her out that night. She faced the street in darkness, on the eve of a holiday, in a country where she had no family. By the end of the call, I was looking for my shoes.

No committee or church official sent me. A need reached my phone, and I took the interruption as a summons to serve, because God does not confine His calls to official channels or convenient hours.

I went willingly, and during the drive one thought stayed with me. A woman standing beside her bags in darkness may have been praying in whatever words she had left. The approaching headlights became part of the answer.

Scripture repeatedly shows providence moving through people. God sent a prophet to a starving widow and sent Philip to explain Scripture to a foreign official, and when a crowd became hungry, Jesus told the disciples, “You give them something to eat” (Luke 9:13).

God could act without created instruments, yet He ordinarily dignifies creatures by giving them a real share in His work, so that the act is still His gift while becoming genuinely ours. Grace does not push human freedom aside. It wakes it up and completes it.

God gives creatures an existence, a power, and a freedom that are really their own. A person who feeds the hungry truly acts, and God does not use him like an inert tool. Providence enables the free act and then gathers it into a purpose larger than the actor sees.

This preserves both gratitude and responsibility. We thank God for the mercy and also thank the person who chose it. We cannot excuse a failure to act by saying God could have used someone else, because created freedom has real consequences within providence.

Later, a newly arrived man in our community told me he had lost his job and had no food for his family. Jesus’ command on the hillside became immediate. I gave him what was in my wallet, too little for a family’s day, yet it was what I possessed.

Christian mercy begins with what we actually have, not with what we wish we had. A small gift does not excuse us from seeking larger or more effective help, but it places what we have within Christ’s compassion and allows the next act to begin.

The feeding of the crowd keeps two truths together: the disciples’ resources were insufficient, and Jesus still asked them to offer those resources. Human limitation became the setting for divine abundance, and the miracle did not make the loaves unnecessary.

We often delay obedience until we can solve the whole problem. Love usually begins with a partial act: one meal, a ride, a telephone call, an introduction. When possible, that act should connect the person to wider help.

My generosity sometimes needs correction. At an intersection I once gave a man one dollar and felt pleased, until someone beside me asked what he could actually buy with it and urged me to give more. The traffic light stayed red. My opinion of myself changed.

Mercy asks what the other person needs. What amount allows the giver to feel generous is a different question. Prudence still matters, and no single encounter carries every obligation, yet the asking should begin with the neighbor’s good.

The apostle James has a name for the very devout way of being useless. He had no patience with a religion that stays indoors with the door shut, and he says so bluntly enough that no commentary can sand it down:

If a brother or sister is ill‑clad and in lack of daily food, and one of you says to them, “Go in peace, be warmed and filled,” without giving them the things needed for the body, what does it profit? (James 2:15‑16)

“Go in peace, be warmed and filled” sounds like a blessing. The speaker has seen a need and responded with religious language, and James asks what the words profit when the body is still hungry and exposed.

Faith becomes active through love. Prayer opens us to God’s action and may also disclose the action grace asks from us. Words are still necessary because they invoke God and speak hope, and they become false when used to avoid a mercy already within reach.

There is a promise in Isaiah that fastens my prayers and my neighbor’s hunger into a single sentence. I did not understand it until I had stood at both ends of it:

Then you shall call, and the Lord will answer;
you shall cry, and he will say, Here I am.
If you take away from the midst of you the yoke,
the pointing of the finger, and speaking wickedness,
if you pour yourself out for the hungry
and satisfy the desire of the afflicted,
then shall your light rise in the darkness
and your gloom be as the noonday. (Isaiah 58:9‑10)

“Here I am” is usually the servant’s response. Samuel says it in the night (1 Samuel 3:4–10), and Isaiah answers, “Here am I! Send me” (Isaiah 6:8). In Isaiah 58, God speaks the words to those who cry to Him.

The promise joins prayer to justice and care for the hungry, though it does not teach that charitable works purchase God’s attention, for the chapter has already condemned fasting separated from mercy. We like to keep prayer in one room and the neighbor in another. The prophet knocks out the wall and says it is one room.

I have often experienced God’s nearness while my door was opening to others. Feeling is not proof, and service may also be dry. Yet love of neighbor disposes the heart toward communion with God, whose image the neighbor bears.

Intercession should make us available. When I place a name before God, I try to ask whether some faithful action is mine, and the answer may cost an evening, money reserved for pleasure, or a conversation avoided.

This is an aspiration rather than a claim that every need is my assignment, and I have often failed to ask it. Discernment includes ability, duties, safety, and the gifts of others. The prayer simply refuses to assume in advance that my role is limited to words.

The person helped may soon forget the helper, and that can be a grace. A messenger serves the sender rather than seeking the center of the story, and Christian service should leave the recipient freer to recognize Christ and less indebted to the servant’s ego.

The woman from the midnight call is safe now and may no longer remember me. I am grateful. She got the help she needed, and I dropped out of the story. That is how it should be.

I FIRST LEARNED THIS from the receiving side. During the financial crisis described in the Prologue, I sought help in religious places and often received advice, while some concrete mercy came through institutions I had regarded as heartless. Several creditors erased debts whose contracts required every cent. Mercy entered from beyond the contract.

Years later I worked for a season within one of those institutions and learned that the debt relief expressed its stated values. God had worked through policies, employees, and accounting decisions. Providence is free among ledgers and offices as well as within church walls.

This does not sanctify every institutional practice, since organizations can exploit and harm. The point is that created structures can still serve justice and mercy when people form and use them well, and my theology had to grant God freedom to work beyond the places I considered religious.

Institutions multiply the reach of personal choices. A merciful policy can help thousands, and an unjust one spreads harm just as widely. Love of neighbor therefore includes attention to structures as well as individual charity.

Debt relief showed that mercy need not oppose justice. Justice names what is owed and protects rightful claims. Mercy may freely remit a claim, or provide a path through it, without denying the obligation’s reality.

I had seen such providence as a boy. Clergymen in that place lived without salaries, so families delivered stacked metal meal tins in rotation. Comfortable households sent heavy tins, and poor households sent thinner ones.

The clergyman gave thanks in the same words and prayed with special tenderness for families whose gift had cost more. I thought I was watching poverty. I was also watching daily bread arrive one door at a time through hands that were often poor themselves.

No one called the arrangement providence. They called it lunch. Divine care often hides in practices so ordinary that gratitude is needed to reveal their depth.

THE HARDEST UNEXPECTED HELP may come through the person who caused the wound. Years ago, while working in another country, I argued with a colleague about a difficult project, and the next morning he sent a harsh attack on my work to people above us. As a newcomer I had more at risk than my colleagues understood. I read the message with a cold weight in my stomach.

I did not retaliate. Others defended me, and I remain grateful. When the colleague next appeared, I touched his shoulder to show that I held nothing against him, and I meant the gesture as far as I could reach that day. I had begun to forgive him, but the healing was slower in coming. I walked beside the ocean asking for peace, and none came for a while.

A week later, work arrangements placed us in one car with him driving. We exchanged small pleasantries while the unresolved harm sat between us. A few minutes into the journey, he spoke without taking his eyes from the road.

“Look. I want to apologize for what happened last week.”

I answered too quickly: “It’s OK.”

“No,” he said. “It’s not OK.”

He described the pressure and anxiety he had carried, how he had misread my action, and how he had chosen a weapon, and he used none of it as an excuse. He named his responsibility.

While we looked ahead at the road, healing came that my solitary walks had not produced. The wound began to close. Compassion for the burden he carried grew alongside a renewed affection for him.

“It’s not OK” was the truest sentence in the car. My quick reassurance had tried to be generous while denying the actual wound. His correction insisted upon truth, because reconciliation needs an honest account of harm.

An unforced apology can be an answer to prayer. We cannot compel repentance, schedule it through a technique, or earn it, and many wounded people never receive the words they deserve. When genuine repentance comes, grace has moved within another person’s freedom in a way we could not manufacture.

Forgiveness still carries cost. If I damage a car and apologize sincerely, the metal stays dented. Forgiveness gives up the right to get even, but the dent is still there, and justice and restitution may still ask for repair. I had already let the matter go before the drive, and his truthful apology opened a path toward reconciliation.

Christ’s reconciliation of the world takes sin’s damage with complete seriousness. He bears its cost in self‑giving love and defeats it, and whenever repentance, forgiveness, justice, and repair come together, our reconciliations borrow from His.

In that car, the elements came apart and I could see each one. My letting go the week before had been forgiveness. His apology was repentance. The two met only when he told the truth. Restitution repairs what can be repaired, justice protects the order damaged by sin, and reconciliation restores communion when truth and freedom permit it. An apology without repair may be incomplete, and forgiveness without reconciliation can still be faithful. Justice pursued without hatred can protect both victim and community.

Most wounds never receive such an apology, so forgiveness cannot depend upon one; it releases hatred and the desire to repay even when the offender denies the harm. None of this cancels moral accountability, justice, restitution, or the protection of the vulnerable.

Forgiveness does not require continued exposure to abuse. We can still name the wrong, keep a safe distance, and report what must be reported. Trust must be rebuilt through truthful conduct, and reconciliation requires the participation of both parties, normally including repentance. Forgiveness and pain can live in the same heart for a long time.

PEOPLE SOMETIMES WRITE AS though a clergyman possessed special access to God and freedom from ordinary limits, and they ask for money, influence, opportunities, and cures I cannot provide. Each request carries hope, so saying no causes real sadness.

Clergy are neither heavenly intermediaries nor substitutes for Christ. In Christ, every believer has access to the Father through the Son in the one Spirit (Ephesians 2:18). Public ministry carries particular responsibilities and gifts, but no private ownership of grace. At such moments, I remember Peter at the temple gate.

Vocation includes limits. A person is called to particular service within Christ’s body while other gifts belong to other members, and admitting inability protects the person asking from false hope and the servant from pretending to be indispensable.

Limits can reveal the actual gift. When money, influence, or cure is unavailable, attentive presence, truthful counsel, prayer, referral, or advocacy may still be possible. Love offers what is real instead of what reputation wants to promise. Peter said as much to the man at the gate:

But Peter said, “I have no silver and gold, but I give you what I have; in the name of Jesus Christ of Nazareth, walk.” (Acts 3:6)

“Silver and gold I do not have” describes many of my limits. I cannot command healing as Peter did. Ordained ministry gives no private supernatural possession. The Word, prayer, and every gift belong to Christ.

Peter’s hinge belongs to every Christian: “I give you what I have.” I can stay in a room after bad news, hear a story without flinching, offer appropriate human touch, and pray slowly in the sufferer’s words. Presence once seemed small to me. I have watched it meet needs money could not reach.

Presence should not become an excuse for withholding practical help. When food, money, medicine, advocacy, or expertise is needed and available, love acts. Yet loneliness itself is a real wound, and faithful company may become the precise mercy requested.

Every story in this chapter stands under Christ’s teaching about the last judgment. He describes the hungry being fed, the stranger welcomed, and the sick and imprisoned visited:

And the King will answer them, ‘Truly, I say to you, as you did it to one of the least of these my brethren, you did it to me.’ (Matthew 25:40)

The King says, “I was sick and you visited me, I was in prison and you came to me” (Matthew 25:36). Many cannot cure disease or unlock a prison, but more can visit, listen, write, advocate, or stay present. Doctors and others with relevant skills should use them fully, and every disciple can offer some form of care.

The judgment scene dignifies ordinary mercy without making it trivial. Food, welcome, clothing, visitation, and presence all concern bodily human need, and love of Christ becomes visible in acts that fit within an afternoon and may still cost convenience, status, or safety. Matthew 25 also changes the direction of every errand. I may believe I am carrying Christ to someone in need, and Jesus says He is already present in the person received. On the midnight drive I thought I was bringing help to a stranger. This passage tells me Christ was already waiting beside her bags.

Christ’s identification with “the least” establishes their dignity before any helper arrives. The hungry, stranger, sick, and imprisoned are neither projects nor material for another person’s virtue, and we cannot claim love for an invisible Christ while despising His presence in inconvenient people. Prayer and service meet because the One addressed in prayer waits within the neighbor. Discernment is still necessary, since no recipient becomes identical with Christ in every desire or action, and the passage calls us to serve real need while honoring sacred dignity.

The frightened woman, the creditors, the families filling meal tins, and the repentant colleague all became places of encounter. We take turns being sent and being rescued, and Christ, who is Lord of the mercy, mysteriously receives it in the vulnerable person.

These answers also carry a family resemblance. Different hands delivered the gifts, but the same generosity was in them, and it points toward one Giver.
