Epilogue

The Last Answered Prayer

✦ ✦ ✦

≈ 5 min read

YEARS AGO, I STOOD at a graveside with a family I did not know. The casket had been lowered and the final prayers spoken. A young boy tugged at his grandfather’s coat and asked, “Can he hear us down there?”

The grandfather said nothing. I do not blame him. Some questions are too large for an immediate answer. I have carried the boy’s words ever since. Christianity answers them through the resurrection of Jesus Christ.

The boy’s question came from love. He could see the grave, but love resisted the claim that the conversation had simply ended. He wanted to know whether the person he loved had fallen beyond every voice and every relationship.

Christian hope does not treat the body as a discarded shell. Death tears apart the unity of body and soul. The dead remain within God’s knowledge and dominion while the body awaits resurrection. God’s final purpose is embodied life restored and transformed in Christ.

This matters because the gospel does not promise that death is harmless. Death is an enemy. It takes people from our sight and leaves real absence behind. Our hope rests in God, who enters death, defeats it, and will raise the dead.

Many prayers in this book have carried a form of the boy’s question. A prayer for healing asks whether bodily weakness has the final word. A prayer for someone far away asks whether any distance exceeds God’s reach. Beneath long waiting lies a fear that silence may finally become absolute.

God answered that fear through a Person and an event. Jesus stood at Lazarus’s tomb among people who were weeping, and He wept with them. His knowledge that Lazarus would soon emerge did not make grief unreal. Then He spoke to Martha:

Jesus said to her, “I am the resurrection and the life. He who believes in Me, though he may die, he shall live. And whoever lives and believes in Me shall never die. Do you believe this?” (John 11:25‑26)

Martha already believed in the resurrection at the last day. Jesus did not correct that hope. He revealed that resurrection is inseparable from Him: “I am.” The future event rests in the present identity of Christ. Life belongs to Him, and those united to Him share His victory.

Soon after raising Lazarus, Jesus entered a tomb Himself. He truly died and was buried. On the third day, He rose bodily. He was no ghost and no corpse briefly restored to ordinary mortal life. His resurrection inaugurated the new creation. The disciples touched Him, ate with Him, and saw the wounds of crucifixion in a body death could no longer master.

The wounds matter. Resurrection does not pretend that suffering never happened. God raises and transforms what was wounded. Easter declares that evil can inflict real injury without gaining the final word.

Ancient Christian images of the Resurrection portray Christ standing upon the broken gates of death and pulling Adam and Eve from their graves by the wrists. The image emphasizes divine initiative. The dead cannot raise themselves. Resurrection is God’s act from beginning to end.

This is why I call the Resurrection the last answered prayer. Every human power has ended in the grave. Help must come entirely from beyond death, and in Christ it has come. He is the firstfruits of those who have died (1 Corinthians 15:20). All will be raised, and the blessed life for which humanity was made is communion with Christ, freely received and finally unveiled.

Paul can therefore write of the last day with confidence: “Death is swallowed up in victory” (1 Corinthians 15:54). He taunts the defeated enemy: “O Death, where is your sting? O Hades, where is your victory?” (1 Corinthians 15:55). Then he gives thanks to God, “who gives us the victory through our Lord Jesus Christ” (1 Corinthians 15:57).

The Corinthians who first heard those words still attended funerals. Christians continued to die. Resurrection changed neither the fact of bereavement nor the need to mourn. It changed the meaning and future of the grave.

Paul tells grieving believers that they need not “sorrow as others who have no hope” (1 Thessalonians 4:13). He expects sorrow. The gospel removes despair, not tears. Jesus wept at a tomb He was about to open. Grief and hope can inhabit the same heart.

I cannot answer the boy’s question by claiming knowledge God has not given me about the immediate awareness of the person in that grave. I can answer with Christ’s promise that an hour is coming “in which all who are in the graves will hear His voice and come forth—those who have done good, to the resurrection of life, and those who have done evil, to the resurrection of condemnation” (John 5:28–29). No grave lies beyond the voice of the Son of God.

✦ ✦ ✦

RETURN NOW TO THE prayer you carried into this book. Perhaps it has been granted. Perhaps you are still learning to recognize what God has done. Perhaps the answer remains delayed, or perhaps the desired outcome will never come within this life. Your grief need not be renamed to protect a theory of prayer.

Beneath every case remains the question of whether divine love outlasts silence. God has answered with His risen Son. The Resurrection does not tell us that every temporal request will receive the outcome we prefer. It tells us that no faithful life and no faithful prayer ends in nothingness. Christ will raise the dead, judge with justice, heal creation, and bring His people into the communion for which they were made.

The vow that began this book is nearly fulfilled. I promised to write what I had learned about answered prayer. The final words should themselves be prayer. I first wrote them during a season when something I deeply wanted was withheld. I offer them now for both of us:

Lord, You know my heart better than I do. You know the prayers I have whispered in the night, and the ache that stays when what I ask for stays unmet.

You see how tightly I have clung to the gifts I want from You, as though my joy depended on receiving them. Loosen my grip.

I am still the child who clings to the gift and forgets the Giver. Teach me to desire You more than the outcome, even while the wanting hurts.

If the thing I pray for comes, let me read it as a sign of Your love and never as a substitute for Your presence.

If it delays, draw me so close to You in the waiting that the gift, when it arrives, finds my joy already full.

You went down into the grave and heard us from the inside, and one morning You will take me by the wrist.

So whether You answer in the way I hope, or in a way I will not understand until I see You, I will praise You. You are my portion, and I will hope in You.

And even if the thing I pray for never comes, I will say with all my heart:

You are the Giver, the Gift, and the Goal.

And You are enough.
