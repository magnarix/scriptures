Chapter Ten

Answered Differently

✦ ✦ ✦

≈ 12 min read

SOME PRAYERS FOR HEALING receive transfiguration rather than cure. God may renew a person inwardly while the body continues to fail. This never makes bodily healing unimportant. It reveals that divine healing reaches deeper than cure and will be completed only in resurrection.

I learned this through two funerals. For years I had filed both prayers under “Refused.” Later, I saw that grace had answered in forms none of us had requested. The first answer appeared in a hospital. The second became visible to me at a coffin when I was a boy.

Years ago, a man in our community received a diagnosis of terminal cancer. We prayed for medical healing. His name appeared on prayer lists. People fasted and prayed in worship, kitchens, and cars. We asked that treatment work and scans become clear. Eventually, the doctors said nothing more could be done. He was dying.

I visited him in intensive care expecting exhaustion. On the way in, I prepared a few consoling words. His face unsettled every expectation. It carried peace. I had come to comfort a dying man, and he began comforting his visitors.

He spoke of family, memory, and joy. He understood what was coming and did not avoid naming it. His peace rested on no denial. It existed in the same room as terminal illness and reached deeper than the disease.

One week before his death, he received baptism at his own request. Water was given in the name of the Father, Son, and Holy Spirit. This was more than a final gesture. Baptism joined him sacramentally to Christ’s death and Resurrection, gave new birth, incorporated him into Christ and His Church, and marked him with a belonging death could not erase.

Baptism does not deny mortality. The baptized still become ill and die. It changes the person’s relation to death because death is now entered within Christ’s own dying and rising. The old life is buried with Him, and new life begins before the body reaches the grave.

This new life is objective before it is emotional. The man’s joy gave a visible sign of grace, but baptism did not depend upon his ability to feel peaceful. God’s promise held him through weakness, medication, fear, and every fluctuation of the final week.

His incorporation into the Church also meant that he did not approach death alone. He belonged to Christ’s body and was carried by its prayer. The community’s failed request for cure was not wasted; it became part of the communion surrounding him as he received a greater gift.

Cancer had taken strength and earthly years. It had no claim over the divine life given in Christ. His face carried a joy that seemed to come from beyond the room. I remembered the face more than his words.

He died within the week. At the funeral, I understood that baptismal life had become the deepest truth of his dying. For months, each medical report had looked like another refusal. Yet grace had reordered his life toward Christ. Fear loosened its hold. He gave peace to visitors during the weeks when they expected to give peace to him.

The cancer was not cured, and his family still grieved. Inward renewal was therefore neither a substitute explanation nor a way to call death good. It was a real work of grace within a body still awaiting resurrection. Our request for healing had received an answer larger than cure and painfully incomplete within this life.

God sometimes removes suffering through cure, and Christians should ask boldly for it. In the Paschal mystery, God also enters suffering and transforms its final meaning. Scripture associates glory with light, cloud, fire, and Christ’s shining face. At Golgotha, glory appears as the Son’s obedient love offered to the end.

The Cross remains an instrument of human cruelty. God reveals His glory there by making self‑giving love victorious over sin and death. The executioner’s tree becomes, through Christ’s free offering, the Tree of Life.

Christ entered suffering fully and rose from death. “Transfiguration” helps me describe what I saw in intensive care. Grace did not remove cancer. It changed the man’s relation to death by joining him to Christ’s risen life.

The word must be used carefully. Suffering itself was not transfigured into something desirable. Love, faith, and baptismal hope became radiant within it. The scans did not improve, and the family buried him. Yet death no longer possessed the authority to define his life.

The human person is a unity of body and soul. Inward renewal cannot make bodily decline irrelevant, and bodily cure alone would not complete salvation. Christ heals the whole person. During earthly life, different dimensions of that healing may appear at different times.

This protects the chapter from false choices. We do not have to choose between medicine and spiritual renewal, or between prayer for cure and preparation for death. Love seeks every genuine good available. When cure becomes impossible, the work of reconciliation, forgiveness, communion, and hope remains urgently real.

Transfiguration names a change in manifestation. Grace reveals a deeper identity already given in Christ. The dying man remained the same person, yet fear no longer dominated what visitors saw. His baptismal life became more visible as physical strength diminished.

Paul knew this from inside his own failing body, and he wrote it down for a church he loved:

Therefore we do not lose heart. Even though our outward man is perishing, yet the inward man is being renewed day by day. For our light affliction, which is but for a moment, is working for us a far more exceeding and eternal weight of glory, while we do not look at the things which are seen, but at the things which are not seen. For the things which are seen are temporary, but the things which are not seen are eternal. (2 Corinthians 4:16‑18)

Paul acknowledges that the outward person is perishing. He attaches no shame to bodily decline. At the same time, the inward person is renewed each day. Both processes can occur within one human life.

In intensive care, monitors measured the bodily decline while the man’s face showed inward renewal. Paul calls affliction “light” and “momentary” only in comparison with an eternal weight of glory. The words would be cruel if spoken from outside suffering. Paul writes as someone beaten, imprisoned, and left for dead.

The contrast is eschatological. Present affliction remains heavy within time. Resurrection glory outweighs it because glory is eternal participation in God’s life. Paul does not minimize pain; he places it within the scale of the coming Kingdom.

The peace I witnessed was entirely gift. The dying man did not manufacture it, and visitors did not bring it. I have known other deaths that remained grim and dark. This story cannot become a formula promising visible radiance to people who pray correctly.

No sick person owes visitors an inspiring death. We should never tell someone in pain that suffering is secretly glory or demand a lesson from the bedside. We come to listen, serve, and remain present. If grace becomes visible, we receive it with gratitude. If it remains hidden, God’s faithfulness has not failed.

THE SECOND FUNERAL CAME much earlier in my life. As a boy, I loved an elderly woman whose body was bent almost double. Her spine curved toward the ground. She cooked, walked, and welcomed visitors in that posture. To meet her eyes, I crouched down. I never saw her stand straight.

Adults eventually explained that the injury came from war. Occupying soldiers had forced villagers to carry crops, timber, and building stones. Years of crushing weight bent many bodies by degrees. Human beings had done this to her, load by load. Long after the soldiers disappeared and the war became history, her spine still carried it.

Her condition frightened me. I wondered whether it was hereditary and whether age would bend me the same way. After watching her pass through a doorway, I sometimes stood as straight as possible, as though posture could be stored against the future. Fear and love occupied the same boy.

She never complained in my hearing. I do not know what she said to God about her back or whether anyone prayed for healing. I did not. To a child, she was simply the person she had always been.

At her funeral, she lay straight in the coffin. The people who prepared her body had done what living years could not. I learned her full height only after death. The sight gave me a child’s wordless conviction: the bent body expressed what violence had done to her, while the straight body hinted at the person violence could never finally define.

That conviction needed theological correction. Death had not healed her, and the undertaker’s skill was not resurrection. Still, the image remained with me as an anticipation of the healing God has promised.

Years later, I found an echo of her story in Luke:

Now He was teaching in one of the synagogues on the Sabbath. And behold, there was a woman who had a spirit of infirmity eighteen years, and was bent over and could in no way raise herself up. But when Jesus saw her, He called her to Him and said to her, “Woman, you are loosed from your infirmity.” And He laid His hands on her, and immediately she was made straight, and glorified God. (Luke 13:10‑13)

The woman asks for nothing. Luke says she “could in no way raise herself up,” removing any suggestion that effort would have solved the condition. Eighteen years had made bentness the settled form of daily life. Jesus noticed her and interrupted His teaching.

The initiative came entirely from Christ. “Woman, you are loosed from your infirmity” (Luke 13:12). He later calls her a daughter of Abraham whom Satan had bound. Her condition appears as bondage rather than identity. Christ’s word reaches the knot and releases it.

She stood straight and glorified God. Bodily healing restored more than function. It returned her to upright relation with the community and revealed God’s liberating reign. Her healed body became praise.

Jesus called her a “daughter of Abraham.” Before the crowd saw a condition, He named covenant identity. Eighteen years of bondage had not erased her place among God’s people. Healing made publicly visible the dignity that suffering had never removed.

The Sabbath intensifies the sign. Sabbath remembers creation, liberation from slavery, and rest in God. Jesus declares that releasing a bound daughter belongs precisely to that day. Healing reveals the Sabbath’s true purpose: creation restored to freedom and praise.

The opposition in the synagogue wanted religious order protected. Christ showed that divine order serves life. The law reaches fulfillment when the burdened person is released into the worshiping community. Her straightened body becomes an enacted interpretation of God’s command.

The One who released her would later accept bonds. The hands laid upon her would be nailed to the Cross. Christ freely entered the depth of human distortion and death to raise humanity with Him. The synagogue healing anticipated the liberation revealed fully at Easter.

The woman I loved had also been bound by human violence, and no visible healing came during her life. The undertaker did not heal her. Human hands arranged her body. Yet the sight gave me an image of what God has promised to accomplish in truth: the restoration of the body and person beyond every distortion imposed by history.

Resurrection means that God raises the person who died. Christian hope reaches beyond the survival of an immaterial soul. The body belongs to personal identity and to God’s purpose for creation. What violence did to her body matters enough for God to redeem.

The risen body will possess continuity and transformation. The person remains herself, recognized and loved, while mortality and deformity lose their power. Resurrection does not replace the wounded person with someone else. God brings His original work to glory.

This is why the coffin image moved me. Straightness did not define her worth, and disability never made her less fully human. The image pointed toward freedom from violence, pain, and death. Resurrection will reveal her person without any wound claiming the final interpretation.

Some healings arrive visibly within history. Final healing awaits the resurrection of the body, when creation’s wounds will no longer deform what God made. The same Christ stands behind both. His word reaches across illness, violence, time, and death. At funerals, I return to John’s promise:

And God will wipe away every tear from their eyes; there shall be no more death, nor sorrow, nor crying. There shall be no more pain, for the former things have passed away. (Revelation 21:4)

God wipes tears with a gesture both intimate and particular. John lists death, sorrow, crying, pain, and finally “the former things.” Those former things include wars, forced labor, timber and stone, and the slow crushing of a spine. The promise does not discard the wounded creation. God makes it new.

At a coffin, I see what the world has done and the limit of its power. Death acts last within the old creation. Resurrection belongs to God and will restore the person in embodied life.

Many people carry an inward bending caused by fear, shame, grief, sin, exhaustion, or memory. We should not equate these conditions casually with the woman’s physical disability. Her story reveals Christ’s attention to people whose suffering has become invisible through familiarity.

Christ’s word of release has different forms. He forgives sin, heals wounds, restores dignity, and will raise the body. Some liberation appears now before witnesses. Its fullness may remain beyond death. In every case, the power that bent the person does not possess the final word.

The two funerals now stand together in my memory. The man we asked God to cure received baptismal joy and inward renewal as he approached death. The straight body of the elderly woman offered an image of bodily resurrection still promised. Neither story removes the grief or turns death into healing. Both enlarge what I mean by answer.

Pray boldly for healing and remain attentive to grace beyond the form requested. A prayer once marked “Refused” may deserve rereading. Do this gently. Sometimes no alternative answer becomes visible. When grace does appear through peace, reconciliation, courage, or renewed faith, receive it without pretending that the desired cure no longer mattered.

Discernment requires humility. We should not declare another person’s suffering an answer or assign spiritual meaning they do not recognize. The person who prayed may need time, distance, and freedom before any different grace becomes visible. Some mysteries remain unresolved throughout life.

An alternative answer should bear the character of God revealed in Christ. It moves toward truth, freedom, love, reconciliation, and hope. It will never require calling abuse good, abandoning justice, or refusing available care. Divine grace heals; it does not baptize what destroys.

We also test our rereading within the Christian community and Scripture. Private interpretation can become a way of avoiding grief. Wise companions help us name genuine fruit while preserving honest lament for what was lost.

Some prayers are also answered exactly as asked. Scans clear, wanderers return, and doors open. I have witnessed answers so precise that they produced laughter. Many came only after years of prayer.

Long waiting tests whether the request will remain before God through dry seasons and contrary evidence. It also changes the person who asks. Jesus chose an insistent widow at a judge’s door as His image of persistence. Her story leads us forward.
