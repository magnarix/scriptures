Chapter Eight

The Thorn That Stays

✦ ✦ ✦

≈ 14 min read

FOR MOST OF MY adult life I have carried what Paul called a thorn in the flesh, and this chapter begins with confession because its teaching has cost me too much to present from a distance. I have prayed standing, kneeling, and face down. I have fasted and wept. People I trusted have laid their hands on me and asked with real faith that God remove it, and I rose with my hair warm from their hands and the thorn unchanged.

After decades, the answer looks like a lifelong No. I will not identify the thorn. Shame is part of the reason, but Paul gives the better reason: he never named his own. Christians have proposed illness, injury, speech difficulty, persecution, and temptation, and the guesses reveal our need to fill the silence.

Paul’s silence is a mercy. Because the affliction has no name, people with very different wounds can recognize the promise addressed to weakness. His thorn is not every form of suffering, yet his testimony reaches beyond one diagnosis.

The chapter holds several truths together. God may refuse a faithful prayer repeatedly, and the refusal proves neither weak faith nor divine punishment. Prayer for healing is still right, because Scripture commands it and God still heals, though no method guarantees the outcome. And when the thorn stays, Christ promises sufficient grace, power perfected within weakness.

Sufficient grace means more than enough courage to endure another day. Grace is participation in the life of God. It forgives, heals, elevates, and draws the person into communion with Christ. The thorn may stay at one level while grace goes deeper, working freedom, love, humility, and hope in the person who carries it.

None of this makes visible healing unimportant. Christianity confesses the goodness of the body and its future resurrection, and Christ healed bodies as signs of the coming Kingdom. We keep asking because bodily suffering matters to Him, and because final salvation includes the redemption of the body. Present refusal therefore lives between two truths: Christ may heal now, and He will finally make His people whole in resurrection. The timing is His. Hope goes on praying without letting present suffering have the last word.

✦ ✦ ✦

PAUL WAS NO SPIRITUAL novice. He had seen the risen Christ and witnessed extraordinary healings. Acts even records that cloths which touched him were carried to the sick (Acts 19:11‑12). He also described being caught up to Paradise, where he heard what human speech could not repeat (2 Corinthians 12:2‑4). Then he wrote:

And to keep me from being too elated by the abundance of revelations, a thorn was given me in the flesh, a messenger of Satan, to harass me, to keep me from being too elated. Three times I besought the Lord about this, that it should leave me; but he said to me, “My grace is sufficient for you, for my power is made perfect in weakness.” I will all the more gladly boast of my weaknesses, that the power of Christ may rest upon me. (2 Corinthians 12:7‑9)

Paul pleaded. The apostle who taught persistent prayer begged for release, and his three pleas may mark distinct seasons of asking. Then Christ gave a rare explanation. Grace would be sufficient, and divine strength would come to its completion in the very weakness Paul wanted removed.

Paul understood the thorn as protection against pride; he repeats that it was given to keep him from being too elated. Extraordinary revelation and humiliating weakness occupied the same life, and the thorn kept spiritual privilege from becoming self‑exaltation.

Paul had kept his vision private for fourteen years, and when he finally mentioned it, he spoke indirectly and refused to describe its secrets, giving more attention to the weakness. Our religious culture often reverses the emphasis. A minor experience becomes a platform, a claim to authority. Paul boasts in what keeps him dependent upon Christ.

This changed the way I carried shame. Weakness stopped being evidence that ministry had failed and became the place where self‑sufficiency failed and Christ’s power could be recognized as His. I do not admire the thorn, and I doubt I ever will. But somewhere along the way I quit treating dependence as a disgrace.

The thorn humiliated me before other people and before God. I had absorbed the idea that mature Christians should move through each day almost effortlessly holy, so when the struggle continued, I blamed weak faith, then insufficient effort, then sin. Each recurrence became a verdict. I felt unworthy before God, and I feared that people would be scandalized by a clergyman with an old unanswered prayer.

An older clergyman broke the isolation. I told him and waited for his expression to change. It did not. He reminded me that people we both admired had served God faithfully while carrying heavier afflictions all their lives, and grace had never depended upon their appearing invulnerable.

Every Christian is a forgiven and dependent person. Public service does not create a separate class of souls. Many believers hide a thorn because grace has not “fixed” them in the expected way. Mine remained. By grace, I also remained.

There is a difference between weakness and hypocrisy, and it matters. Hypocrisy uses a holy appearance to conceal chosen falsehood, while weakness tells the truth about itself. It resists sin and asks for help, and it knows it depends on mercy. A person may fail and need repentance without becoming a fraud in every act of service.

Grace also creates responsibility. A thorn is never an excuse for harming another person. Persistent temptation may require confession, accountability, treatment, or firm limits, and sufficient grace includes the power to take these concrete steps even while the underlying struggle continues.

The older clergyman’s unchanged face gave me room to tell the truth. Shame thrives in secrecy, because secrecy makes the struggle appear unique. Wise companionship returns weakness to the common life of redeemed sinners and helps a man tell what must be accepted from what must be changed.

The continuing thorn does not cancel prayer for healing, for Scripture gives a direct command:

Is any among you sick? Let him call for the elders of the church, and let them pray over him, anointing him with oil in the name of the Lord; and the prayer of faith will save the sick man, and the Lord will raise him up; and if he has committed sins, he will be forgiven. Therefore confess your sins to one another, and pray for one another, that you may be healed. The prayer of a righteous man has great power in its effects. (James 5:14‑16)

We obey this command without embarrassment. The sick call, and the elders pray and anoint with oil in the Lord’s name. We entrust the whole person to Christ, asking Him to strengthen, forgive, sustain, and heal according to His will, and the whole act is prayerful obedience rather than the operation of a mechanism.

I believe God heals; I have witnessed recoveries I could not explain, and I gave thanks. We should ask wholeheartedly for ourselves and for others, since James’s command rests in the compassion of Christ, who drew near to suffering bodies and touched people others avoided.

James joins bodily care to communal prayer, forgiveness, and confession. The sick person is received as a whole human being whose body, relationships, and life with God belong together. We should not reduce the passage to one promised physical result while ignoring its larger movement of restoration.

The elders come representing the praying community, acting in the Lord’s name. The oil is an outward sign of Christ’s touch placed upon a suffering body, and the prayer entrusts that body to Christ, whose mercy may bring cure, strength, forgiveness, and peace, even in the face of death.

Every genuine healing is a sign, since a healed body is still mortal and salvation is not yet complete in it. The sign points toward resurrection, when healing will not be temporary anymore and death will not reclaim what grace has restored.

YEARS AGO I PASSED a building in a large city that advertised daily miracles like those in Acts. The building stood empty. I wondered why no one had gathered if healing like that happened every day.

Scripture does not present healing as a transaction with guaranteed results. The popular formula says that sufficient faith must produce healing, so that continued illness exposes failure. It can quote verses, and it sounds confident, but its fruit reveals the damage.

The teaching hands a sick person a diagnosis and blame together. It stands at the hospital bed asking whether the sick man believed hard enough, and it leaves grieving families wondering whether unbelief caused a death. I have listened to people wounded by this theology, and what it produces is exhaustion, shame, and the fear that God is inspecting their trust for defects.

Scripture contradicts the formula. Paul saw others healed but could not pray away his thorn, and Christ said nothing about deficient faith. Paul advised Timothy to take a remedy for recurring stomach trouble (1 Timothy 5:23) and left Trophimus sick at Miletus without a word of blame (2 Timothy 4:20). Jesus sometimes withdrew from crowds seeking healing to pray (Luke 5:15‑16). His compassion was never something a crowd could demand.

A healing you could produce by technique would not be a miracle at all. It would be another kind of medicine. Medicine is a gift and should be used, but prayer belongs to a personal relation with God. James teaches both the prayer of faith and the words, “If the Lord wills, we shall live and we shall do this or that” (James 4:15). Faith trusts a Person whose wisdom is free.

If someone blamed your continued sickness on small faith, that teaching wounded you, and it was false. The affliction caused one pain and the verdict added another. Bring the verdict before God and reject it. Scripture never promises a life without bodily suffering before the Resurrection. Some of the most faithful people I have known were sick until the day they died, and their weakness did not reduce their place in Christ.

✦ ✦ ✦

NOT EVERY THORN IS physical illness. Persistent weakness may involve the mind, the memory, a family history, or a temptation resisted for decades. These conditions differ and should not be collapsed into one explanation. What they share is the experience of carrying something prayer has not removed.

A man who served God publicly once wrote to me for help. Before his calling he had assumed that public servants of the church lived above ordinary temptations. Grace, he imagined, dissolved disordered desire and sustained a nearly effortless holiness. Then his oldest struggle followed him into service.

He had begged God to remove it. He tried discipline, treatment, argument, and the passage of time, and the struggle outlasted them all. Sometimes he felt abandoned or angry, and in his worst hours he wondered whether his public service had become a lie.

I answered slowly, because his letter reached my own experience of carrying what prayer had not removed. Clergy are ordinary human beings under grace, and holiness does not mean the absence of struggle. Temptation itself is not consent, and persistent struggle does not prove hypocrisy. The decisive question is what we do with desire before God.

Holy people bring humiliating desires into the light. They seek forgiveness when they fall and practical help where it is needed, and they set wise boundaries against harm. They keep returning the whole self to God, and sanctity grows through that repeated consent to grace.

God can meet a person within the ache that stays. Desire is not a curse, and it is not an authority to obey either. It is a human power that can love or destroy, and Christ purifies it slowly, over a lifetime. Grace heals the soul’s disordered loves and directs them toward God and the true good of others.

Far from erasing the personality, this purification restores its freedom. A desire may go on making itself felt while losing the right to govern action or identity. Holiness means offering the whole self to God, the embarrassing part included, and allowing grace to establish a right order over time.

Ordered desire begins with the love of God as the soul’s highest good, and other desires then find their proper measure beneath that love. Hunger, affection, sexuality, ambition, anger, and the need for belonging are human powers. Sin twists what they reach for and how they reach for it, and grace restores in them the capacity to serve communion.

Purification can involve renunciation, for some desires cannot be acted upon faithfully. Yet Christian renunciation aims at freedom for love and holds no contempt for the body or the personality. Christ asks for the whole person and returns the person more truly himself.

This work stays unfinished in earthly life. We carry patterns that require vigilance, and sanctification often advances through repeated small obediences. The persistence of conflict may humble us without canceling the real transformation grace has already accomplished.

We must not claim to know why God permits each struggle to continue. What the man needed more urgently than an explanation was freedom from shame. Christ was not ashamed to receive him, and communion with Christ gave him a place from which to resist, to repent, and to serve honestly.

I once saw an old carving of the Crucifixion in which Christ’s right arm had come loose and reached downward toward the person standing below. The image stayed with me. Christ has already carried the Cross in its fullness, and His grace reaches down to the person beneath a burden and joins that person to His strength.

My thorn drove me into Scripture. A clergyman must open the Bible for others whether his own soul feels ordered or ruined, so week after week I returned to the text. At first I searched for a cure, some verse that would remove the struggle. Later I searched for a promise strong enough for the month.

Eventually I noticed that I had stopped searching for an exit and was simply staying with God in His Word. Scripture had become a room where shame could not rule, because its pages were filled with dependent people: Jacob limped, Moses struggled to speak, David carried a history, Jeremiah wept, and Paul bore a thorn.

The Bible did more than provide examples of weakness. Through it Christ addressed me, and corrected and fed me besides. The promise of a Kingdom without pain and tears gave present suffering a horizon beyond this life. The thorn never became good, but grace used it to press me toward the Shepherd.

This is one way weakness becomes spiritually fruitful. It returns us to sources of life we might otherwise treat as optional. Yet the fruit belongs to God. Without grace, community, and the discipline of returning to Scripture, the same affliction could simply have isolated me.

Paul went further than I have. He received the answer that grace would suffice, and he wrote:

For the sake of Christ, then, I am content with weaknesses, insults, hardships, persecutions, and calamities; for when I am weak, then I am strong. (2 Corinthians 12:10)

I will not pretend to take pleasure in the thorn, but I have begun to understand Paul’s desire that Christ’s power “rest upon” him (2 Corinthians 12:9). The verb carries the image of a tent pitched over a place. Paul, who made tents, describes weakness as ground covered by Christ’s presence.

Weakness and power occupy the same place. Christ’s strength does not always replace human frailty with visible competence. Sometimes it sustains love, obedience, and service within the frailty, and then the glory belongs clearly to Him, because the human vessel cannot claim self‑sufficiency.

A friend once told me that preaching strength perfected in weakness would never be popular. He was right. It offers no advertisement for effortless success. Its credibility comes from lives sustained by grace through conditions they would never have chosen.

I still ask occasionally for the thorn to go, though the request now resembles an old subject raised with an old friend. I stopped counting long ago. The emotional weather changed. I used to pray against the thorn like a man at war. Now I usually bring it with me as I come to God.

Christ says, “My grace is sufficient for you.” The present tense matters. Grace is sufficient for this morning and for this portion of weakness. I have never received a year’s supply in advance, and that daily sufficiency keeps dependence personal. My prayer has become simple:

Lord, You know what is still in me. Your grace was enough yesterday; let it be enough today.

So far, grace has been enough.

I will not decorate a lifelong No. A thorn continues to hurt, and its effects may stay hidden for years. I can say only what I have seen. God healed shame in a place where He did not remove weakness, and that healing was real even though it was incomplete.

The promise of sufficient grace does not forbid treatment, accountability, protection from harm, or renewed prayer. Grace often works through these means. It also directs us toward final healing in the Resurrection, where every faithful prayer for wholeness will receive an answer greater than present experience can contain.

Resurrection keeps sufficient grace from turning into resignation, as if God had made His peace with the damage. God does not intend an eternal coexistence with the thorn. He sustains us now while preparing the complete healing of creation, and the weakness that shaped our earthly life will not define our risen body.

This final hope also protects the present from despair. God gathers every act of resistance, every honest confession, every medical treatment, and every return to prayer into the healing He is preparing, and none of it is wasted, even when the thorn lasts until death.

Christ is not indifferent to pleading. In a garden, He Himself asked repeatedly that a cup be taken away. The asking, silence, tears, and refusal we have carried through these chapters now lead us among those trees.
