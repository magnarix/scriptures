Chapter Eight

The Thorn That Stays

✦ ✦ ✦

≈ 13 min read

FOR MOST OF MY adult life, I have carried what Paul called a thorn in the flesh. This chapter begins with confession because its teaching has cost me too much to present from a distance. I have prayed standing, kneeling, and face down. I have fasted and wept. People I trusted have laid their hands on me and asked with real faith that God remove it. I rose with my hair warm from their hands and the thorn unchanged.

After decades, the answer appears to be a lifelong No. I will not identify the thorn. Shame is part of the reason. Paul gives the better reason: he never named his own. Christians have proposed illness, injury, speech difficulty, persecution, and temptation. The guesses reveal our need to fill the silence.

Paul’s silence is a mercy. Because the affliction remains unnamed, people with very different wounds can recognize the promise addressed to weakness. His thorn cannot be reduced to every form of suffering, yet his testimony reaches beyond one diagnosis.

The chapter holds several truths together. God may refuse a faithful prayer repeatedly. The refusal does not prove weak faith or divine punishment. Healing prayer remains right because Scripture commands it and God still heals. No method guarantees the outcome. When the thorn remains, Christ promises sufficient grace and power perfected within weakness.

Sufficient grace means more than enough courage to endure another day. Grace is participation in the life of God. It forgives, heals, elevates, and draws the person into communion with Christ. The thorn may remain at one level while grace works more deeply in freedom, love, humility, and hope.

This does not make visible healing unimportant. Christianity confesses the goodness of the body and its future resurrection. Christ healed bodies as signs of the coming Kingdom. We continue asking because bodily suffering matters to Him and because final salvation includes the redemption of the body.

Present refusal therefore lives between two truths: Christ may heal now, and He will finally make His people whole in resurrection. The timing remains His. Hope neither abandons present prayer nor treats present suffering as the last word.

Paul was no spiritual novice. He had seen the risen Christ and witnessed extraordinary healings. Acts even records that cloths which touched him were carried to the sick (Acts 19:11‑12). He also described being caught up to Paradise and hearing what human speech could not repeat (2 Corinthians 12:2‑4). Then he wrote:

And lest I should be exalted above measure by the abundance of the revelations, a thorn in the flesh was given to me, a messenger of Satan to buffet me, lest I be exalted above measure. Concerning this thing I pleaded with the Lord three times that it might depart from me. And He said to me, “My grace is sufficient for you, for My strength is made perfect in weakness.” Therefore most gladly I will rather boast in my infirmities, that the power of Christ may rest upon me. (2 Corinthians 12:7‑9)

Paul “pleaded.” The apostle who taught persistent prayer begged for release. His three pleas may mark distinct seasons of asking. Then Christ gave a rare explanation: grace would remain sufficient, and divine strength would reach its completion in the weakness Paul wanted removed.

Paul understood the thorn as protection against pride. He repeats “lest I be exalted above measure.” Extraordinary revelation and humiliating weakness occupied the same life. The thorn kept spiritual privilege from becoming self‑exaltation.

Paul had kept his vision private for fourteen years. When he finally mentioned it, he spoke indirectly and refused to describe its secrets. He gave more attention to weakness. Our religious culture often reverses the emphasis. A minor experience becomes a platform or claim to authority. Paul boasts in what keeps him dependent upon Christ.

This changed my relation to shame. Weakness was no longer evidence that ministry had failed. It became the place where self‑sufficiency failed and Christ’s power could be recognized as His. I did not begin to admire the thorn. I began to stop treating dependence as disgrace.

The thorn humiliated me before other people and before God. I had absorbed the idea that mature Christians should move through each day almost effortlessly holy. When struggle continued, I blamed weak faith, insufficient effort, and sin. Each recurrence became a verdict. I felt unworthy before God and feared that people would be scandalized by a clergyman with an old unanswered prayer.

An older clergyman broke the isolation. I told him and waited for his expression to change. It did not. He reminded me that people we both admired had served God faithfully while carrying heavier afflictions throughout life. Grace had never depended upon their appearing invulnerable.

Every Christian remains a forgiven and dependent person. Public service does not create a separate class of souls. Many believers hide a thorn because grace has not “fixed” them in the expected way. Mine remained. By grace, I also remained.

The distinction between weakness and hypocrisy is important. Hypocrisy uses a holy appearance to conceal chosen falsehood. Weakness admits the truth, resists sin, seeks help, and depends upon mercy. A person may fail and need repentance without becoming a fraud in every act of service.

Grace also creates responsibility. The existence of a thorn cannot excuse harm to another person. Persistent temptation may require confession, accountability, treatment, or firm limits. Sufficient grace includes power to take these concrete steps, even when the underlying struggle remains.

The older clergyman’s unchanged face gave me room to tell the truth. Shame thrives in secrecy because secrecy makes the struggle appear unique. Wise companionship returns weakness to the common life of redeemed sinners and helps distinguish what must be accepted from what must be changed.

The continuing thorn does not cancel prayer for healing. Scripture gives a direct command:

Is anyone among you sick? Let him call for the elders of the church, and let them pray over him, anointing him with oil in the name of the Lord. And the prayer of faith will save the sick, and the Lord will raise him up. And if he has committed sins, he will be forgiven. Confess your trespasses to one another, and pray for one another, that you may be healed. The effective, fervent prayer of a righteous man avails much. (James 5:14‑16)

We obey this command without embarrassment. The sick call, and the elders pray and anoint with oil in the Lord’s name. We entrust the whole person to Christ, asking Him to strengthen, forgive, sustain, and heal according to His will. This act is prayerful obedience rather than a mechanism.

I believe God heals. I have witnessed recoveries I could not explain and gave thanks. We should ask wholeheartedly for ourselves and others. James’s command rests in the compassion of Christ, who drew near to suffering bodies and touched people others avoided.

James joins bodily care, communal prayer, forgiveness, and confession. The sick person is received as a whole human being whose body, relationships, and life with God belong together. We should not reduce the passage to one promised physical result while ignoring its larger movement of restoration.

The elders do not arrive as technicians. They represent the praying community and act in the Lord’s name. Oil remains a created sign placed upon a suffering body. The prayer entrusts that body to Christ, whose mercy may bring cure, strength, forgiveness, peace, or preparation for death.

Every genuine healing is a sign rather than the completion of salvation. A healed body remains mortal. The sign points toward resurrection, when healing will no longer be temporary and death will no longer reclaim what grace has restored.

Years ago I passed a building in a large city that advertised daily miracles like those in Acts. The building stood empty. I wondered why no one had gathered if such healing occurred every day. Then I walked on.

Scripture does not present healing as a transaction with guaranteed results. The popular formula says that sufficient faith must produce healing, so continued illness exposes failure. It can quote verses and sounds confident. Its fruit reveals the damage.

The teaching gives a sick person both a diagnosis and blame. It audits faith at the hospital bed and leaves grieving families wondering whether unbelief caused a death. I have listened to those wounded by this theology. It produces exhaustion, shame, and fear that God is inspecting trust for defects.

Scripture contradicts the formula. Paul saw others healed but could not pray away his thorn. Christ said nothing about deficient faith. Paul advised Timothy to use a remedy for recurring stomach trouble (1 Timothy 5:23). He left Trophimus sick at Miletus without blame (2 Timothy 4:20). Jesus sometimes withdrew from crowds seeking healing to pray (Luke 5:15‑16). Divine compassion remained free rather than governed by demand.

A predictable technique would no longer describe a miracle. It would function like medicine. Medicine is a gift and should be used, but prayer belongs to a personal relation with God. James teaches both the prayer of faith and the words, “If the Lord wills, we shall live and do this or that” (James 4:15). Faith trusts a Person whose wisdom remains free.

If someone blamed your continued sickness on small faith, the teaching wounded you and was false. Affliction caused one pain; the verdict added another. Bring that verdict before God and reject it. Scripture never promises a life without bodily suffering before the Resurrection. Some of the most faithful people I have known remained sick until death. Their weakness did not reduce their place in Christ.

NOT EVERY THORN IS physical illness. Persistent weakness may involve the mind, memory, family history, or a temptation resisted for decades. These conditions differ and should not be collapsed into one explanation. They share the experience of carrying something prayer has not removed.

A man who served God publicly once wrote to me for help. Before his calling, he assumed that public servants of the Church lived above ordinary temptations. Grace, he imagined, dissolved disordered desire and sustained a nearly effortless holiness. Then his oldest struggle followed him into service.

He had begged God to remove it. He tried discipline, treatment, argument, and the passage of time. The struggle remained. Sometimes he felt abandoned or angry. In his worst hours, he wondered whether his public service had become a lie.

I answered slowly because I was writing to myself as well. Clergy remain ordinary human beings under grace. Holiness does not mean the absence of struggle. Temptation itself is not consent, and persistent struggle does not prove hypocrisy. The decisive question concerns what we do with desire before God.

Holy people bring humiliating desires into the light. They seek forgiveness when they fall, practical help where needed, and wise boundaries against harm. They keep returning the whole self to God. Sanctity grows through this repeated consent to grace.

God can meet a person within the ache that remains. Desire is neither a curse nor an authority to obey. It is a human power capable of love or destruction. Christ purifies desire until the fire becomes an altar. Grace heals the soul’s disordered loves and directs them toward God and the true good of others.

This purification does not erase personality. It restores freedom. A desire may continue to make itself felt while losing the right to govern action or identity. Holiness means offering the whole self to God, including the part that causes embarrassment, and allowing grace to establish a right order over time.

Ordered desire begins with the love of God as the soul’s highest good. Other desires then find their proper measure beneath that love. Hunger, affection, sexuality, ambition, anger, and the need for belonging are human powers. Sin distorts their object or manner. Grace restores their capacity to serve communion.

Purification can involve renunciation. Some desires cannot be acted upon faithfully. Yet Christian renunciation aims at freedom for love rather than contempt for the body or personality. Christ asks for the whole person and returns the person more truly himself.

This work remains unfinished in earthly life. We carry patterns that require vigilance, and sanctification often advances through repeated small obediences. The persistence of conflict may humble us. It does not cancel the real transformation grace has already accomplished.

We must not claim to know why God permits each struggle to continue. The man needed freedom from shame more urgently than an explanation. Christ was not ashamed to receive him, and communion with Christ gave him a place from which to resist, repent, and serve honestly.

I once saw an old carving of the Crucifixion in which Christ’s right arm had come loose and reached downward toward the person standing below. The image stayed with me. Christ has already carried the Cross in its fullness. His grace reaches the person beneath a burden and joins that person to His strength.

My thorn drove me into Scripture. A clergyman must open the Bible for others whether his own soul feels ordered or ruined. Week after week, I returned to the text. At first I searched for a cure, some verse that would remove the struggle. Later I searched for a promise strong enough for the month.

Eventually I noticed that I was no longer searching for an exit. I was remaining with God in His Word. Scripture had become a room where shame could not rule because its pages were filled with dependent people: Jacob limped, Moses struggled to speak, David carried a history, Jeremiah wept, and Paul bore a thorn.

The Bible did more than provide examples of weakness. Through it, Christ addressed, corrected, and nourished me. The promise of a Kingdom without pain and tears gave present suffering an eschatological horizon. The thorn did not become good. Grace used it to press me toward the Shepherd.

This is one way weakness becomes spiritually fruitful. It returns us to sources of life we might otherwise treat as optional. Yet the fruit belongs to God. The same affliction could have isolated me without grace, community, and the discipline of returning to Scripture.

Paul went further than I have. He received the answer that grace would suffice and wrote:

Therefore I take pleasure in infirmities, in reproaches, in needs, in persecutions, in distresses, for Christ’s sake. For when I am weak, then I am strong. (2 Corinthians 12:10)

I will not pretend to take pleasure in the thorn. I have begun to understand Paul’s desire that Christ’s power “rest upon” him (2 Corinthians 12:9). The verb carries the image of a tent pitched over a place. Paul, who made tents, describes weakness as ground covered by Christ’s presence.

Weakness and power occupy the same place. Christ’s strength does not always replace human frailty with visible competence. It may sustain love, obedience, and service within frailty. The glory belongs clearly to Him because the human vessel cannot claim self‑sufficiency.

A friend once told me that preaching strength perfected in weakness would never be popular. He was right. It offers no advertisement for effortless success. Its credibility comes from lives sustained by grace through conditions they would never have chosen.

I still ask occasionally for the thorn to go. The request now resembles an old subject raised with an old friend. I stopped counting long ago. The emotional weather changed. I once prayed against the thorn like a man at war. Now I usually bring it with me as I come to God.

Christ says, “My grace is sufficient for you.” The present tense matters. Grace is sufficient for this morning and this portion of weakness. I have never received a year’s supply in advance. Daily sufficiency keeps dependence personal. My prayer has become simple:

Lord, You know what is still in me. Your grace was enough yesterday; let it be enough today.

So far, grace has been enough. I will not decorate a lifelong No. A thorn continues to hurt, and its effects may remain hidden for years. I can say only what I have seen: God healed shame in a place where He did not remove weakness. That healing was real even though it was incomplete.

The promise of sufficient grace does not forbid treatment, accountability, protection from harm, or renewed prayer. Grace often works through these means. It also directs us toward final healing in the Resurrection, where every faithful prayer for wholeness will receive an answer greater than present experience can contain.

Resurrection keeps sufficient grace from becoming mere accommodation to damage. God does not intend eternal coexistence with the thorn. He sustains us now while preparing the complete healing of creation. The weakness that shaped our earthly life will not define our risen body.

This final hope also protects the present from despair. Every act of resistance, every honest confession, every medical treatment, and every return to prayer participates in God’s movement toward wholeness. None is wasted, even when the thorn remains until death.

Christ is not indifferent to pleading. In a garden, He Himself asked repeatedly that a cup be taken away. The asking, silence, tears, and refusal we have carried through these chapters now lead us among those trees.
