Chapter Sixteen

When God Answers a Prayer

✦ ✦ ✦

≈ 10 min read

WHAT HAPPENS WHEN GOD answers prayer? He acts within His providence, gives or withholds according to His wisdom, and draws the person who prays into deeper communion with Himself. The outward gift is real, but it is never isolated from the Giver. Grace enlarges our capacity to receive both.

The central claim of this book can now be stated in full: An answered prayer, seen whole, turns out to be a person: the one you have become in the hands of God, to whom you kept bringing your requests.

This definition does not turn every answer into psychology. God truly acts. Circumstances change, bodies may heal, doors open, and material needs are met. Nor does it mean that a person who remains unchanged has earned the right outcome. It means that God’s purpose reaches beyond the immediate request. He wills our communion with Him, and every gift finds its proper end within that communion.

I learned this through the longest prayer of my life.

As a young man finishing my studies, I felt called to spend my life serving God’s people. I told a respected teacher whose blessing I expected. The response was immediate. I should remain in the work for which I had trained; this other desire wasted a good mind.

I left wounded and buried the calling for years. Other respected voices later expressed similar judgments. Their opposition raised a painful question: if the desire came from God, why did people with legitimate authority and wisdom stand in its way?

The desire persisted. Persistence can be one sign of vocation, though desire alone proves nothing. A call to serve the Church involves interior discernment and recognition by the Church. Neither can simply cancel the other.

I tried to starve the desire and cover it with other work. Still, I kept writing about God. Readers sometimes assumed I already held a formal ministry. People sought teaching, prayer, and pastoral care from me even though I held no office. I dreamed of serving God’s people and woke with an ache I would not name. For a long time, I could not even speak the desire directly in prayer.

Practical obligations also stood in the way. The path appeared financially impossible. I studied the numbers for years and found no honest route. My repeated prayer became simple: my humanity cannot see the path.

I surrendered the calling, took it back, and surrendered it again. Twice I tried to abandon it. Older men who guided me urged me to rest instead. The desire remained after every rest.

This continued for decades. If answered prayer means the prompt arrival of the requested result, the prayer failed for thirty years. That measure is inadequate because it notices only the final event and ignores the work of grace throughout the waiting.

Abraham asked for a son and waited twenty‑five years. During those years, he learned the faithfulness of God, who had made the promise. We should not romanticize the waiting. Abraham sometimes failed, acted from fear, and tried to secure the promise by his own means. Yet God remained faithful and formed Abraham through the long history of promise and fulfillment.

Desire often begins too narrow for its object. It wants fulfillment without transformation. My first desire for ministry contained mixed motives. I wanted to serve, but I also wanted recognition. I wanted the original judgment against me to be overturned. The calling may have been real while my way of carrying it still needed purification.

Delay, frustration, and uncertainty exposed those motives. The romance of ministry faded as I learned that service is ordinary labor, often hidden and thankless. Formal office gives no private access to God. The desire for authoritative recognition also weakened. I gradually learned to serve the people already before me, teach those who asked, and pray with those who suffered.

The heart of the calling could be lived before the office arrived. This did not make formal recognition unimportant. It freed the vocation from depending on status. I could finally tell God that I would receive the office with gratitude or remain without it and still possess Him.

Prayer is therefore a school of communion. God is life to be entered, not power to be leveraged. Petition brings our actual desires into relationship with the living God, where they can be corrected, purified, strengthened, or fulfilled.

The answer to a long prayer may begin before fulfillment becomes visible. Paul helps us understand why:

For all the promises of God in Him are Yes, and in Him Amen, to the glory of God through us. (2 Corinthians 1:20)

A divine promise is covenantal speech. It declares what God will do and calls His people to trust the One who speaks. All God’s promises find their Yes in Christ. We must therefore resist detaching a biblical promise from Christ and treating it as a private guarantee of our preferred outcome.

God’s promises form the people who receive them. Trust learns endurance. Hope is purified. Obedience becomes possible before sight. Fulfillment reveals the faithfulness that sustained the whole period of waiting. The waiting does not earn the promise, but grace can use it to prepare us to receive what God gives.

WHEN THE CALLING FINALLY appeared possible, a different obstacle remained. The path required the consent of people rightly entrusted with its discernment. Their agreement was uncertain. After years of ridicule and financial impossibility, I feared that a few human decisions would close the way.

One night I dreamed that I was arguing with Jesus. I report the dream as an experience, not as revelation binding on anyone else.

“What if they never agree?” I asked Him. “If they say no, will You say no with them?”

And He said, “I am not beneath them. I am the Head of My Church.”

The dream gave me no permission to defy anyone. If the calling came from Christ, He could open the way through the very people whose consent was required. If they refused after honest discernment, their decision would still fall within His providence. Christ’s headship does not make the Church’s discernment optional.

Private experiences must remain under Scripture. They add nothing necessary to faith, and a Christian who never has such an experience lacks nothing. The words in my dream repeated a public truth: Christ is the Head of His Church. I received them as reassurance, never as proof or authority to bypass others.

The way eventually opened late in my life, through people and means I had never anticipated. For years I had imagined the few routes by which it might happen. God used none of them. I will not give the details because they also belong to other people, and no one should turn my path into a pattern for his own.

The years taught me that my inability to see a path does not define what God can do. At the same time, confidence in God’s power does not guarantee that every inward desire is His calling. He may open or close the way through communal discernment. Vocation is received as gift, never possessed as a private entitlement.

When the door opened, I saw that the waiting had changed me. The young man who first spoke to his teacher wanted recognition and vindication. The older man had been corrected, freed from some need for applause, and taught to hold the calling more lightly. Earlier fulfillment might have placed the same office in the hands of a substantially different person.

Jeremiah spoke to exiles who would wait seventy years for a return home:

Then you will call upon Me and go and pray to Me, and I will listen to you. And you will seek Me and find Me, when you search for Me with all your heart. (Jeremiah 29:12‑13)

These verses are often detached from exile and used as a promise of quick personal success. Their original setting says the opposite. God promised to be found by a displaced people during a long covenantal restoration. Before Israel returned to Jerusalem, the exiles were called to seek God with their whole heart. Their deepest homecoming began in renewed communion with Him.

The final prayer we must consider is Christ’s own. On the night of His betrayal, Jesus prayed for Himself and for the disciples at the table. Then He prayed for future believers:

I do not pray for these alone, but also for those who will believe in Me through their word. (John 17:20)

Every Christian has received the apostolic witness in some form. It has traveled through Scripture, preaching, teaching, and the ordinary faithfulness of believers. If you believe in Jesus through that witness, you stand within His prayer. Before you prayed for the first time, Christ had prayed for people fitting your description.

In the same prayer, Jesus defines eternal life:

And this is eternal life, that they may know You, the only true God, and Jesus Christ whom You have sent. (John 17:3)

Eternal life is personal knowledge of the Father through the Son. It begins now and reaches fulfillment in the age to come. Christian tradition speaks of participation in the divine nature (cf. 2 Peter 1:4): creatures remain creatures, yet through Christ and in the Spirit we truly share by grace in God’s life. Salvation gives us more than endless existence. It gives communion with God.

Our prayers belong within Christ’s prayer. He seeks our sanctification, unity, preservation, and final presence with Him. Every particular answer must serve that larger saving purpose. Daily bread, healing, guidance, and vocation become gifts within the communion for which Christ prayed.

The Church calls our union with Christ incorporation because we become members of His body. We receive as participants in His life, and our desires gradually learn the pattern of His love. Paul turns this mystery into praise:

Now to Him who is able to do exceedingly abundantly above all that we ask or think, according to the power that works in us, to Him be glory in the church by Christ Jesus to all generations, forever and ever. Amen. (Ephesians 3:20‑21)

The power that exceeds our asking is already at work within God’s people. Its purpose is not merely to increase our possessions or magnify our plans. Paul has prayed that believers would be strengthened, rooted in love, and filled with the fullness of God. The abundance of verse 20 answers that prayer.

Romans 8 gives this formation a face. Paul writes, “And we know that all things work together for good to those who love God, to those who are the called according to His purpose” (Romans 8:28). Those whom God foreknew are “predestined to be conformed to the image of His Son” (Romans 8:29). God does not call evil good. He brings His children through all things toward a glory shaped like the Son. Paul then asks, “If God is for us, who can be against us?” (Romans 8:31).

Paul grounds this confidence in the cross:

He who did not spare His own Son, but delivered Him up for us all, how shall He not with Him also freely give us all things? (Romans 8:32)

The words with Him govern the promise. God has given His Son, and every saving good comes in union with Him. Romans 8 does not promise every conceivable object of desire. It promises that nothing needed for God’s redemptive purpose will be withheld from those who are in Christ.

The cross reveals God’s settled intention toward us. The Father did not spare His Son but delivered Him for us, and the Son freely gave Himself in love. Prayer therefore begins under demonstrated grace, not uncertainty about whether God is fundamentally for His children.

We can now gather the chapter’s answer. God may grant the specific request, deny it, delay it, or answer through means we did not expect. The request matters because creaturely goods and sorrows matter to Him. Yet the complete work of answered prayer also includes the formation of the person who asks. Desire learns consent, capacity grows, and life is drawn more deeply into Christ.

This formation is not a substitute offered when God fails to act. It is one dimension of His action. Nor should every inner change be called an answer to the original petition. A request for healing remains a request for healing. If healing does not come, honesty requires lament rather than verbal rearrangement. Still, no faithful prayer is wasted. God receives it, sustains the one who prays, and gathers it into His saving purpose, even when the requested outcome remains absent.

At hospital bedsides, beside graves, and in the hours before dawn, our requests eventually reach a deeper question: Are we and those we love descending into silence, or are we held in faithful hands? Christianity answers through an event in history. Early one morning, the tomb of Jesus was found empty. That morning is where this book must end.
