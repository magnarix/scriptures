Chapter Sixteen

When God Answers a Prayer

✦ ✦ ✦

≈ 10 min read

WHAT HAPPENS WHEN GOD answers prayer? He acts within His providence. He gives or withholds as His wisdom sees fit. And all the while He is drawing the one who prays closer to Himself. The outward gift is real, but it is never isolated from the Giver, and grace enlarges our capacity to receive both.

Here is the central claim of this book, stated in full. An answered prayer, seen whole, turns out to be a person, the one you have become in the hands of God, to whom you kept bringing your requests.

This definition does not turn every answer into psychology, for God truly acts: circumstances change, bodies may heal, doors open, and material needs are met. And it does not mean that an unchanged person has earned the right outcome. God’s purpose simply reaches further than the immediate request. He wills our communion with Him, and every gift finds its proper end within that communion.

I learned this through the longest prayer of my life.

As a young man finishing my studies, I felt called to spend my life serving God’s people, and I told a respected teacher whose blessing I expected. The answer came at once. I should stay in the work for which I had trained; this other desire wasted a good mind.

I left wounded and buried the calling for years. Other respected voices later said much the same, and their opposition raised a painful question. If the desire came from God, why did people with legitimate authority and wisdom stand in its way?

The desire persisted, and persistence can be one sign of vocation, though desire alone proves nothing. A call to serve the church involves both interior discernment and confirmation by the church; desire alone does not establish the call.

I tried to starve the desire and cover it with other work, but I kept writing about God, and readers sometimes assumed I already held a formal ministry. People sought teaching, prayer, and pastoral care from me even though I had no office. I dreamed of serving God’s people and woke with the ache still there. For a long time I could not even speak the desire directly in prayer.

Practical obligations also stood in the way. The path looked financially impossible, and I studied the numbers for years without finding an honest route. My repeated prayer became simple: my humanity cannot see the path.

I surrendered the calling, took it back, and surrendered it again. Twice I tried to abandon it, and the older men who guided me urged me to rest instead. After every rest, the desire was still there.

This continued for decades. If answered prayer means the prompt arrival of what you asked for, then the prayer failed for thirty years; but that measure notices only the final event and ignores the work of grace throughout the waiting.

Abraham asked for a son and waited twenty‑five years, and during those years he learned the faithfulness of the God who had made the promise. We should not romanticize the waiting. Abraham sometimes failed, acted from fear, and tried to secure the promise by his own means. Yet God stayed faithful and formed him through the long history of promise and fulfillment.

Desire often begins too narrow for its object, wanting fulfillment without transformation. My first desire for ministry contained mixed motives. I wanted to serve, but I also wanted recognition, and I wanted the original judgment against me to be overturned. The calling may have been real while my way of carrying it still needed purification.

Delay and frustration and sheer uncertainty exposed those motives; the romance of ministry faded as I learned that service is ordinary labor, often hidden and thankless. Formal office gives no private access to God. The desire for authoritative recognition weakened too, and I gradually learned to serve the people already before me, teach those who asked, and pray with those who suffered.

The heart of the calling could be lived before the office arrived. Formal recognition still mattered, but the vocation no longer depended on status, and I could finally tell God that I would receive the office with gratitude or live without it and still possess Him.

Prayer is therefore a school of communion. God is a life we enter, not a power we put to use. Petition brings our actual desires into relationship with the living God, where they can be corrected, purified, strengthened, or fulfilled.

The answer to a long prayer may begin before fulfillment becomes visible, and Paul helps us understand why:

For all the promises of God find their Yes in him. That is why we utter the Amen through him, to the glory of God. (2 Corinthians 1:20)

A divine promise is covenantal speech. It declares what God will do and calls His people to trust the One who speaks. All God’s promises find their Yes in Christ, so we must not detach a biblical promise from Him and treat it as a private guarantee of our preferred outcome.

God’s promises form the people who receive them. He works at the speed of hearts, which is slow. Over the years the one who waits learns to endure and to obey before he can see, and his hope is purified. Fulfillment, when it comes, reveals the faithfulness that sustained the whole period of waiting. The waiting does not earn the promise, but grace can use it to prepare us to receive what God gives.

✦ ✦ ✦

WHEN THE CALLING FINALLY looked possible, one obstacle was left: the path required the consent of those the church entrusts to discern such a calling, and their agreement was uncertain. After years of ridicule and financial impossibility, I feared that a few human decisions would close the way.

One night I dreamed that I was arguing with Jesus. I tell it as an experience, not as revelation binding on anyone else.

“What if they never agree?” I asked Him. “If they say no, will You say no with them?”

And He said, “I am not beneath them. I am the Head of My Church.”

The dream gave me no permission to defy anyone. If the calling came from Christ, He could open the way through the very people whose consent was required; and if they refused after honest discernment, their decision would still fall within His providence. Christ’s headship does not make the church’s discernment optional.

Private experiences must stay under Scripture and the judgment of the church. They add nothing necessary to faith, and a Christian who never has such an experience is at no disadvantage. The words in my dream repeated a public truth, that Christ is the Head of His Church. I received them as reassurance, never as proof or authority to bypass others.

The way eventually opened late in my life, through people and means I had never anticipated. For years I had imagined the few routes by which it might happen; God used none of them. I will not give the details, because they also belong to other people and no one should turn my path into a pattern for his own.

The years taught me that my inability to see a path does not define what God can do, though confidence in His power is no guarantee that every inward desire is His calling. He may open or close the way through communal discernment, because vocation comes as gift and no one owns it as a private entitlement.

When the door opened, I saw that the waiting had changed me. The young man who first spoke to his teacher wanted recognition and vindication. The older man had been corrected, freed from some need for applause, and taught to hold the calling more lightly. Earlier fulfillment might have placed the same office in the hands of a substantially different person.

JEREMIAH SPOKE TO EXILES who would wait seventy years for a return home:

Then you will call upon me and come and pray to me, and I will hear you. You will seek me and find me; when you seek me with all your heart. (Jeremiah 29:12‑13)

These verses are often detached from exile and used as a promise of quick personal success, but their original setting says the opposite. God promised to be found by a displaced people during a long covenantal restoration. Before Israel returned to Jerusalem, the exiles were called to seek God with their whole heart, and their deepest homecoming began in renewed communion with Him.

✦ ✦ ✦

THE FINAL PRAYER WE must consider is Christ’s own. On the night of His betrayal, Jesus prayed for Himself and for the disciples at the table, and then He prayed for future believers:

I do not pray for these only, but also for those who believe in me through their word. (John 17:20)

Every Christian has received the apostolic witness in some form, for it has traveled through Scripture, preaching, teaching, and the ordinary faithfulness of believers. If you believe in Jesus through that witness, you stand within His prayer. Before you prayed for the first time, Christ had prayed for you.

In the same prayer, Jesus defines eternal life:

And this is eternal life, that they know thee the only true God, and Jesus Christ whom thou hast sent. (John 17:3)

Eternal life is personal knowledge of the Father through the Son; it begins now and reaches fulfillment in the age to come. Scripture speaks of our becoming partakers of the divine nature (2 Peter 1:4). Creatures remain creatures, yet through Christ and in the Spirit we truly share by grace in God’s life. Salvation gives us communion with God, which is far more than endless existence.

Our prayers belong within Christ’s prayer. He seeks our sanctification, unity, preservation, and final presence with Him, and every particular answer must serve that larger saving purpose. Daily bread, healing, guidance, and vocation become gifts within the communion for which Christ prayed.

Scripture calls our union with Christ membership in His body, for we are joined to Him as members of one body (1 Corinthians 12:12‑13). We share in His life, and our desires gradually learn the pattern of His love. Paul turns this mystery into praise:

Now to him who by the power at work within us is able to do far more abundantly than all that we ask or think, to him be glory in the church and in Christ Jesus to all generations, for ever and ever. Amen. (Ephesians 3:20‑21)

The power that exceeds our asking is already at work within God’s people, and its purpose is larger than increased possessions or magnified plans. Paul has prayed that believers would be strengthened, rooted in love, and filled with the fullness of God, and the abundance of verse 20 answers that prayer.

Romans 8 gives this formation a face. Paul writes, “We know that in everything God works for good with those who love him, who are called according to his purpose” (Romans 8:28). Those whom God foreknew are “predestined to be conformed to the image of his Son” (Romans 8:29). God does not call evil good; He brings His children through all things toward a glory shaped like the Son. Paul then asks, “If God is for us, who is against us?” (Romans 8:31).

He grounds this confidence in the cross:

He who did not spare his own Son but gave him up for us all, will he not also give us all things with him? (Romans 8:32)

The words with Him govern the promise. God has given His Son, and every saving good comes in union with Him. Romans 8 does not promise us every conceivable object of desire. It promises that nothing needed for God’s redemptive purpose will be withheld from those who are in Christ.

The cross reveals God’s settled intention toward us. The Father did not spare His Son but delivered Him for us, and the Son freely gave Himself in love. Prayer therefore begins under demonstrated grace, because the question of whether God is fundamentally for His children has already been answered.

We can now answer the question with which this chapter began. God may grant the specific request, deny it, delay it, or answer through means we did not expect, and the request matters because creaturely goods and sorrows matter to Him. Yet the complete work of answered prayer also includes the formation of the person who asks. He learns to consent to what God gives. His capacity to receive grows, and his life is drawn more deeply into Christ.

This formation is itself one dimension of God’s action, not a substitute offered when He fails to act. Nor should every inner change be called an answer to the original petition. A request for healing means what it says. If healing does not come, the honest response is lament, and we should not dress the loss up as an answer. Still, no faithful prayer is wasted. God receives it, sustains the one who prays, and gathers it into His saving purpose, even when the outcome we asked for never arrives.

At a hospital bedside, or beside a grave, our requests eventually reach a deeper question. Are we and those we love simply descending into silence, or is someone holding us? Christianity answers through an event in history. Early one morning, the tomb of Jesus was found empty. That morning is where this book must end.
