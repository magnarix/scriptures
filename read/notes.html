Notes

✦ ✦ ✦

≈ 2 min read

SCRIPTURE QUOTATIONS ARE TAKEN from the New King James Version®. Copyright © 1982 by Thomas Nelson. Used by permission. All rights reserved.

Much of this book adapts reflections, homilies, and essays I first wrote and published over many years of ministry; they appear here revised, combined, and expanded.

Prologue. The psalm that anchors these pages is Psalm 34:6; Job’s confession is Job 1:21.

Chapter 1. C. S. Lewis’s counsel that we must lay before God “what is in us, not what ought to be in us” is from Letter IV of Letters to Malcolm: Chiefly on Prayer (New York: Harcourt, Brace & World, 1964), 20–23. Augustine’s teaching that God commands our asking so that the asking may enlarge our desire is from Letter 130 to Proba, 8.17 (A.D. 412).

Chapter 5. Mother Teresa’s words, “I look and do not see, listen and do not hear,” are from a September 1979 letter to Michael van der Peet, published in Mother Teresa: Come Be My Light, edited by Brian Kolodiejchuk, M.C. (New York: Doubleday, 2007), 288.

Chapter 11. The bishop’s words to Monica, traditionally translated as “it is impossible that the son of these tears should perish,” and the surrounding episode are from Augustine’s Confessions, 3.12.21. Müller’s account of praying for five people appears in A Narrative of Some of the Lord’s Dealings with George Müller, in the entry beginning November 1844. Later reports of the final two conversions vary in chronology; the text therefore treats the posthumous details as biographical tradition rather than established fact. See also Arthur T. Pierson, George Müller of Bristol: His Witness to a Prayer‑Hearing God (New York: Fleming H. Revell, 1899).

Epilogue. The description of the traditional Anastasis icon, including Christ raising Adam and Eve by the wrists, follows the iconographic explanation in Greek Orthodox Archdiocese of America, “The Icon of the Resurrection,” https://www.goarch.org/-/the‑icon‑of‑the‑resurrection.

A Simple Way to Pray. The rhythm of morning, evening, and midday prayer follows Psalm 55:17 and Daniel 6:10.

All persons in this book are real, and I have told their stories as faithfully as memory allows. Names, places, and identifying details have been omitted or altered to protect their privacy, and a few accounts are composites drawn from many similar conversations, presented as such in the text.
