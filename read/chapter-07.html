Chapter Seven

How Prayer Survives Disappointment

✦ ✦ ✦

≈ 13 min read

A MAN WHO HAD been like a father to me approached death, and I discovered the limits of my composure. He had shaped my character and my life, and when the doctor told the family that everything possible had been done, a pillar within me seemed to shake. I had prayed as a son prays, and the answer was no.

Years of preaching resurrection had not trained my heart to lose this man. The doctrine held, but it did not remove the grief, and what I knew and what I could bear felt like two different countries. I begin here because I have lived inside disappointment rather than studying it from a safe distance.

Christian hope does not ask us to treat death as harmless. Death is an enemy, one Christ has defeated and will finally abolish, but until the resurrection is fully revealed, love still experiences separation as a wound. Tears can live beside firm belief in the life to come.

This distinction matters pastorally. A grieving believer may know every true sentence and still feel unable to stand beneath its weight. A sharper explanation will not help him. He may need another person to bear the truth beside him until his heart can receive it again.

My disappointment was also filial. I had asked God for the life of someone who had given so much shape to mine, and the refusal touched everything at once: prayer, memory, gratitude, fear of a future without him. Such grief has to be brought whole into communion, because it cannot be reduced to one theological problem.

Disappointment usually appears as fatigue rather than rebellion. A man once wrote to me that he had formerly prayed with confidence and joy, and that now he felt numb and hurt and could not pray as before. He asked whether he had lost his faith. I have heard the same question in many forms from people who would never put it in a letter.

The heart learns self‑protection quietly. A request goes unanswered, then another, until hope becomes expensive and asking feels dangerous. The person goes on worshiping and serving and praying for others, and one desire drops out of the conversation with God. This is often a flinch rather than a settled rejection of faith.

I told him that exhaustion did not prove the death of faith. Elijah once lay beneath a broom tree and asked to die shortly after a great answer to prayer, and God gave him sleep and food, twice, before speaking about the future. Grace attended to his exhausted body before assigning another task. When the wish to die outlasts sleep and food, the same grace works through doctors and wise companions, and asking for their help is another way of receiving His care.

Isaiah says of the Servant, “A bruised reed he will not break, and a dimly burning wick he will not quench” (Isaiah 42:3). A dimly burning wick is a good image for wounded faith: the flame may be hard to see, but the smoke tells you an ember is still there. Faith does not always look like fire.

The man’s letter turned toward God even while it described the numbness. Someone entirely finished with the relationship would not ask whether its silence meant the end. His question carried the wounded desire to believe, and the complaint about prayer was itself already a kind of prayer.

Faith has seasons because human beings have bodies and histories and limits. Confidence may weaken after an illness, a bereavement, a long strain, and the weakening is no proof of moral or spiritual failure. Elijah needed sleep and food because grace addresses the whole creature.

The faith of the whole church can also carry a person whose own strength has failed. We confess, sing, and pray with others partly because no one is equally strong in every season, and when I cannot say the words with conviction, I let the community say them around me until I can join in again.

There is no hypocrisy in this borrowed strength. It is communion. The body of Christ lets one member’s hope support another member’s wound. The tired believer does not have to manufacture fire. All that is asked of him is enough consent to stay near the hearth.

✦ ✦ ✦

I LEARNED THIS AT a door of my own. During a dark period, several prayers seemed to go unanswered, and I grew tired of hoping, more tired still of speaking publicly about hope. In that exhaustion I prayed words I still remember:

Jesus, I want You out of my life. Leave my heart and stay beyond that door.

No lightning followed, and neither did relief. Scripture contains a similar request. After Jesus healed the man among the tombs, the townspeople “began to beg Jesus to depart from their neighborhood” (Mark 5:17). Many of us ask Him to leave through indifference while avoiding the words. I had said them out loud.

I could not stop looking toward the imagined door. In prayer I pictured Jesus waiting beyond it, not forcing the handle. Later I recognized the contradiction. I had told Jesus to leave by speaking to Jesus, so even my rejection was still conversation. My anger had not ended the relationship, because Christ’s patience was greater than my demand.

When does expectation become entitlement? That is where disappointment turns spiritually dangerous. Expectation brings a desire to God. Entitlement goes further and decides beforehand what a refusal will mean. The two may make the very same request and differ only in the judgment passed when the request is refused.

The Gospel heals disappointment without teaching us to want less. Jesus teaches us to ask for daily bread, and the desire may stay intense enough to wake us at night. Faith simply stops treating one outcome as the judge of the entire relationship.

Prayer is vulnerable because love is vulnerable. Refusing to ask can prevent one kind of disappointment, but it also closes the heart against receiving. Some believers call this lowered expectation maturity, when often it is a wound protecting itself, and the protected heart becomes like a shuttered house. Less gets in, the good along with the bad.

Expectation accepts the risk that comes with loving, so it can rejoice when the gift comes and grieve when it does not, and neither response is easy. None of this requires indifference to outcomes. The sick person wants healing, the parent wants the child home, the grieving family wants death itself reversed, and Christian surrender keeps those desires alive while refusing to turn them into demands on God.

Grace purifies expectation gradually. We may begin by asking God to confirm our plan, and through long prayer the desire becomes more truthful and less possessive. It opens to goods we could not foresee. The original longing may still be there, though its relation to God has changed.

Chapter 9 will face the relation between suffering and the Father’s love at the Cross. The pastoral task here is smaller and more immediate. A painful answer gives us no right to turn grief into a final verdict about God, and we may lament what happened while leaving judgment of the whole relationship open to His revealed character.

✦ ✦ ✦

MARK SHOWS CHRIST RECEIVING hope that arrives wounded. A father brought his suffering son after the disciples had failed to heal him, while the scribes argued and the crowd watched. Years of pain shaped the father’s request: “If you can do anything, have pity on us and help us” (Mark 9:22). Jesus answered, “If you can! All things are possible to him who believes” (Mark 9:23). The father replied:

Immediately the father of the child cried out and said, “I believe; help my unbelief!” (Mark 9:24)

Jesus received belief and unbelief in the same prayer. He let the man’s honesty, with no felt certainty behind it, become the place of encounter. Faith here means staying turned toward Christ while admitting everything that weakens the turning.

The Psalms place lament, protest, and plain confusion inside faith. “How long, O Lord?” is prayer (Psalm 13:1). “Why dost thou hide thy face from me?” is prayer (Psalm 88:14). Lament protests because it still expects God’s promise to be true, and Scripture gives sorrow a language while teaching the worshiping community to speak it together.

Lament has a structure even when emotion feels chaotic. It addresses God, describes suffering, remembers His character, asks for help, and often reaches toward praise. Not every lament moves neatly through each part. Psalm 88 ends in darkness, and its presence in Scripture tells us that unresolved prayer still belongs to the worship of God’s people.

The address is crucial. Pain we only tell ourselves goes round in circles. When it is said to God, it enters covenant conversation, and even the one who accuses is still saying “You.” The relation survives inside the complaint.

Lament also resists cheap consolation, refusing to call evil good or rush past loss. By telling the truth before God, it waits for a redemption worthy of the wound, and Christian hope can afford that honesty because resurrection promises more than emotional adjustment.

I have watched grieving people apologize to God for crying during prayer. The Psalms make no such apology. Tears can tell the truth before God when sentences fail, and they neither earn an answer nor disqualify the one who asks.

Why are you cast down, O my soul,
and why are you disquieted within me?
Hope in God; for I shall again praise him,
my help and my God. (Psalm 42:5‑6)

The same person is cast down and hoping in the same moment. The psalm turns toward God before the feelings improve, the sadness coming along with the turning, and this is how the Psalter teaches us to speak disappointment in God’s presence.

Disappointment becomes dangerous when it hardens into a verdict about God’s character. There is a difference between saying “He has not given me this” and saying “He does not care.” The first can live inside prayer. The second has already decided what the silence means.

I have heard people move toward that border, and I have crossed it myself. The movement is usually quiet. Pain begins interpreting the silence and then treats its interpretation as revelation. Honest lament can prevent this hardening because it keeps the question addressed to God.

Lamentations gives another response. The poet writes amid the rubble of a destroyed city, and the hunger, the mockery, the grief, the smoke are all still in his lines. Yet he does not let the ruins become the sole account of God. In the middle of devastation he deliberately recalls what he knows:

But this I call to mind,
and therefore I have hope:
The steadfast love of the Lord never ceases,
his mercies never come to an end;
they are new every morning;
great is thy faithfulness. (Lamentations 3:21‑23)

“But this I call to mind.” Hope here is an act of memory. The poet admits that the city has fallen, then sets the ruins beside God’s covenant mercy and refuses to let visible destruction erase revealed faithfulness.

Christian memory works this way. We remember creation and covenant, the Cross and the Resurrection, and we remember particular mercies received in our own lives. Memory cannot force the present pain to make sense, but it does keep the present moment from claiming to be the whole story.

Remembering is different from nostalgia, which escapes into a past we can no longer inhabit. Christian remembering brings God’s past faithfulness into the present as a promise about who He is: He acted then, and He is the One before whom we stand now.

This memory is nourished by Scripture, because private recollection is fragile and grief can make earlier mercies feel unreal. Israel recited what God had done, over and over, so that suffering generations could receive a history larger than their own experience. We need the same gift.

At the hospital my personal world felt reduced to one terrible afternoon, and the biblical story placed that afternoon within creation and resurrection. The loss was not made any smaller, only kept from becoming the horizon of reality.

God’s mercies are “new every morning.” The verse does not promise that every morning will feel merciful. It confesses that His compassion is already at work before we perceive its form, and hope receives another day from Him without denying what yesterday destroyed.

✦ ✦ ✦

YEARS AGO I MET a woman from a country far from mine. After college she worked in a city more than a thousand miles from home. The fast train took eleven to fifteen hours; the slow one took nearly twice as long. Visits were precious. She loved her father and usually asked him to meet her at the station.

One year she planned to surprise the family. She told no one she was coming and expected simply to walk to the house and announce her arrival. When she stepped from the early train, her father stood on the platform.

“Who told you I was coming home?”

“No one told me. I come here every morning in case you decide to come home.”

Jesus placed such a father within His parable of the lost son:

And he arose and came to his father. But while he was yet at a distance, his father saw him and had compassion, and ran and embraced him and kissed him. (Luke 15:20)

The father saw his son while he was “yet at a distance.” He had been watching the road. The son approached with a rehearsed speech: “Treat me as one of your hired servants” (Luke 15:19). Failure had taught him to expect a reduced place in the household. He misjudged his father’s heart.

The father ran, embraced him, and interrupted the attempt to bargain for servant status. Restoration began in the father’s compassion before the son had even finished speaking. Repentance truly brought the son home. Yet the welcome arose from love already waiting on the road.

The parable preserves both grace and repentance. The son rises, leaves the far country, and confesses his sin, while the father’s compassion precedes the completed speech and exceeds every proposed term. Grace does not deny the truth of the failure, but it restores the sinner to a relation he could never have rebuilt as wages.

Disappointed believers often approach God with a similar servant speech. We promise to expect less and ask for less if He will only let us stay nearby. The Father refuses this diminished bargain. In Christ He gives the Spirit of adoption and teaches wounded people to address Him again as Father.

This return may take time. The son in the parable walks a long road before the embrace. Grace is active in the hunger that wakes him, in the memory of home, in the decision to rise, in the father’s running. No step of it happens outside mercy.

I read this beside my own prayer that Jesus stay outside the door. Disappointment had told me the withdrawal would become mutual, but the parable reveals a God whose initiative precedes our return, a Father watching for the first movement home even while we rehearse our diminished expectations.

The station story gives the image human scale. The father came each morning because love made room for an arrival no message had announced. Divine faithfulness is greater still. Grace itself awakens the desire to rise and go to Him before any message of return could be sent.

The answer to wounded expectation is a direction rather than a technique. Bring the disappointment itself to God. Tell Him that hope frightens you and that asking again hurts, and name the desire without trimming it into something safer. The verdict you are tempted to write about Him is one more thing to hand over.

Prayer survives disappointment by carrying it into communion. The pain does not disappear, and hope becomes larger than prediction, but we remain before God without requiring the present outcome to explain the entire relationship. Over time His revealed faithfulness can become firmer ground than our reading of events.

Christian hope is participation in Christ’s risen life. It reaches beyond optimism, which only estimates what circumstances are likely to produce. Hope rests in a future that God has promised and has already begun in the Resurrection, so it can live alongside probabilities that offer little encouragement.

This hope still has a human voice. Sometimes it sings. Sometimes it says only, “Help my unbelief.” Its strength lies in Christ, not in the force of our confidence. He holds the person whose grasp has weakened.

Disappointment becomes part of communion when we allow Christ to receive it, and the unanswered desire is known, lamented, held before the Father. We may leave prayer still hurting, but we do not leave alone.

This chapter has mostly described wounded expectation that can still imagine an answer ahead. Some prayers receive a clear No that lasts for decades; I have carried such a prayer through most of my adult life. The next chapter will face the thorn that remains after pleading, tears, and time.
