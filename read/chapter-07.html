Chapter Seven

How Prayer Survives Disappointment

✦ ✦ ✦

≈ 12 min read

A MAN WHO HAD been like a father to me approached death, and I discovered the limits of my composure. He had shaped my character and life. When the doctor told the family that everything possible had been done, a pillar within me seemed to shake. I had prayed as a son prays, and the answer was no.

Years of preaching resurrection had not trained my heart to lose this man. Doctrine remained true, but truth did not remove grief. What I knew and what I could bear felt like two different countries. I begin here because I have lived inside disappointment rather than studying it from a safe distance.

Christian hope does not ask us to treat death as harmless. Death is an enemy Christ has defeated and will finally abolish. Until resurrection is fully revealed, love still experiences separation as a wound. Tears can therefore coexist with firm belief in the life to come.

This distinction matters pastorally. A grieving believer may know every true sentence and still feel unable to stand beneath its weight. He does not need a sharper explanation. He may need another person to bear the truth beside him until his heart can receive it again.

My disappointment was also filial. I had asked God for the life of someone who had given so much shape to mine. The refusal touched prayer, memory, gratitude, and fear of a future without him. Such grief cannot be reduced to one theological problem. It must be brought whole into communion.

Disappointment usually appears as fatigue rather than rebellion. A man once wrote that he had formerly prayed with confidence and joy. Now he felt numb, hurt, and unable to pray as before. He asked whether he had lost his faith. I have heard the same question in many forms from people who would never put it in a letter.

The heart learns self‑protection quietly. A request goes unanswered, then another. Hope becomes expensive, and asking feels dangerous. The person continues worshiping, serving, and praying for others. One desire disappears from conversation with God. This is often a flinch rather than a settled rejection of faith.

I told him that exhaustion did not prove the death of faith. Elijah once lay beneath a broom tree and asked to die shortly after a great answer to prayer. God first gave him sleep and food, twice, before speaking about the future. Grace attended to his exhausted body before assigning another task.

Isaiah says of the Servant, “A bruised reed He will not break, And smoking flax He will not quench” (Isaiah 42:3). Smoking flax is a good image for wounded faith. The flame may be difficult to see, but smoke shows that an ember remains. Faith does not always appear as fire.

The man’s letter also turned toward God even while describing numbness. Someone entirely finished with the relationship would not ask whether its silence meant the end. His question carried the wounded desire to believe. Sometimes the complaint about prayer is already a form of prayer.

Faith has seasons because human beings have bodies, histories, and limits. Confidence may weaken after illness, bereavement, or repeated strain. The change does not automatically reveal a moral or spiritual failure. Elijah needed sleep and food because grace addresses the whole creature.

The Church’s faith can also carry a person whose private strength has failed. We confess, sing, and pray with others partly because no individual remains equally strong in every season. When I cannot say the words with conviction, I may allow the community to say them around me until I can join again.

This borrowed strength is not hypocrisy. It is communion. The body of Christ allows one member’s hope to support another’s wound. The tired believer does not have to manufacture fire; he needs only enough consent to remain near the hearth.

I learned this at a door of my own. During a dark period, several prayers seemed unanswered. I was tired of hoping and even more tired of speaking publicly about hope. In that exhaustion I prayed words I still remember:

Jesus, I want You out of my life. Leave my heart and stay beyond that door.

No lightning followed, and neither did relief. Scripture contains a similar request. After Jesus healed the man among the tombs, the townspeople “began to plead with Him to depart from their region” (Mark 5:17). Many of us ask Him to leave through indifference while avoiding the words. I had spoken them directly.

I could not stop looking toward the imagined door. In prayer, I pictured Jesus waiting beyond it without forcing the handle. Later I recognized the contradiction: I had told Jesus to leave by speaking to Jesus. Even rejection remained conversation. My anger had not ended the relationship because Christ’s patience was greater than my demand.

Disappointment becomes spiritually dangerous when expectation turns into entitlement. Expectation names desire before God. Entitlement decides in advance what the outcome must prove. Both may make the same request. They differ in the judgment made when the request is refused.

The Gospel never heals disappointment by teaching us to want less. Jesus teaches us to ask for daily bread. Desire may remain intense enough to wake us at night. Faith releases the claim to make one outcome the judge of the entire relationship.

Prayer remains vulnerable because love is vulnerable. Refusing to ask can prevent one kind of disappointment, but it also closes the heart against receiving. Some believers call this lowered expectation maturity. Often it is a wound protecting itself. The protected heart becomes like a shuttered house: less can enter, including light.

Entitlement seeks certainty by controlling the meaning of the answer. Expectation accepts the risk of relation. It can rejoice when the gift comes, grieve when it does not, and return to God without pretending that either response is emotionally easy.

This does not require indifference to outcomes. The sick person wants healing, the parent wants the child home, and the grieving family wants death reversed. Christian surrender keeps these desires alive while refusing to turn them into claims of ownership over providence.

Grace gradually purifies expectation. We may begin by asking God to confirm our plan. Through prayer, desire becomes more truthful, less possessive, and more open to goods we could not foresee. The original longing may remain. Its relation to God changes.

Chapter 9 will face the relation between suffering and the Father’s love at the Cross. Here the pastoral task is smaller and immediate. A painful answer does not authorize us to turn grief into a final verdict about God. We may lament what happened while leaving judgment of the whole relationship open to His revealed character.

Mark shows Christ receiving hope that arrives wounded. A father brought a suffering son after the disciples had failed to heal him. The scribes argued while the crowd watched. Years of pain shaped the father’s request: “If You can do anything, have compassion on us and help us” (Mark 9:22). Jesus answered, “If you can believe, all things are possible to him who believes” (Mark 9:23). The father replied:

Immediately the father of the child cried out and said with tears, “Lord, I believe; help my unbelief!” (Mark 9:24)

Jesus received belief and unbelief in the same prayer. He did not require the father to return with psychological certainty. The man’s honesty became the place of encounter. Faith here means remaining oriented toward Christ while admitting everything that weakens that orientation. A trembling prayer remains prayer.

The Psalms place lament, protest, and confusion inside faith. “How long, O LORD?” is prayer (Psalm 13:1). “Why do You hide Your face from me?” is prayer (Psalm 88:14). Lament protests because it still expects God’s promise to be true. Scripture gives sorrow language and teaches the worshiping community to speak it together.

Lament has a structure even when emotion feels chaotic. It addresses God, describes suffering, remembers His character, asks for help, and often reaches toward praise. Every lament does not move neatly through each part. Psalm 88 ends in darkness. Its presence in Scripture tells us that unresolved prayer still belongs to the worship of God’s people.

The address is crucial. Pain spoken only to the self can become a closed echo. Pain spoken to God enters covenant conversation. The person may accuse, question, or protest, yet the grammar still says “You.” Relation survives inside the complaint.

Lament also resists cheap consolation. It will not call evil good or rush past loss. By telling the truth before God, it waits for redemption worthy of the wound. Christian hope can afford such honesty because resurrection promises more than emotional adjustment.

I have watched grieving people apologize to God for crying during prayer. The Psalms make no such apology. Tears can tell the truth before God when sentences fail. They neither earn an answer nor disqualify the person who asks.

Why are you cast down, O my soul?
And why are you disquieted within me?
Hope in God, for I shall yet praise Him
For the help of His countenance. (Psalm 42:5)

The same person is cast down and hoping in the same moment. The psalm turns toward God before feelings improve. Sadness comes with the turning. The Psalter teaches us to speak disappointment in God’s presence.

Disappointment becomes dangerous when it hardens into a verdict about God’s character. “He has not given me this” names an experience. “He does not care” claims to explain God. The first can remain inside prayer. The second attempts to close the case.

I have heard people move toward that border, and I have crossed it myself. The movement is usually quiet. Pain begins interpreting silence, then treats its interpretation as revelation. Honest lament can prevent this hardening because it keeps the question addressed to God.

Lamentations gives another response. The poet writes amid the rubble of a destroyed city. Hunger, mockery, grief, and smoke remain in his lines. He does not let the ruins become the sole account of God. In the middle of devastation, he deliberately recalls what he knows:

This I recall to my mind,
Therefore I have hope.
Through the LORD’s mercies we are not consumed,
Because His compassions fail not.
They are new every morning;
Great is Your faithfulness. (Lamentations 3:21‑23)

“This I recall to my mind.” Hope here is an act of memory. The poet does not pretend the city still stands. He places the ruins beside God’s covenant mercy and refuses to let visible destruction erase revealed faithfulness.

Christian memory works this way. We remember creation, covenant, the Cross, and the Resurrection. We also remember particular mercies received in our own lives. Memory cannot force the present pain to make sense. It prevents the present moment from claiming to be the whole story.

Remembering is different from nostalgia. Nostalgia escapes into a past we can no longer inhabit. Theological memory brings God’s past faithfulness into the present as a promise about His identity. God acted then and remains the One before whom we stand.

This memory is nourished by Scripture because private recollection is fragile. Grief can make earlier mercies feel unreal. Israel repeatedly recited what God had done so that suffering generations could receive a history larger than their own experience. We need the same gift.

At the hospital, my personal world felt reduced to one terrible afternoon. The biblical story placed that afternoon within creation and resurrection. It did not make the loss smaller. It prevented loss from becoming the horizon of reality.

God’s mercies are “new every morning.” The statement does not promise that every morning will feel merciful. It confesses that God’s compassion remains active before we perceive its form. Hope receives another day from Him without denying what yesterday destroyed.

YEARS AGO I MET a woman from a country far from mine. After college she worked in a city more than a thousand miles from home. The fast train took eleven to fifteen hours; the slow one took nearly twice as long. Visits were precious. She loved her father and usually asked him to meet her at the station.

One year she planned to surprise the family. She told no one she was coming. She expected to walk to the house and announce her arrival. When she stepped from the early train, her father stood on the platform.

“Who told you I was coming home?”

“No one told me. I come here every morning in case you decide to come home.”

Jesus placed such a father within His parable of the lost son:

And he arose and came to his father. But when he was still a great way off, his father saw him and had compassion, and ran and fell on his neck and kissed him. (Luke 15:20)

The father saw his son “a great way off.” He had been watching the road. The son approached with a rehearsed speech: “Make me like one of your hired servants” (Luke 15:19). Failure had taught him to expect a reduced place in the household. He misjudged his father’s heart.

The father ran, embraced him, and interrupted the attempt to bargain for servant status. Restoration began in the father’s compassion before the son finished speaking. Repentance truly brought the son home, yet the welcome arose from love already waiting on the road.

The parable preserves both grace and repentance. The son truly rises, leaves the far country, and confesses his sin. The father’s compassion precedes the completed speech and exceeds every proposed term. Grace does not deny the truth of failure. It restores the sinner to a relation he could never rebuild as wages.

Disappointed believers often approach God with a similar servant speech. We promise to expect less, ask for less, and make fewer demands if He will merely let us remain nearby. The Father refuses this diminished bargain. In Christ He gives the Spirit of adoption and teaches wounded people to address Him again as Father.

This return may take time. The son in the parable walks a long road before the embrace. Grace is active in the hunger that awakens him, the memory of home, the decision to rise, and the father’s running. No step occurs outside mercy.

I read this beside my own prayer that Jesus remain outside the door. Disappointment had told me that withdrawal would become mutual. The parable reveals a God whose initiative precedes our return. Even while we rehearse diminished expectations, the Father watches for the first movement home.

The station story gives the image human scale. The father came each morning because love made room for an arrival no message had announced. Divine faithfulness is greater. God does not need information about our return. Grace awakens the desire to rise and go to Him.

The answer to wounded expectation is a direction rather than a technique. Bring the disappointment itself to God. Say that hope frightens you and that asking again hurts. Name the desire without trimming it into something safer. Then release the verdict you are tempted to write about God.

Prayer survives disappointment by carrying it into communion. The pain does not disappear, and hope becomes larger than prediction. We remain before God without requiring the present outcome to explain the entire relationship. Over time, His revealed faithfulness can become a firmer ground than our reading of events.

Theological hope is participation in Christ’s risen life. It reaches beyond optimism, which estimates what circumstances are likely to produce. Hope rests in a future God has promised and begun in the Resurrection. It can therefore live alongside probabilities that offer little encouragement.

This hope still has a human voice. Sometimes it sings. Sometimes it says only, “Help my unbelief.” Its strength lies in Christ rather than in the force of our confidence. He holds the person whose grasp has weakened.

Disappointment becomes part of communion when we allow Christ to receive it. The unanswered desire remains known, lamented, and held before the Father. We may leave prayer still hurting. We do not leave alone.

This chapter has mostly described wounded expectation that can still imagine an answer ahead. Some prayers receive a clear No that lasts for decades. I have carried such a prayer through most of my adult life. The next chapter will face the thorn that remains after pleading, tears, and time.
