Chapter Fourteen

Desiring God Above His Gifts

✦ ✦ ✦

≈ 11 min read

WAITING CAN PURIFY DESIRE. It can loosen our grip on one requested gift and teach us to receive God Himself as our greatest good. Christian tradition has called this the right ordering of love. We go on loving people, health, justice, work, and daily bread, but we learn to love every created good in God and under God, without asking it to bear the weight that belongs to Him alone.

The distinction matters because a good desire can become an idol. The object need not be sinful; idolatry begins when a finite good becomes necessary to our trust, peace, or willingness to love God. At that point prayer can go on being fervent while the worship underneath it has shifted toward the desired result.

A child may open a present and become absorbed in the gift, and there is no sin in that joy. An old man receiving a small gift from a friend of forty years responds differently. He sees the friendship in it. The object matters, but its deepest meaning is the person who gave it. Mature prayer learns to receive in that second way, delighting in God’s gifts while looking through them toward God.

I learned this by recognizing a distortion in my own prayer. Some years ago I wanted something good very badly, and I prayed about it morning and night. I fasted. If a sermon touched the subject, I treated it as confirmation, and when another month passed without an answer, I searched my life for the sin that must be blocking God.

From the outside it looked like devotion, fervent and tearful and full of biblical language. It was also proud. I had begun to think that faith meant wanting something with enough intensity to compel God.

One morning, while I was praying, I saw what had happened: my longing for the outcome had become greater than my longing for God. I still knelt and used the vocabulary of prayer, but I was trying to control Him. The posture of worship had concealed an idol.

This is one of the least confessed dangers of a devout life because it looks so much like spiritual earnestness. Persistence in prayer is biblical; Jesus teaches us not to lose heart. But persistence goes on trusting the Father. What I was doing was trying to master Him. Faith keeps asking, and it leaves God free to be wise and good beyond what we understand.

Three questions now help me examine my desire. Can I pray gladly about anything else, or does every prayer return to this single request? When I imagine God refusing it, do I feel honest grief, or the resentment of someone who believes God has failed an obligation? If He withheld this one thing, would I still return tomorrow because I love Him?

These questions are not meant to condemn sorrow. Jesus wept, lamented, and prayed in anguish, and a heart can love God truly while aching for a different outcome. The issue is whether the desired gift has become the condition on which we permit God to be God.

Waiting has often exposed that condition in me. I do not claim that every delay has the same purpose, or that I can read all of God’s hidden reasons. I can say what some delays have done in me. They have moved a desired good away from the center and restored God to His rightful place.

When the answer later came, if it came, I received it differently. I was still glad, but the gift no longer carried my whole hope. Waiting had taught me that God’s presence had been the blessing from the beginning, not a consolation prize handed out when the real blessing failed to arrive.

✦ ✦ ✦

PSALM 73 TRACES THIS reordering of desire. Asaph sees the arrogant prosper, healthy and secure, free from trouble, and envy brings him near spiritual collapse: “My feet had almost stumbled, my steps had well nigh slipped” (Psalm 73:2). He wants the life of those who disregard God.

The crisis changes when he enters the sanctuary and sees both their end and his own confusion. His circumstances have not yet improved, but his vision has. The psalm reaches its center in a confession that joins supreme desire with human frailty:

Whom have I in heaven but thee?
And there is nothing upon earth that I desire besides thee.
My flesh and my heart may fail,
but God is the strength of my heart and my portion for ever. (Psalm 73:25‑26)

The two verses belong together. Asaph desires God above everything in heaven and earth, yet his flesh and heart still fail. Supreme love for God does not turn him into an untouched saint; he is still weak, and his confidence rests in God, who holds him when his own strength gives way.

The word portion carries Israel’s history. When the land was divided, the priestly tribe did not receive a territory like the other tribes, because the Lord Himself was their inheritance. Asaph was a Levite, and his vocation embodied the very truth he had to rediscover, that God was his portion.

Calling God our portion does not mean despising the rest of His creation. It means admitting that no created thing can be our final possession, since no created good can be held forever apart from Him. God alone can be loved as the portion that cannot be lost, and every other love is kept forever by being loved in Him.

✦ ✦ ✦

MANY YEARS AGO, WHEN I asked the Lord to use me in His Kingdom, I made two specific requests: never to use the church’s funds, even to support my own ministry, and never to become a celebrity.

I prayed that way because the church belongs to Jesus, and people must follow Him rather than the man who speaks about Him. I had seen how an audience and an income could slowly change a servant’s sense of ownership, how a person could begin by pointing to Christ and gradually make his own influence the measure of faithfulness. I feared that temptation and asked God to keep me poor in the currency of attention.

Decades later, I can say that this prayer was answered as asked. I have taught, I have preached, and I have served, all without accepting payment, and I have not gathered a following or become a public name. By ordinary measures of visibility my service is small. I regard that hiddenness as one of the great answers of my life.

This is not a universal rule for ministry. Scripture teaches that those who preach the gospel may receive their living from the gospel, and public influence can be a stewardship given by God. Being hidden proves nothing about a man’s holiness, and being well known proves nothing against it. My prayer concerned the dangers of my own heart and the form of service I believed I was called to offer.

Still, our age finds such a request strange. Christians often pray for measurable reach, influence, and growth, some from sincere love and a desire to serve more people. The danger begins when scale becomes our evidence that God is present. The Kingdom cannot be measured by visibility alone. A cup of cold water given in Christ’s name may be remembered in heaven after celebrated ministries have disappeared from human memory.

Hiddenness has cost me something. I have watched others receive recognition for work no better than work I had done without notice, and I have felt envy. At those moments, I remember what I asked and why. The desire for applause has weakened over time, though it has never become a temptation I can safely declare dead.

Renouncing money and attention did not settle every question of desire. I still carried a dream about the future shape of my service, whose details I will keep private, and I pursued it for years through discouragement and closed doors. Persistence seemed like faith, but obsession had become mixed with obedience.

Even spiritual work can become an idol. Study, silence, prayer, ministry, and vocation are gifts that carry us toward God. None of them is God. We discover the difference when one of these supports is removed, for faith rooted in God may grieve the loss and still stand on its foundation.

Then, during a period of about ten days, I learned things that brought my long‑held dream to an end. I did not let it go serenely; those were among the harder days of my adult life, and I spent long hours with trusted men, trying to hear truth beneath my disappointment.

During that season, I asked those around me to stop using certain titles for me. “Call me a child of God,” I told them. It was the one identity I trusted would still be true ten thousand years from now.

When the immediate pain settled, I took stock. The dream was gone, but what I valued most was untouched: Christ was still my Lord and Savior, and I could still bring people toward His Kingdom, as I had done before anyone gave me a title. The loss changed the form I had imagined without removing the calling to love God and serve those He placed near me.

I wrote to friends, “I have not lost anything, because I possess the greatest gift of all, our Lord Jesus Christ.” The disappointment was real. I meant that it had not touched my final good, and that distinction let me grieve and still be glad in the same months.

A simple calculation helped. A century from now someone else will live where I live, and that person will probably never have heard my name; everyone who knows my face now will be gone. My unrealized ambitions will carry no weight in the Kingdom of God. What will matter is whether I loved God and the people He placed within my reach, and the failed dream could not prevent either. Remembering our mortality makes it harder to baptize every ambition as a calling.

✦ ✦ ✦

HABAKKUK FACED A FAR greater loss. He looked toward invasion and ruin, then named the collapse of an entire agricultural economy:

Though the fig tree do not blossom,
nor fruit be on the vines,
the produce of the olive fail
and the fields yield no food,
the flock be cut off from the fold
and there be no herd in the stalls,
yet I will rejoice in the Lord,
I will joy in the God of my salvation. (Habakkuk 3:17‑18)

Figs, grapes, olives, grain, sheep, and cattle are all gone. Habakkuk does not minimize the loss or call material deprivation unimportant; he names it precisely, and then he rejoices in God his Savior. He can rejoice in the middle of ruin because his joy rests in God, and the ruin cannot take God from him.

The prophet sent these words to the Chief Musician to be played on stringed instruments. Israel was to sing its worst case before God. Biblical joy can do this because it is deeper than optimism. It confesses that God is still worthy and that His saving purpose will stand, whether or not the fig tree blossoms next season.

This is the soil of thanksgiving. Paul writes, “Have no anxiety about anything, but in everything by prayer and supplication with thanksgiving let your requests be made known to God” (Philippians 4:6). Thanksgiving does not suppress the request. It places it inside the remembered faithfulness of God.

Gratitude recalls the gifts we have received and keeps present need from becoming the whole story. Without thanksgiving, prayer turns into a list of everything we do not have. Thanksgiving lets us ask as people who have already received grace, so the new desire, real as it is, no longer defines all reality.

Paul wrote those words while under guard, and in the same letter he thanked the Philippians for material support and explained what Christ had taught him:

Not that I complain of want; for I have learned, in whatever state I am, to be content. I know how to be abased, and I know how to abound; in any and all circumstances I have learned the secret of facing plenty and hunger, abundance and want. I can do all things in him who strengthens me. (Philippians 4:11‑13)

“I can do all things through Christ who strengthens me” is often quoted, in its familiar older wording, as a promise of success, but in context Paul is speaking about abundance and deprivation. Christ gives him strength to stay faithful when he is full and when he is hungry. The promise concerns contentment on both sides of an answered prayer.

Paul says twice that he learned this. Contentment did not arrive as temperament or instant revelation. Abasement and abundance became his teachers because Christ was faithful in both, so plenty never ruled him, and need never told him who he was.

The goal of prayer has always been communion with God, and by grace that communion is already the beginning of eternal life. Gifts, delays, and refusals can all be taken into His work of drawing us more deeply into fellowship with Himself.

None of this destroys desire. Grace heals desire and enlarges it, and we become more capable of loving created goods because we no longer demand that they save us. We can ask boldly, take what comes with gratitude, grieve without pretending, and let go when we must. All four become possible when God is our portion.

I want more today than I once did, but my deepest desire has changed. I want God. That desire does not make work, sickness, fatigue, failure, or bereavement disappear. A person who prays still lives under the conditions shared by every human life, and his neighbor who never prays may pass through the same outward events.

Here is the question we cannot avoid. If believers still labor, suffer, age, and bury those they love, what difference has prayer actually made? The question deserves more than a devotional answer, and it takes us to the final movement of this book.
