Chapter Fourteen

Desiring God Above His Gifts

✦ ✦ ✦

≈ 10 min read

WAITING CAN PURIFY DESIRE. It can loosen our grip on one requested gift and teach us to receive God Himself as our greatest good. Christian tradition has called this the right ordering of love, the ordo amoris. We do not stop loving people, health, justice, meaningful work, or daily bread. We learn to love every created good in God and under God, without asking it to bear the weight that belongs to Him alone.

This distinction matters because a good desire can become an idol. The object need not be sinful. Idolatry begins when a finite good becomes necessary to our trust, peace, or willingness to love God. At that point, prayer may remain fervent while worship has shifted toward the desired result.

A child may open a present and become absorbed in the gift. There is no sin in that joy. An old man receiving a small gift from a friend of forty years may respond differently. He sees the friendship in the gift. The object matters, but its deepest meaning is the person who gave it. Mature prayer learns to receive in that second way. It delights in God’s gifts while looking through them toward God.

I learned this by recognizing a distortion in my own prayer. Some years ago, I wanted something good very badly. I prayed about it morning and night. I fasted. If a sermon touched the subject, I treated it as confirmation. When another month passed without an answer, I searched my life for the sin that must be blocking God.

From the outside, it looked like devotion. It was fervent, tearful, and filled with biblical language. It was also proud. I had begun to think that faith meant wanting something with enough intensity to compel God.

One morning, while I was praying, I saw what had happened. My longing for the outcome had become greater than my longing for God. I still knelt and used the vocabulary of prayer, but I was trying to control Him. The posture of worship had concealed an idol.

This is one of the least confessed dangers of a devout life because it resembles spiritual earnestness. Persistence in prayer is biblical. Jesus teaches us not to lose heart. Yet persistence trusts the Father; compulsion tries to master Him. Faith continues to ask while remaining willing for God to be wise, free, and good beyond our understanding.

Three questions now help me examine my desire. Can I pray gladly about anything else, or does every prayer return to this single request? When I imagine God refusing it, do I feel honest grief, or the resentment of someone who believes God has failed an obligation? If He withheld this one thing, would I still return tomorrow because I love Him?

These questions do not condemn sorrow. Jesus wept, lamented, and prayed in anguish. A heart can love God truly while aching for a different outcome. The issue is whether the desired gift has become the condition on which we permit God to be God.

Waiting has often exposed that condition in me. I do not claim that every delay has the same purpose or that I can read all of God’s hidden reasons. I can say what some delays have done within me. They have moved a desired good away from the center and restored God to His rightful place.

When the answer later came, if it came, I received it differently. I was still glad, but the gift no longer carried my whole hope. Waiting had taught me that God’s presence was not the consolation prize offered when the real blessing failed to arrive. He was the blessing from the beginning.

Psalm 73 traces this reordering of desire. Asaph sees the arrogant prosper. They appear healthy, secure, and free from trouble. Envy brings him near spiritual collapse: “My feet had almost stumbled; my steps had nearly slipped” (Psalm 73:2 NKJV). He wants the life of those who disregard God.

The crisis changes when he enters the sanctuary. There he sees both their end and his own confusion. His circumstances have not yet improved. His vision has. The psalm reaches its center in a confession that joins supreme desire with human frailty:

Whom have I in heaven but You?
And there is none upon earth that I desire besides You.
My flesh and my heart fail;
But God is the strength of my heart and my portion forever. (Psalm 73:25‑26)

The two verses belong together. Asaph desires God above everything in heaven and earth, yet his flesh and heart still fail. Supreme love for God does not turn him into an untouched saint. He remains weak. His confidence rests in God, who holds him when his own strength gives way.

The word portion carries Israel’s history. When the land was divided, the priestly tribe did not receive a territory like the other tribes. The Lord Himself was their inheritance. Asaph was a Levite. His vocation embodied the truth he had to rediscover: God was his portion.

To call God our portion is not to despise the rest of His creation. It is to confess that no created thing can be our final possession. Every other gift can be lost, changed, or taken by death. God alone can be loved without fear that eternity will make the love meaningless.

✦ ✦ ✦

MANY YEARS AGO, WHEN I asked the Lord to use me in His Kingdom, I made two specific requests. I asked never to use the Church’s money, even to support my own ministry, and never to become a celebrity.

I prayed that way because the Church belongs to Jesus. People must follow Him, not the person who speaks about Him. I had seen how an audience and an income could slowly change a servant’s sense of ownership. A person could begin by pointing to Christ and gradually make his own influence the measure of faithfulness. I feared that temptation and asked God to keep me poor in the currency of attention.

Decades later, I can say that this prayer was answered as asked. I have taught, preached, and served without accepting payment. When someone has offered me an envelope, I have returned it with thanks. I have not gathered a following or become a public name. By ordinary measures of visibility, my service has remained small. I regard that hiddenness as one of the great answers of my life.

This is not a universal rule for ministry. Scripture teaches that those who preach the gospel may receive their living from the gospel. Public influence can also be a stewardship given by God. Hiddenness is no proof of holiness, just as prominence is no proof of compromise. My prayer concerned the dangers of my own heart and the form of service I believed I was called to offer.

Still, our age finds such a request strange. Christians often pray for measurable reach, influence, and growth. Some do so from sincere love and a desire to serve more people. The danger begins when scale becomes our evidence that God is present. The Kingdom cannot be measured by visibility alone. A cup of cold water given in Christ’s name may be remembered in heaven after celebrated ministries have disappeared from human memory.

Hiddenness has cost me something. I have watched others receive recognition for work no better than work I had done without notice, and I have felt envy. At those moments, I remember what I asked and why. The desire for applause has weakened over time, though it has never become a temptation I can safely declare dead.

Renouncing money and attention did not settle every question of desire. I still carried a dream about the future shape of my service. I will keep its details private. I pursued it for years through discouragement and closed doors. Persistence seemed like faith, but obsession had become mixed with obedience.

Even spiritual work can become an idol. Study, silence, prayer, ministry, and vocation are gifts that carry us toward God. None of them is God. We discover the difference when one of these supports is removed. Faith rooted in God may grieve the loss, but its foundation remains.

Then, during a period of about ten days, I learned things that brought my long‑held dream to an end. I did not relinquish it serenely. Those days were among the harder days of my adult life. I spent long hours with trusted men, trying to hear truth beneath my disappointment.

During that season, I asked those around me to stop using certain titles for me. “Call me a child of God,” I told them. It was the one identity I knew would remain true ten thousand years from now.

When the immediate pain settled, I took stock. The dream was gone, but what I valued most remained. Christ was still my Lord and Savior. I could still bring people toward His Kingdom, as I had done before anyone gave me a title. The loss changed the form I had imagined. It did not remove the calling to love God and serve those He placed near me.

I wrote to friends, “I have not lost anything, because I possess the greatest gift of all, our Lord Jesus Christ.” I did not mean that the disappointment was unreal. I meant that it had not touched my final good. The distinction allowed grief and joy to inhabit the same season.

A simple calculation helped. A century from now, someone else will live where I live, and that person will probably never have heard my name. Everyone who knows my face now will be gone. My unrealized ambitions will carry no weight in the Kingdom of God. What will matter is whether I loved God and the people He placed within my reach. The failed dream could not prevent either. Remembering our mortality makes it harder to baptize every ambition as a calling.

Habakkuk faced a far greater loss. He looked toward invasion and ruin, then named the collapse of an entire agricultural economy:

Though the fig tree may not blossom,
Nor fruit be on the vines;
Though the labor of the olive may fail,
And the fields yield no food;
Though the flock may be cut off from the fold,
And there be no herd in the stalls—
Yet I will rejoice in the LORD,
I will joy in the God of my salvation. (Habakkuk 3:17‑18)

Figs, grapes, olives, grain, sheep, and cattle are all gone. Habakkuk does not minimize the loss or call material deprivation unimportant. He names it precisely. Then he rejoices in God his Savior. His joy is an act of defiance against devastation because it rests in the One whom devastation cannot remove.

The prophet sent these words to the Chief Musician to be played on stringed instruments. Israel was to sing its worst case before God. Biblical joy can do this because it is deeper than optimism. It does not predict that the fig tree will blossom next season. It confesses that God remains worthy and that His saving purpose will stand.

This is the soil of thanksgiving. Paul writes, “Be anxious for nothing, but in everything by prayer and supplication, with thanksgiving, let your requests be made known to God” (Philippians 4:6). Thanksgiving does not suppress the request. It places the request inside the remembered faithfulness of God.

Gratitude recalls gifts already received and keeps present need from becoming the whole story. Without thanksgiving, prayer can become an inventory of absence. With thanksgiving, we ask as people who have already received grace. The new desire remains real, but it no longer defines all reality.

Paul wrote those words while under guard. In the same letter, he thanked the Philippians for material support and explained what Christ had taught him:

Not that I speak in regard to need, for I have learned in whatever state I am, to be content: I know how to be abased, and I know how to abound. Everywhere and in all things I have learned both to be full and to be hungry, both to abound and to suffer need. I can do all things through Christ who strengthens me. (Philippians 4:11‑13)

“I can do all things through Christ who strengthens me” is often quoted as a promise of success. In context, Paul is speaking about abundance and deprivation. Christ gives him strength to remain faithful when he is full and when he is hungry. The promise concerns contentment on both sides of an answered prayer.

Paul says twice that he learned this. Contentment did not arrive as temperament or instant revelation. Abasement and abundance became his teachers because Christ was faithful in both. Plenty could not become his master, and need could not become his identity.

The goal of prayer has always been communion with God. By grace, that communion is already the beginning of eternal life. Gifts, delays, and refusals can all be taken into God’s work of drawing us more deeply into fellowship with Himself.

This does not destroy desire. Grace heals desire and enlarges it. We become more capable of loving created goods because we no longer demand that they save us. We can ask boldly, receive gratefully, grieve honestly, and relinquish faithfully. All four acts become possible when God is our portion.

I want more today than I once did, but my deepest desire has changed. I want God. That desire does not make work, sickness, fatigue, failure, or bereavement disappear. A person who prays still experiences the conditions shared by every human life. His neighbor who never prays may experience the same outward events.

This raises an unavoidable question. If believers still labor, suffer, age, and bury those they love, what difference has prayer actually made? The question deserves more than a devotional answer. It takes us to the final movement of this book.
