Chapter Three

Unprotected Before God

✦ ✦ ✦

≈ 13 min read

MANY BELIEVERS HAVE STOPPED praying about the heaviest burden in their lives. They may still worship, serve, and believe. One subject has simply disappeared from their prayers.

I have heard the explanation more than once. They prayed hard for a long time and heard nothing. Eventually they concluded that God would do as He intended whether they kept asking or stopped. One man said, “I don’t want to nag Him,” then moved the conversation to safer ground. He called his silence humility.

I did not argue because I have made the same decision. It feels mature. We tell ourselves that we are sparing God and protecting our hearts from another disappointment. The decision rarely announces itself as unbelief.

Yet the person who stops asking becomes the sole interpreter of God’s silence. God did not call the prayer nagging. The silence itself gave no explanation. We supply the meaning, sign God’s name beneath it, and obey what we have written. Then silence can no longer surprise us because we have locked it inside our own conclusion.

This raises a hard question. If prayer does not reliably produce the requested outcome, how does praying differ from leaving the matter alone? Rain falls on the fields of those who pray and those who do not. A tumor may grow or shrink without any visible relation to devotion. When preachers call delay “formation” and silence “invitation,” are we describing reality or protecting faith from disappointment? I have asked this in cold and honest hours.

Scripture answers by showing that prayer is more than an attempt to produce a result. Prayer places desire, without protection, before God. The exposure occurs within a covenant. We bring the heart to God, who has already bound Himself to His people. The desire and the silence now stand within that relationship. Circumstances may remain fixed, yet the person who prays no longer determines their meaning alone.

Covenant changes the meaning of exposure. Standing uncovered before a stranger would be danger. Standing uncovered before God, who has pledged Himself to us, is the beginning of healing. He knows the truth before we speak, yet He invites us to speak so that the truth may become part of our conscious life with Him. What was carried alone becomes shared within communion.

God’s knowledge is personal. He does not observe us as a distant examiner collecting facts. Scripture describes Him as the One who searches the heart, remembers His promise, and calls His people by name. His knowledge carries love and judgment together. He reveals what is false because He intends the restoration of the person He sees. Prayer consents to that truthful gaze.

This is why hiding from God disorders the soul even when the hidden thing is innocent desire. We divide ourselves. One part speaks religious language while another carries anger, grief, or longing in secret. Grace seeks the whole person. God, who created us, does not ask for a polished representative. He asks us to come.

Real asking admits what we prefer to hide. We want something. We cannot secure it ourselves. We have entrusted the outcome to another Person. Each admission weakens our illusion of control. That is why we often work, worry, plan, and complain long before we pray. Activity allows us to remain in command. Prayer places us before God with empty hands.

The psalmist understood all of this long before I did.

Trust in Him at all times, you people;
Pour out your heart before Him;
God is a refuge for us. Selah (Psalm 62:8 NKJV)

David commands us to pour out the heart. A poured vessel releases what it contains. Prayer surrenders custody of what has been hidden. In the psalm, refuge and pouring belong together. We often seek safety by remaining composed and offering God a manageable account. David finds refuge after the heart has spilled into God’s presence.

The Psalms repeatedly give sacred language to experiences we would prefer to censor. They contain accusation, confusion, fear, longing, and praise. The same worshiper may confess God’s faithfulness and ask why God seems absent. Scripture does not resolve the tension by deleting one side. It teaches us to carry the tension into prayer.

Lament is faith refusing isolation. The lamenting person still addresses God. Even protest preserves a relation that despair would abandon. This does not make every angry conclusion true. It places anger where God can answer, correct, and transform it. A complaint spoken to God remains open to God; a complaint sealed within the self soon becomes its own world.

The command to pour out the heart therefore concerns truth before it concerns intensity. Some people weep, and others remain quiet. One may speak for an hour while another can manage only a sentence. Exposure cannot be measured by volume. It occurs when the words offered to God correspond to what the heart actually carries.

We frequently give God an edited prayer. A woman terrified for her son asks only for strength to trust. A man who longs for healing asks only for grace to accept the future. Both requests may be good, but they can also conceal the desire that feels too dangerous to name. The Gospels name bread, healing, and deliverance directly.

I have prayed at hospital bedsides in a steady clergy voice while my heart was saying six desperate words. That voice sounds reverent and keeps me protected. Prayer asks for the true desire at its actual size. God already knows what the edited version leaves out.

One night I finally stopped editing a burden of my own. I will not describe its shape because it belongs to other people. Its weight is enough for this story. I had prayed about it in every respectable register. That night I begged God to take it away. I no longer wanted strength to carry it. I wanted it gone. The same words slowly lost their grammar and became crying. I once felt ashamed of that prayer. I no longer do.

I experienced an answer that night, and I describe it cautiously. One word came to me with unusual force: SOVEREIGNTY. Nothing followed. I understood the word as God’s answer and cried, “Lord, come back! Talk to me more!” Only silence followed. Yet it felt like the silence of Someone present who had finished speaking.

This kind of private experience is rare in my life and adds nothing to Christian revelation. Scripture and the faith entrusted to the Church remain sufficient. A believer who never experiences anything similar lacks nothing. Every impression must be judged by the truth God has publicly given; anything that contradicts it must be rejected. I tell this as personal experience because of the word’s effect upon me.

The burden remained unchanged the next morning. Measured only by outcomes, the prayer had failed. Yet the word placed that burden within the sovereignty of God. My struggle was neither outside His knowledge nor beyond His providence. I had treated it as meaningless bad luck and searched for any exit I could engineer. Slowly, and with considerable complaint, I began to carry it as something God had permitted within a life He still governed.

Providence did not make the burden good in itself. It meant that evil, suffering, and confusion could not make my life unintelligible to God. His sovereignty was the rule of crucified love, not the cold force of fate. He could oppose what was evil, sustain me within what remained, and draw good from what I could not change. The exposure of that night allowed grace to reach a place my composed prayers had kept closed.

Christian providence never requires us to call darkness light. God remains opposed to sin and death. His sovereignty means that neither can finally defeat His purpose. The Cross is the clearest sign: human cruelty remained truly evil, and God made that very place the instrument of redemption. Divine rule appears there as love able to enter suffering, bear its cost, and bring life where evil intended only death.

I could therefore resist what was wrong in the burden and still entrust my life to God. Acceptance did not mean passivity. It meant that my struggle took place within His care. I was free to seek change without treating change as the condition of His faithfulness. This freedom emerged only after the heart had been honestly opened.

MARK SHOWS THIS EXPOSURE in the story of Bartimaeus. Jesus was leaving Jericho with His disciples while a large crowd pressed around Him. A blind beggar sat beside the road.

Now they came to Jericho. As He went out of Jericho with His disciples and a great multitude, blind Bartimaeus, the son of Timaeus, sat by the road begging. And when he heard that it was Jesus of Nazareth, he began to cry out and say, “Jesus, Son of David, have mercy on me!” Then many warned him to be quiet; but he cried out all the more, “Son of David, have mercy on me!” (Mark 10:46‑48)

The crowd told him to be quiet. A respected teacher was passing, and one man was disturbing the procession by shouting from the ditch. Composed people are often embarrassed by uncomposed need. The same crowd speaks within us: He is busy; He already knows; do not make a scene. I have heard each warning in my own prayers, usually dressed as good theology. Bartimaeus had little dignity left to protect. He shouted even louder.

So Jesus stood still and commanded him to be called. Then they called the blind man, saying to him, “Be of good cheer. Rise, He is calling you.” And throwing aside his garment, he rose and came to Jesus. (Mark 10:49‑50)

Mark records that Bartimaeus threw aside his garment. By long custom, a beggar’s cloak could serve as covering at night and a place to sit during the day. At the least, it was one of the few possessions close at hand. Bartimaeus cast it aside while still blind. He had received a summons, not a promise of healing. He left his covering and moved toward the voice of Jesus.

So Jesus answered and said to him, “What do you want Me to do for you?” The blind man said to Him, “Rabboni, that I may receive my sight.” Then Jesus said to him, “Go your way; your faith has made you well.” And immediately he received his sight and followed Jesus on the road. (Mark 10:51‑52)

Jesus asked, “What do you want Me to do for you?” The need seemed obvious, and Jesus already knew it. His question invited Bartimaeus to speak the desire himself. Before the crowd that had silenced him, the man said plainly, “that I may receive my sight.”

Jesus healed him, and Bartimaeus followed on the road. The gift opened a life of discipleship. Mark calls the man’s movement toward Christ faith: he shouted, endured rebuke, left his cloak, crossed the distance in darkness, and named what he wanted.

This story does not give us a sequence for obtaining results. Exposure cannot obligate God. If we turn honesty into a condition for receiving an answer, we have created another technique. We will inspect our motives, measure our vulnerability, and perform surrender in hope of payment. A performance of openness becomes another form of armor.

Jesus forms people capable of trust. He does not train spiritual technicians. God’s freedom and ours remain intact in the encounter. Bartimaeus had an actual cloak to release. Each of us knows the covering we use to avoid being seen. Dropping it earns nothing. It simply allows us to come as we are.

John gives us another person uncovered before Christ. At a well in Samaria, Jesus asked a woman for a drink. During their conversation He said, “Go, call your husband, and come here” (John 4:16). He already knew her history: five husbands, and the man then in her life was not her husband. Jesus touched the most painful fracture in her story because grace intended to enter there.

She received the severe mercy of being fully known and still welcomed. Jesus spoke to this woman about worship in spirit and truth. Such worship begins with standing before God without pretense. His gaze reveals without abandoning. Grace does more than issue pardon from a distance. It restores communion where sin and sorrow have fractured the person.

The woman left her water jar and went into the town. She told others about a Man who had disclosed her life. The knowledge that might have felt like condemnation became good news because the One who knew her offered living water. Christian confession rests on this hope: the truth about us is safest with the One whose truth is joined to mercy.

Being known by Christ also restores the woman’s relation to other people. She entered the town carrying the very history that may once have isolated her, yet she now spoke openly. Christ had not erased her past. His presence had broken its power to define her in secret. Grace returned her to community as a witness.

This is one fruit of truthful prayer. We become less governed by the fear of discovery. God has already seen the hidden room and entered it with mercy. We may still need repentance, repair, wise boundaries, or patient healing. None of those can proceed while pretense controls the door. Communion with God gives us courage to live in truth before others.

The New Testament does eventually gather the invitation into a single sentence.

Let us therefore come boldly to the throne of grace, that we may obtain mercy and find grace to help in time of need. (Hebrews 4:16)

The invitation to approach a throne boldly would have startled ancient readers. A throne displayed authority and made every petitioner conscious of his smallness. Hebrews can speak this way because the throne belongs to Christ, our compassionate High Priest. He has entered human weakness and temptation without sin. Our confidence rests in the One who receives us.

Christ’s priesthood gives prayer its objective foundation. We do not gain access by achieving the right emotional state or revealing ourselves with perfect honesty. The Son has opened the way through His self‑offering. We come unprotected because we are already covered by His mercy. Even a frightened and confused prayer reaches the Father through Him.

The High Priest represents the people before God. Christ does this perfectly because He shares our humanity and remains the beloved Son. He carries human need into the Father’s presence, and He brings the Father’s grace to human need. Prayer does not have to build a bridge between earth and heaven. We pray upon the bridge His incarnation, death, resurrection, and ascension have already established.

This protects us from making honesty another achievement. We will never understand every motive before we pray. Sometimes we discover what we wanted only after hearing ourselves say it. Christ receives the unfinished prayer. His intercession is greater than our self‑knowledge, and the Spirit helps us in the weakness that words cannot master. Exposure means consenting to their work, not completing it beforehand.

Boldness therefore has a quiet form. It may appear as confident speech, tears, or a barely audible plea. Its essence is access: the believer truly comes near because Christ has brought him near. Feelings may lag behind this reality. The throne remains a throne of grace on days when the heart feels only fear.

The verse promises mercy and grace in time of need. It lists no timetable or specified outcome. God pledges His own help to the person who approaches. Mercy meets what is sinful and wounded. Grace supplies what human weakness lacks. The throne of judgment has become, through Christ, the place where need finds welcome.

Prayer does not always soothe us. It can intensify the tension between God’s promise and present reality. It keeps both before the heart. Disappointment remains painful, yet it is denied the right to become the final truth about God or the world.

This is how praying differs from remaining silent even when circumstances do not change. The person who refuses to ask must interpret everything alone. Delay means whatever he decides it means, and the world as it appears becomes closed and final. The person who prays places the same facts inside a relationship. Their meaning now belongs to God as well.

When people tell me they stopped asking because they did not want to nag God, I sometimes ask whether they have ever told Him the whole truth. Have they named the anger, the fear, and the sentences they consider unfit for prayer? The silence after that question is often long. More than one person has finally said, “No. I wouldn’t know how to start.”

The beginning is unimpressive. Say it badly to God. Resist tidying the words as they emerge. Their stories remain unfinished, so I will not invent satisfying conclusions. Yet the admission “I don’t know how to start” is itself a turn toward God. The locked door has begun to open.

Once we consent to pray unguardedly, another fear appears: what if I ask for the wrong thing? We do not know God’s will in every matter. It may seem safer to name nothing specific. That fear can return the cloak to our shoulders and lead us back to the roadside. It deserves a careful answer because it raises one of prayer’s most practical questions: what should we ask when we do not know what God wants?
