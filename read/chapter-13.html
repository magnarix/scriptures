Part IV · The Giver

Chapter Thirteen

Every Answer Reveals Who He Is

✦ ✦ ✦

≈ 12 min read

WHEN GOD ANSWERS PRAYER, He does more than alter a circumstance. He makes His character known. The need may belong to one season. What we learn of Him can remain for the rest of our lives.

This does not mean that private experience adds to God’s revelation. Jesus Christ is the full and decisive revelation of the Father. Providence does not give us a different God from the One revealed in Christ. It teaches us to recognize that God within the particulars of our own lives. An answer to prayer becomes personal instruction in truths the gospel has already made known.

People often reveal this without realizing it. They tell me how the work came through, the child returned, or the medical report changed. Near the end of the story, their attention shifts from the answer to the One who gave it. Then comes a sentence such as, “I never knew He was like that.”

Sometimes the request was granted exactly as asked. Sometimes the person needed years to understand the answer. Either way, the lasting discovery concerns God. The circumstance settles into ordinary life. The knowledge of the Giver remains.

Years ago, I was unemployed for months and prayed every day for work. Near the end of that period, I had a phone interview for a job I badly wanted. I finished the call convinced that I had failed. I called the recruiter and said, “If I don’t get this job, please don’t call me. I am on an emotional roller coaster.” He kindly agreed.

A week later, on a Thursday afternoon, his number appeared on my phone. I answered, though my first thought was that he had broken his promise. He was calling with an offer better than I had dared to expect.

I told him how poorly I thought the interview had gone. He replied, “Yes, the manager actually mentioned that you seemed quite nervous during the interview.” They had heard the nervousness and hired me anyway.

The job mattered, but the deeper gift was the correction of my view of God. I had been treating Him as another interviewer, imagining that heaven grades our performance and gives only to those who appear composed. The offer exposed that false picture. Grace had met me in weakness. I also came away convinced that God has a sense of humor.

John 11 gives us a greater example. Lazarus was dying, so Martha and Mary sent word to Jesus: “Lord, behold, he whom You love is sick” (John 11:3). Their prayer contained no instructions. They placed the sickness beside the love of Christ and entrusted the rest to Him. At hospital beds, I have often borrowed their words: Lord, the one You love is in trouble.

Jesus loved Martha, Mary, and Lazarus. John states that fact immediately before telling us, “He stayed two more days in the place where He was” (John 11:6). The placement is deliberate. Love explains the delay, though the delay did not look loving to those who endured it. Jesus had already said, “This sickness is not unto death, but for the glory of God, that the Son of God may be glorified through it” (John 11:4). His purpose included both rescue and revelation.

By the time Jesus approached Bethany, Lazarus had been in the tomb for four days. Martha would later say, “Lord, by this time there is a stench, for he has been dead four days” (John 11:39). She went out to meet Jesus and said, “Lord, if You had been here, my brother would not have died” (John 11:21). Her words joined faith to grief. She believed Jesus could have healed her brother, but she could not understand why He had failed to arrive.

Martha’s theology was true but incomplete. She knew Christ as healer. Hidden within that knowledge, however, was a timetable. A healer must come before death. Jesus had missed the deadline, so the possibility appeared to be gone. Still, her faith reached beyond her understanding: “But even now I know that whatever You ask of God, God will give You” (John 11:22).

Jesus said, “Your brother will rise again” (John 11:23). Martha answered with sound doctrine: “I know that he will rise again in the resurrection at the last day” (John 11:24). She believed in the final resurrection, but that truth seemed far away from the brother whose body lay in a cave. Jesus then drew the doctrine into His own Person.

Jesus said to her, “I am the resurrection and the life. He who believes in Me, though he may die, he shall live. And whoever lives and believes in Me shall never die. Do you believe this?” She said to Him, “Yes, Lord, I believe that You are the Christ, the Son of God, who is to come into the world.” (John 11:25‑27)

Martha had asked for healing. Jesus gave her a fuller knowledge of Himself. He did not deny what she already knew. He enlarged it. The One who heals is also the Resurrection and the Life. Resurrection is not merely an event under Christ’s control. It is bound to His identity. Life belongs to Him and comes through Him.

The delay made that revelation possible. If Jesus had arrived when Martha expected, she would have known again that He could heal. Because He came after every human possibility had ended, she learned that death itself stood under His authority.

We should be careful here. We cannot interpret every delay as a promise that God will eventually give us the outcome we wanted. John tells us what Jesus intended at Bethany because Scripture reveals it. We do not possess that kind of access to every hidden purpose of providence. Yet delays can expose the false gods we carry within our true beliefs. We may confess the living God while expecting Him to function as a dispenser: request entered, faith measured, blessing released. When He refuses that role, disappointment uncovers the bargain we had mistaken for trust.

The imaginary god can be managed, but he cannot be met because he does not exist. The living God is free, wise, and personal. He gives Himself even when He does not submit to our script. At Bethany, the broken timetable opened Martha’s eyes to the Lord of life.

Moses learned that divine glory is also self‑disclosure. He had seen plagues cease, the sea open, bread cover the ground, and water come from rock. Then he prayed, “Please, show me Your glory” (Exodus 33:18). God hid him in the cleft of the rock and passed before him.

And the LORD passed before him and proclaimed, “The LORD, the LORD God, merciful and gracious, longsuffering, and abounding in goodness and truth.” (Exodus 34:6)

Moses asked to see and heard God name Himself. Divine glory was revealed as character: mercy, grace, patience, goodness, and truth. This corrects an imagination that associates glory only with brightness and power. God’s power is glorious because it is the power of this God. His holiness is never detached from His goodness, and His mercy never compromises His truth.

Israel returned to this proclamation for generations. Psalmists repeated it. Prophets appealed to it. Jonah complained about it. God had interpreted His own glory, and His people learned to pray with His self‑description on their lips.

The miracles of Jesus must be read in the same way. They are acts of mercy, displays of authority, and signs of the Kingdom. They also reveal the King. John calls them signs because they point beyond the event to the identity and glory of Jesus. He feeds the multitude and declares, “I am the bread of life” (John 6:35). He gives sight to a man born blind and declares, “I am the light of the world” (John 9:5). He calls Lazarus from the tomb after telling Martha, “I am the resurrection and the life.”

The deed interprets the claim, and the claim interprets the deed. Jesus does not perform wonders merely to prove that wonders are possible. His works reveal what God’s reign is like. Hunger, blindness, corruption, and death do not have the final word in His Kingdom.

The crowd often wanted the benefit without receiving the revelation. After the feeding, Jesus told them, “you seek Me, not because you saw the signs, but because you ate of the loaves and were filled” (John 6:26). They had experienced the miracle but missed the sign. Their stomachs were full, yet they had not understood who stood before them.

Jesus fed them anyway. His generosity extends even to those who misunderstand His gifts. That mercy should lead us toward Him, not encourage us to reduce Him to a source of desired outcomes. Preaching that promises health or wealth in exchange for sufficient faith takes a real human hunger and attaches it to a false account of God. It teaches people to seek His hand while ignoring His face. Biblical faith receives the gift with gratitude and follows the sign to Christ.

David understood how easily gifts can become detached from the Giver. Psalm 103 begins with a command to his own forgetful soul.

Bless the LORD, O my soul,
And forget not all His benefits:
Who forgives all your iniquities,
Who heals all your diseases,
Who redeems your life from destruction,
Who crowns you with lovingkindness and tender mercies,
Who satisfies your mouth with good things,
So that your youth is renewed like the eagle’s. (Psalm 103:2‑5)

Every benefit hangs on the word Who. David remembers forgiveness, healing, redemption, lovingkindness, mercy, and satisfaction. His list of gifts becomes a confession about the Giver. Memory protects worship because it reconnects God’s acts with God’s character.

This is why I encourage people to record answered prayers. The practice certainly cultivates gratitude, but it does more. Over time, the record becomes a portrait. One entry remembers provision, another patience, another correction, another sustaining grace. No single event contains the whole portrait. Together they train the heart to recognize the faithful God Scripture proclaims.

MANY YEARS AGO, AN old friend from my student days asked me to meet him in a large city far from both our homes. He had also been a spiritual mentor to me, and we had not seen each other for years. I reached the city first, checked into my hotel, and returned to the airport that evening to meet his flight.

The terminal was almost empty. I waited at baggage claim, where we had agreed to meet, but he did not appear. I could not reach him by phone. Because he rarely traveled that far, I dreaded having to tell his family that I had lost him on the other side of the world.

I searched the terminal for two hours. I went from baggage claim to ticketing, studying every face and checking every bench. Eventually I found him outside the doors with his bag at his feet. I hurried toward him and called his name.

He looked at me without recognition. We had not met in years, and I had gained some weight, but I was standing directly in front of him and repeating his name. After a long moment, he recognized me. We embraced and laughed. Later I asked how he had failed to know me.

“I have been looking for you for two hours,” he said. “I have come to a realization that I might not meet you here, and so I have closed my heart.”

He had stopped expecting me. His eyes continued to search, but his conclusion governed what he could recognize. The friend he had crossed an ocean to meet stood before him and looked like a stranger.

That experience helped me understand why we miss some answers to prayer. Expectation affects recognition. We imagine the shape, timing, and means of the answer. Then we watch for what we imagined. Provision may come through work we considered beneath us. The change we requested in another person may begin with gestures too small for our expectations. Because the answer does not resemble our picture, we declare that nothing has happened.

Our picture of the answer often contains a picture of God. Martha expected a healer who would arrive before death. We may expect a provider who uses only the means we respect, a restorer who follows our schedule, or a Father who proves His love through one particular gift. God will not conform Himself to those portraits. He answers as Himself.

The disciples also struggled to recognize God when His work contradicted their expectations. The travelers on the road to Emmaus said, “But we were hoping that it was He who was going to redeem Israel” (Luke 24:21). Their hope was in the past tense because the cross looked like the defeat of redemption. Yet the risen Redeemer was walking beside them. “But their eyes were restrained, so that they did not know Him” (Luke 24:16).

Mary Magdalene mistook Jesus for the gardener. Again and again, those who loved Him failed at first to recognize the risen Lord. This was more than a psychological failure. The Gospel accounts emphasize that recognition depended on Christ’s gracious self‑disclosure. He opened the Scriptures at Emmaus and made Himself known in the breaking of bread. He said, “Mary!” (John 20:16), and she knew His voice.

That grace also appeared at the airport. My friend’s closed heart did not end our meeting. I remained in front of him and kept calling his name until he recognized me. In a far greater way, Christ overcomes our failure to see. Human blindness does not exhaust divine patience.

As a child, I assumed that the stone was rolled away from Jesus’ tomb so that He could leave. But the risen Christ did not need an open door. He later appeared among disciples gathered behind locked doors. The opened tomb was for the witnesses. They needed to see that death had been defeated.

Christ walked the Emmaus road with disciples who could not recognize Him. He called Mary while she saw only a gardener. If disappointment has closed your heart, tell Him. Ask for the grace to recognize Him outside the limits of your expectation. The prayer itself is an act of openness.

When an answer comes, ask two questions. The first is natural: Did I receive what I requested? The second is more important: What does this show me about God as revealed in Jesus Christ?

Ask that question after a swift yes. Ask it when provision comes by an unexpected means. Ask it when the answer has taken so long that you nearly forgot the prayer. Test every conclusion by Scripture and by Christ. An outcome alone can be misread. Read within the truth of the gospel, however, answered prayer becomes a continuing education in the character of God.

Past answers can train recognition. We begin to notice the patience, holiness, generosity, and wisdom of God in forms we would once have overlooked. Surprise remains, because God remains greater than our understanding. Yet surprise no longer makes Him a stranger. We learn to say, “This is like Him.”

Martha came to the road grieving a healing that had not happened. Before the tomb opened, she confessed, “Yes, Lord, I believe that You are the Christ, the Son of God” (John 11:27). Lazarus would soon walk out alive, but Christ had already given Martha the deeper gift. He had made Himself known.

That knowledge changes desire. We still bring real needs and ask for real help. Yet as we see the Giver within His gifts, our wants begin to take their proper place around a greater love. Prayer has reached its deepest work when receiving from God leads us to desire God Himself.
