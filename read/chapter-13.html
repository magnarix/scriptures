Part IV · The Giver

Chapter Thirteen

Every Answer Reveals Who He Is

✦ ✦ ✦

≈ 12 min read

WHEN GOD ANSWERS A prayer, something of His character comes through with the answer. The need may belong to one season, but what we learn of Him can stay with us for the rest of our lives.

This does not mean that private experience adds to God’s revelation. Jesus Christ is the full and final revelation of the Father. Providence does not give us a different God from the One revealed in Christ. It teaches us to recognize Him within the particulars of our own lives, so that an answer to prayer becomes personal instruction in truths the gospel has already made known.

People often reveal this without realizing it. They tell me how the work came through, the child returned, or the medical report changed, and near the end of the story their attention shifts from the answer to the One who gave it. Then comes a sentence like, “I never knew He was like that.”

Sometimes the request was granted exactly as asked, and sometimes the person needed years to understand the answer. Either way, the lasting discovery concerns God. The circumstance settles into ordinary life, but the knowledge of the Giver stays.

Years ago, I was unemployed for months and prayed every day for work. Near the end of that period, I had a phone interview for a job I badly wanted, and I finished the call convinced that I had failed. I called the recruiter and said, “If I don’t get this job, please don’t call me. I am on an emotional roller coaster.” He kindly agreed.

A week later, on a Thursday afternoon, his number appeared on my phone. I answered, though my first thought was that he had broken his promise. He was calling with an offer better than I had dared to expect.

I told him how poorly I thought the interview had gone. He replied, “Yes, the manager actually mentioned that you seemed quite nervous during the interview.” They had heard the nervousness and hired me anyway.

The job mattered, but the deeper gift was the correction of my view of God. I had been treating Him as another interviewer, imagining that heaven grades our performance and gives only to those who appear composed. The offer exposed that false picture. Grace had met me in weakness, and I came away convinced that God’s grace is gentler and freer than my fear had imagined.

✦ ✦ ✦

JOHN 11 GIVES US a greater example. Lazarus was dying, so Martha and Mary sent word to Jesus: “Lord, he whom you love is ill” (John 11:3). Their prayer contained no instructions; they placed the sickness beside the love of Christ and entrusted the rest to Him. At hospital beds, I have often borrowed their words: Lord, the one You love is in trouble.

Jesus loved Martha, Mary, and Lazarus, and John states that fact immediately before telling us, “He stayed two days longer in the place where he was” (John 11:6). The placement is deliberate. Love explains the delay, though the delay did not look loving to those who endured it. Jesus had already said, “This illness is not unto death; it is for the glory of God, so that the Son of God may be glorified by means of it” (John 11:4). His purpose included both rescue and revelation.

By the time Jesus approached Bethany, Lazarus had been in the tomb for four days. Martha would later say, “Lord, by this time there will be an odor, for he has been dead four days” (John 11:39). She went out to meet Jesus and said, “Lord, if you had been here, my brother would not have died” (John 11:21). There was faith in her words, and grief too. She believed Jesus could have healed her brother, but she could not understand why He had not come.

Martha’s theology was true but incomplete. She knew Christ as healer, but hidden within that knowledge was a timetable. A healer must come before death. Jesus had missed the deadline, so the possibility appeared to be gone. Still, her faith reached beyond her understanding: “And even now I know that whatever you ask from God, God will give you” (John 11:22).

Jesus said, “Your brother will rise again” (John 11:23). Martha answered with sound doctrine: “I know that he will rise again in the resurrection at the last day” (John 11:24). She believed in the final resurrection, but that truth seemed far away from the brother whose body lay in a cave. Jesus then drew the doctrine into His own Person.

Jesus said to her, “I am the resurrection and the life; he who believes in me, though he die, yet shall he live, and whoever lives and believes in me shall never die. Do you believe this?” She said to him, “Yes, Lord; I believe that you are the Christ, the Son of God, he who is coming into the world.” (John 11:25‑27)

Martha had asked for healing, and Jesus gave her a fuller knowledge of Himself. He enlarged what she already knew rather than denying it. The One who heals is also the Resurrection and the Life. Resurrection is under Christ’s control, and it is also bound to His identity. Life belongs to Him and comes through Him.

The delay made that revelation possible. If Jesus had arrived when Martha expected, she would have known again that He could heal. Because He came after every human possibility had ended, she learned that death itself stood under His authority.

We should be careful here. We cannot interpret every delay as a promise that God will eventually give us the outcome we wanted. John tells us what Jesus intended at Bethany because Scripture reveals it; we do not possess that kind of access to every hidden purpose of providence. Yet delays can expose the false gods we carry within our true beliefs. We may confess the living God while expecting Him to work like a machine that pays out a blessing once enough faith has been put in. When He refuses that role, disappointment uncovers the bargain we had mistaken for trust.

An imaginary god can be managed, but no one ever meets him, because there is no one there. The living God is free and wise, Someone who gives Himself even when He does not submit to our script. At Bethany the broken timetable opened Martha’s eyes to the Lord of life.

Moses learned that divine glory is also self‑disclosure. He had seen plagues cease, the sea open, bread cover the ground, and water come from rock. Then he prayed, “I pray thee, show me thy glory” (Exodus 33:18). God hid him in the cleft of the rock and passed before him.

The Lord passed before him, and proclaimed, “The Lord, the Lord, a God merciful and gracious, slow to anger, and abounding in steadfast love and faithfulness.” (Exodus 34:6)

Moses asked to see and heard God name Himself. Divine glory was revealed as character, in mercy, grace, patience, goodness, and truth. This corrects the way we tend to imagine glory, as if it were only brightness and power. God’s power is glorious because it is the power of this God, whose holiness is never detached from His goodness and whose mercy never compromises His truth.

Israel returned to this proclamation for generations: psalmists repeated it, prophets appealed to it, and Jonah complained about it. God had interpreted His own glory, and His people learned to pray with His self‑description on their lips.

The miracles of Jesus must be read in the same way. They are acts of mercy, displays of authority, and signs of the Kingdom, and they also reveal the King. John calls them signs because they point beyond the event to the identity and glory of Jesus. He feeds the multitude and declares, “I am the bread of life” (John 6:35). He gives sight to a man born blind and declares, “I am the light of the world” (John 9:5). He calls Lazarus from the tomb after telling Martha, “I am the resurrection and the life.”

The deed and the claim interpret each other. Jesus does not perform wonders merely to prove that wonders are possible. His works reveal what God’s reign is like, a Kingdom where hunger, blindness, corruption, and death do not have the final word.

The crowd often wanted the benefit without receiving the revelation. After the feeding, Jesus told them, “you seek me, not because you saw signs, but because you ate your fill of the loaves” (John 6:26). They had experienced the miracle but missed the sign. Their stomachs were full, yet they had not understood who stood before them.

Jesus fed them anyway. His generosity extends even to those who misunderstand His gifts, and that mercy should lead us toward Him rather than teach us to treat Him as a source of desired outcomes. Preaching that promises health or wealth in exchange for sufficient faith takes a real human hunger and attaches it to a false account of God. It teaches people to seek His hand while ignoring His face. Biblical faith receives the gift with gratitude and follows the sign to Christ.

David understood how easily gifts can become detached from the Giver. Psalm 103 begins with a command to his own forgetful soul.

Bless the Lord, O my soul,
and forget not all his benefits,
who forgives all your iniquity,
who heals all your diseases,
who redeems your life from the Pit,
who crowns you with steadfast love and mercy,
who satisfies you with good as long as you live
so that your youth is renewed like the eagle’s. (Psalm 103:2‑5)

Every benefit hangs on the word Who. David remembers forgiveness, healing, redemption, lovingkindness, mercy, and satisfaction, and his list of gifts becomes a confession about the Giver. Memory protects worship because it reconnects God’s acts with God’s character.

This is why I encourage people to record answered prayers. The practice certainly cultivates gratitude, but it does more: over time the record becomes a portrait. One entry remembers provision, another patience, another correction, another sustaining grace. No single event contains the whole portrait, but together the entries train the heart to recognize the faithful God Scripture proclaims.

MANY YEARS AGO, AN old friend from my student days asked me to meet him in a large city far from both our homes. He had also been a spiritual mentor to me, and we had not seen each other for years. I reached the city first, checked into my hotel, and returned to the airport that evening to meet his flight.

The terminal was almost empty. I waited at baggage claim, where we had agreed to meet, but he did not appear. I could not reach him by phone. Because he rarely traveled that far, I dreaded having to tell his family that I had lost him on the other side of the world.

For two hours I searched the terminal, going from baggage claim to ticketing, studying every face and checking every bench. Eventually I found him outside the doors with his bag at his feet, and I hurried toward him and called his name.

He looked at me without recognition. We had not met in years, and I had gained some weight, but I was standing directly in front of him and repeating his name. After a long moment he recognized me, and we embraced and laughed. Later I asked how he had failed to know me.

“I have been looking for you for two hours,” he said. “I have come to a realization that I might not meet you here, and so I have closed my heart.”

He had stopped expecting me. He kept looking, but he had already decided I was not coming, and after that he could not see me. The friend he had crossed an ocean to meet stood before him and looked like a stranger.

That experience helped me understand why we miss some answers to prayer. Expectation affects recognition. We imagine the shape, the timing, and the means of the answer, and then we watch for what we imagined. Provision may come through work we considered beneath us. The change we requested in another person may begin with gestures too small for our expectations. Because the answer does not resemble our picture, we declare that nothing has happened.

Our picture of the answer often contains a picture of God. Martha expected a healer who would arrive before death. We may expect a provider who uses only the means we respect, a restorer who follows our schedule, or a Father who proves His love through one particular gift. God does not conform Himself to those portraits. He answers as He is.

The disciples also struggled to recognize God when His work contradicted their expectations. The travelers on the road to Emmaus said, “But we had hoped that he was the one to redeem Israel” (Luke 24:21). Their hope was in the past tense because the cross looked like the defeat of redemption. Yet the risen Redeemer was walking beside them. “But their eyes were kept from recognizing him” (Luke 24:16).

Mary Magdalene mistook Jesus for the gardener. Again and again, those who loved Him failed at first to recognize the risen Lord. Psychology alone does not explain this. The Gospel accounts emphasize that recognition depended on Christ’s gracious self‑disclosure. He opened the Scriptures at Emmaus and made Himself known in the breaking of bread. He said, “Mary” (John 20:16), and she knew His voice.

That grace also appeared at the airport. My friend’s closed heart did not end our meeting. I stood in front of him and kept calling his name until he recognized me. In a far greater way, Christ overcomes our failure to see. Human blindness does not exhaust His patience.

As a child, I assumed that the stone was rolled away from Jesus’ tomb so that He could leave. But the risen Christ did not need an open door; He later appeared among disciples gathered behind locked doors. The opened tomb was for the witnesses, who needed to see that death had been defeated.

Christ walked the Emmaus road with disciples who could not recognize Him, and He called Mary while she saw only a gardener. If disappointment has closed your heart, tell Him. Ask for the grace to recognize Him outside the limits of your expectation. The prayer itself is an act of openness.

When an answer comes, ask two questions. Did I receive what I requested? That one comes naturally. The more important question is what this answer shows me about God as revealed in Jesus Christ.

Ask that question after a swift yes. Ask it when provision comes by an unexpected means, and ask it again when the answer has taken so long that you nearly forgot the prayer. Test every conclusion by Scripture and by Christ, because an outcome alone can be misread. When we read an answer in the light of the gospel, it becomes another lesson in who God is.

Past answers can train recognition. We begin to notice the patience, holiness, generosity, and wisdom of God in forms we would once have overlooked. We are still surprised, because God is greater than our understanding, yet the surprise no longer makes Him a stranger. We learn to say, “This is like Him.”

Martha came to the road grieving a healing that had not happened. Before the tomb opened, she confessed, “Yes, Lord; I believe that you are the Christ, the Son of God” (John 11:27). Lazarus would soon walk out alive, but Christ had already given Martha the deeper gift. He had made Himself known.

That knowledge changes desire. We still bring real needs and ask for real help, yet as we see the Giver within His gifts, our wants begin to take their proper place around a greater love. Prayer has reached its deepest work when receiving from God leads us to desire God Himself.
