Chapter Four

Praying Without Knowing His Will

✦ ✦ ✦

≈ 13 min read

YOU MAY ASK FOR what you want before you know whether God wills it. Many believers think 1 John forbids this: “And this is the confidence which we have in him, that if we ask anything according to his will he hears us” (1 John 5:14). John begins with confidence. We often turn his assurance into a threat. If God hears only requests that match His will, we reason, we must discover that will before we speak.

The search can swallow the prayer. We wait for peace, a sign, or complete certainty. We read one book about finding God’s will and another explaining why the first book failed, and all the while the desire goes unspoken. I knew of a man who wanted to ask God for a wife. For two years he asked instead for a sign that marriage belonged to God’s will, because he feared making the request without prior approval.

The deeper fear is rejection. We fear God’s No, and we fear that the request itself will displease Him. Some teaching makes this worse by insisting that real faith always knows precisely what to ask, so the person who feels uncertain concludes that silence is safer. And we call the paralysis reverence.

I do not always know God’s will, and years of serving as a clergyman have not changed this. Scripture clearly says that God wills our sanctification and thanksgiving. The particular decisions mostly stay hidden. I have prayed with all my attention and risen no more certain than when I knelt. People ask clergy after funerals or in hospital corridors what God is doing, as though a title came with a key to His secret counsels.

No such key is issued. Anyone who claims unfailing knowledge of God’s will and perfectly predictable answers is describing a life no Christian possesses. I would be lying if I claimed it for myself.

Much of God’s will has already been revealed. Love God with all that you are. Love your neighbor. Forgive, speak truth, care for the vulnerable, and give thanks. These commands govern every season, and Scripture gives far less about the particulars that consume us: which school, which city, which work, and whether to marry.

Several choices may all be faithful: two roads can lie within the same obedience, one college need not contradict another, and becoming a doctor need not be more obedient than becoming an engineer. God’s moral will gives freedom its true boundaries, and within them discernment usually calls for wisdom rather than a private revelation.

This freedom belongs to our dignity as creatures. God does not ordinarily make our decisions in our place. Grace heals our judgment and strengthens virtue until we can choose what is good, and the person formed by Scripture and prayer can weigh circumstances, seek counsel, and decide. Such a choice may still involve risk. Faithfulness does not always feel certain.

We sometimes want a sign because a sign would relieve us of responsibility. If God selected the school, job, or city beyond dispute, we could never be blamed for choosing poorly. Yet mature obedience includes responsible freedom. God is still Lord of the roads we take, and His providence can work through imperfect decisions; a mistake may carry consequences without placing a repentant and trusting person beyond His care.

This does not make every option equally wise. Some choices fit our duties, gifts, relationships, and circumstances better than others, and discernment takes those realities seriously while accepting that we may understand them only in part. Wisdom chooses the good it can see now and entrusts what it cannot see to God.

A young man once agonized over two college acceptances, believing one envelope contained God’s will and the other a lifelong mistake. Neither school would prevent him from loving God or neighbor, so I told him to choose and enter the choice wholeheartedly. God does not conceal His will and then punish a child for failing to guess; He can meet us on either faithful road, though the work awaiting us will differ.

Our gifts and desires also belong to discernment. As a boy I was hopeless at the game every other boy played, and I needed no revelation to rule out a professional career. God could have performed a miracle, but building my future around that possibility would have been foolish. What I loved and could actually do provided ordinary signs of vocation.

Scripture does rule out some requests. Asking God to help us sin or to harm another person contradicts His revealed character. Yet that boundary surrounds a wide field of freedom. Within it we may ask without waiting for certainty.

God’s will is larger than the answer to a private puzzle. It is His wise and loving purpose to unite all things in Christ and make His people holy. A particular answer belongs within that purpose. We may not see how. This protects discernment from superstition. Rather than leaving us to play games with signs, God forms people who can use their freedom faithfully.

The leper’s words from the previous chapter provide a brief model: “Lord, if you will, you can make me clean.” He named Christ’s power and left Christ’s intention free. Some preaching moves too easily from what God can do to what He will do. Divine power guarantees neither our prediction nor our preferred result, and promises God never made eventually produce disillusionment.

We can make the opposite mistake after asking. We attach ourselves to the desired object and rename our preference God’s will, so that if things turn out otherwise we feel God has failed. Petition should name the desire fully and entrust its form to God, for our heart belongs to Him rather than to any gift He may give or withhold. Nothing reaches us outside His knowledge, though much that He permits is painful and gives Him no delight.

The hardest prayer often concerns healing, for we may never know in this life whether God will restore the body. I have seen healing come. I have also watched illness stay while grace formed the sufferer and touched other lives through it. Disease remains an enemy. Providence can still draw good from what God permits.

God created the body for life, and Christ’s healings reveal the compassion of the Kingdom, so we may ask boldly for bodily restoration. When illness continues, we do not have to pretend that pain is a hidden blessing; we may oppose it with medicine, care, and prayer while trusting that it cannot separate us from Christ.

Christ alone redeems, and suffering has no saving power of its own; yet He does not waste the pain of those who love Him. He can take unavoidable pain, borne in union with Him, and make it fruitful for others in ways beyond our knowledge, comforting us so that we may comfort others (2 Corinthians 1:4). This is never a reason to neglect relief or to glorify harm.

Providence holds both truths together: God opposes evil, and evil cannot defeat His final purpose. We often see only the first truth in our longing for rescue or only the second in our effort to surrender. Christian prayer keeps asking for deliverance while refusing to believe that an unanswered request places life outside God’s hands.

We do not need complete knowledge of God’s will before we pray. We can name the desire and surrender to a will we know only in part, trusting God’s goodness and accepting that His wisdom exceeds our sight.

Scripture gives us different voices of faith. Mary believed that God’s promise to her would be fulfilled, while Shadrach, Meshach, and Abednego confessed before the furnace that God could save them and declared that they would stay faithful if He did not. Both responses trusted God. Faith may expect a miracle or stand firm without one; its center is the presence and character of God within uncertainty.

Scripture also offers a prayer for this uncertainty. Luke first places it on the lips of ten men with leprosy, whose disease kept them outside the village and away from healthy people; Jews and a Samaritan had been forced into one company by their affliction. When Jesus approached, they stood at a distance and cried, “Jesus, Master, have mercy on us” (Luke 17:13).

They did not specify the form of the answer; their condition made the desire obvious, so they appealed to Christ’s mercy and left its shape with Him. The cry joined distress and trust. They had reached the end of their resources, yet they believed that His compassion could reach across the distance. Jesus sent them toward the priests, and all ten were cleansed along the way.

The mercy prayer leaves room for specific requests and gathers them into a larger appeal to God’s character. We may say, “Heal me,” and also say, “Have mercy.” The first names the form we desire, and the second entrusts us to the compassionate wisdom of Christ.

Mercy is more than leniency toward failure. In Scripture it is God’s faithful love moving toward misery. It forgives sin. It hears distress and answers with help, restoring the communion that guilt and shame have broken. The word lets our prayer stay precise about need while leaving God free to answer according to the whole truth of our life.

The cry can carry many situations without becoming vague. “Lord, have mercy” may contain a plea for healing, protection, courage, repentance, or peace. God hears the desire inside the words; we appeal to what He has revealed about Himself when we cannot discern what form His help should take.

Years ago, someone complained after hearing believers in a distant country repeat “Lord, have mercy” throughout their worship. He thought repetition proved weak faith. Surely confident people would say the words once and move on. Yet the ten lepers had no more complete prayer, and Christians facing death have carried this cry when every other request had fallen away.

We never need special permission to ask for mercy, for God has revealed Himself as merciful: “The Lord is merciful and gracious, slow to anger and abounding in steadfast love” (Psalm 103:8). We can pray these words in a hospital corridor, at a graveside, in traffic, or at night when an old fear returns. The prayer requires only need and some small willingness to turn toward God.

Jesus placed the same appeal in another story. Two men went to the temple to pray, a Pharisee and a tax collector; Jesus addressed the parable to people confident of their own righteousness.

But the tax collector, standing far off, would not even lift up his eyes to heaven, but beat his breast, saying, “God, be merciful to me a sinner!” (Luke 18:13)

The tax collector offers no virtues or explanations; he asks God to cross a distance he cannot cross. Nearby, the Pharisee names real religious practices, and his fasting and tithing may be true, yet each sentence returns to his achievement. God becomes a witness to goodness the Pharisee treats as his own possession. Even gratitude can become self‑congratulation.

The tax collector stands in truth, with no acceptable version of himself to present, and depends upon God’s character. Jesus says that he “went down to his house justified rather than the other” (Luke 18:14). Justification comes to the person who has stopped trying to establish a claim upon God.

✦ ✦ ✦

I ONCE PRAYED THE Pharisee’s prayer within minutes of receiving mercy. While driving to church, I entered an intersection after the light turned green, and a van crossed against its red light at high speed and missed me by a few feet. My heart pounded. Then I prayed, “Thank God I am not that person,” and began imagining the driver’s sins.

God had spared my life, and my first response was an inventory of another person’s failures. Whenever we say, “Thank God I am not,” the blank fills quickly: another political group, a troubled family, a reckless driver. I had turned rescue into judgment.

The lepers and the tax collector needed no hidden information about God’s plan; they knew their need and appealed to His mercy. “God, be merciful to me a sinner” always agrees with His revealed will, because God desires the sinner’s return and gives grace to those who seek Him.

Name the desire when you can, and pray for mercy when you cannot tell whether to ask for an open door or strength before a closed one. Mercy can mean pardon, or healing, or simply the presence of God in the form His wisdom chooses. The Father understands the request before we can arrange it into words.

✦ ✦ ✦

SOMETIMES EVEN SEVEN WORDS are too many. I have held a hand beside a hospital bed, intending to pray aloud for the family, and found only my own breathing. If prayer depended upon our ability to speak, such moments would be prayerless; but Christian prayer is participation before it is speech, and the Spirit draws our weakness into the Son’s intercession before the Father. Paul says:

Likewise the Spirit helps us in our weakness; for we do not know how to pray as we ought, but the Spirit himself intercedes for us with sighs too deep for words. (Romans 8:26)

Paul includes himself among those who do not know how to pray as they should. This ignorance belongs to ordinary Christian life, and God has made provision for it within the life of the Trinity. The Spirit receives the groaning weakness of believers and intercedes within them.

The next verse says that the Spirit intercedes “according to the will of God” (Romans 8:27). The Spirit knows the will hidden from us, and our uncertainty cannot interrupt His prayer. Even the sigh we never managed to put into words is already before the Father, carried in the communion of the Spirit and the Son.

The life of prayer is therefore Trinitarian even at its weakest. The believer turns toward the Father; the Son gives access and intercedes as High Priest; the Spirit dwells within human frailty and makes it capable of communion. We may experience only confusion, yet divine love is active beneath and within that confusion.

Romans 8 does not treat groaning as failed prayer. Creation groans, believers groan, and the Spirit intercedes with groanings beyond speech. These movements belong to the labor of a creation awaiting redemption. The groan acknowledges that the present world is unfinished and still trusts the God who will complete it.

Even silence beside a hospital bed can bear prayer. A hand held in love, a breath offered to God, or tears that cannot explain themselves may be taken into the Spirit’s intercession, and the Father receives them through the Son with greater understanding than our best sentences could provide.

Our own prayer is still needed, for the Spirit heals and elevates it from within. He does not replace our desire with something foreign. He carries the whole person toward the Father’s purpose, including the confused desire we cannot sort out. Our weakness becomes the place of divine assistance rather than a barrier to prayer.

✦ ✦ ✦

A DANGER REMAINS AFTER we ask. We may entrust a request to God at night and reclaim it by noon. Repetition slowly becomes inspection, and the prayer turns into a project we manage with divine assistance. I have tried to steer God’s hand toward an outcome already chosen and called my grip faith.

Faith relies upon the living God and can return to the same petition for years, yet it does not make constant surveillance a proof of devotion. Obsession can dress itself up as faith. Persistence goes back to God. Surveillance just keeps checking whether He has done what we told Him.

When I feel that grip forming, I use a simple practice, personal medicine rather than a universal method. I pray:

Lord, I am going to rest for a while from praying about this, so that I do not become a slave to the outcome I am hoping for. I have already placed it in Your hands.

Then I stop repeating that request for several days or weeks while continuing to meet God about everything else. This pause does not oppose persistence. I have carried some petitions for decades. The pause interrupts only the anxious repetition that digs up the seed each morning to inspect it. A farmer leaves the seed in the earth and sleeps. When I have kept this rest honestly, the request has stayed safe with God and my heart has grown freer.

The freedom may not last. We can name the desire and surrender the outcome, and we can pray for mercy. Then we wait. The scan comes back unchanged. A door stays shut, and no call comes. Heaven may seem to say nothing. I know that country and have lived there for years at a time. Prayer without knowledge of God’s will eventually leads us into the still harder mystery of waiting when no answer can be heard.
