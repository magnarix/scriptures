Part II · The Waiting

Chapter Five

The Fourth Answer

✦ ✦ ✦

≈ 13 min read

PRAYER SOMETIMES RECEIVES NO answer we can recognize. Silence is different from a clear refusal or an evident delay. We ask, and nothing seems to return. Christian tradition has sometimes called this spiritual desolation: felt consolation disappears, and faith must cling to God without emotional support.

I was taught that God answers Yes, No, or Wait. The saying contains truth, and I repeated it for years. It also gives every experience a tidy shelf. Silence has no shelf. No door opens or closes. We search for a sin that may be blocking the answer. We fast, ask others to pray, and try again with tears at midnight or discipline at noon. Still nothing moves. Eventually a colder question appears: is anyone there?

Silence visits large and small decisions. I have asked which of two faithful roads to take and risen with no more certainty. I have carried petitions for loved ones through years without recognizing a reply. Someone who prayed for a wandering child told me that the quiet became harder to bear than the fear itself.

Some believers stop expecting anything from prayer. They describe it as writing repeatedly to an address that never answers. I understand the fatigue. Speaking into a room where nothing moves has emptied hearts more effectively than many arguments against God.

Silence also tempts us to interpret what has not been said. We write the message ourselves: God is angry; I asked wrongly; I am beneath His notice. Faithful people have handed me these conclusions in tears. They believed they were reading God’s verdict while holding only a blank page and their own fear. I have done the same. We can live for years inside a condemnation God never pronounced.

This chapter makes a careful claim. Divine silence does not prove divine absence. Scripture often shows God hidden from human perception while remaining faithfully present. Sometimes that hiddenness draws faith beyond dependence on feelings and explanations. We cannot assign this purpose confidently to every unexplained sorrow. We can refuse to let silence contradict what God has revealed about Himself in Christ.

Several experiences can feel like divine silence. Exhaustion, grief, depression, trauma, and physical illness can dull our awareness of everything, including God. Sin can make us avoid His voice. A season of ordinary spiritual dryness may come without any clear cause. These conditions deserve different forms of care. Prayer should never be used to avoid medical help, honest repentance, rest, or the company of wise people.

The theological question begins after we have admitted those realities. God is never merely one more feeling inside us. His presence does not rise and fall with our ability to perceive it. The covenant rests upon His action and promise. A clouded mind can lose the sensation of sunlight without moving the sun.

This distinction does not make the darkness painless. Human beings know God through created faculties: thought, memory, affection, body, and community. When these cease to carry consolation, faith feels stripped. We continue by holding to what God has made objectively known in Christ until feeling and understanding return, or until faith passes into sight.

The Bible speaks from within this experience. Habakkuk was a prophet, someone called to hear God’s word, yet his book begins with a complaint:

O LORD, how long shall I cry, And You will not hear? Even cry out to You, “Violence!” And You will not save. (Habakkuk 1:2)

“How long” tells us that the silence has outlasted ordinary patience. Habakkuk still addresses the LORD. His belief that God hears and saves makes this particular silence unbearable. Scripture preserves his protest without making it more respectable. God does not require us to claim an answer we have not received.

Lament keeps faith truthful. It remembers God’s character and names the experience that seems to contradict it. The tension remains inside prayer. Habakkuk does not protect God with a false report, and he does not protect himself by walking away. He waits before the One whose silence he cannot explain.

This waiting is an act of covenant faith. Habakkuk uses God’s name while asking why God appears unlike Himself. The complaint depends upon a history of promise. A person who expected nothing from God would feel no scandal in His silence. Lament hurts because faith remembers that the LORD has heard, saved, and acted before.

Scripture gives the complaint a place within revelation itself. God allows human anguish to speak in the book through which He speaks to us. This tells us something about inspiration and prayer: divine truth does not erase the human voice. Grace can carry protest without approving every conclusion the sufferer reaches.

Lament also keeps pain from becoming accusation against the self. The sufferer need not assume that silence proves personal failure. Habakkuk directs the unresolved question toward God and waits for God’s reply. The blank page remains blank until God writes upon it.

YEARS AGO I STOOD beside someone I loved moments after he died. In the corridor I had prepared myself to pray aloud, speak a true word, and perhaps quote Scripture. I had done these things at other bedsides. Then I entered the room. Death was closer than I had ever known it. The stillness felt unnatural, and every prepared sentence collapsed. I could only tremble.

I had expected to feel God’s distance. Instead, the room carried a severe nearness that felt more like summons than consolation. Words seemed too small. I had entered expecting to close the story with prayer. I stood before a silence I could not contain.

Years later, reading the end of Mark’s Gospel, I recognized where I had been standing. Mark tells how the women came to the tomb at dawn with spices, carrying the last gestures of love in a world still governed by death. They found the stone rolled away, the body gone, and a messenger announcing news too large for any human frame to hold. Mark sets down what happened next in a single astonishing sentence:

So they went out quickly and fled from the tomb, for they trembled and were amazed. And they said nothing to anyone, for they were afraid. (Mark 16:8)

The women’s fear once looked like failure to me. The hospital room helped me read it differently. They had encountered an event too large for their understanding. The empty tomb overturned everything they knew about death. Their trembling was the body’s response to a mystery the mind could not yet hold.

The tomb looked like another absence. Christ’s body was gone, and the women initially had only a promise that He had gone ahead. Yet this absence announced the Resurrection. We should not turn that pattern into an easy explanation for every silence. The empty tomb does show that apparent absence can conceal an action of God greater than anything we expected.

The silence came again more recently. Someone I loved lay in a hospital approaching death. For days, machines made the loudest sounds in the room. I had spent years beside the dying and mourning. I knew Scripture, prayers, and the language used to accompany grief. When the dying person was mine to lose, that vocabulary shrank. Knowing consoling words proved different from being consoled by them.

One afternoon a nurse found me in the hallway. She was doing nothing official. She had simply noticed, the way some people notice, that the man who was supposed to be the strong one was worn through. She looked at me and said the sentence I have carried ever since.

“It is okay to grieve, since grief is an expression of love.”

I had spoken similar words to many people. They carried different weight when I received them. The nurse comforted the comforter without knowing she had done so. Her kindness bore grace as surely as many sermons.

Her sentence also spoke Christian truth. Grief grows from love. Resurrection hope does not cancel tears because hope does not erase the relationship death has interrupted. While heaven seemed silent over my petitions, mercy reached me through an ordinary person in a hospital corridor. God’s help had taken a form I was in danger of overlooking.

JESUS ENTERED THE DEPTHS of human desolation on the Cross. Matthew records that at the ninth hour He cried aloud with the opening words of Psalm 22:

My God, My God, why have You forsaken Me? Why are You so far from helping Me, And from the words of My groaning? (Psalm 22:1)

The eternal Son remained in unbroken communion with the Father. On the Cross He carried the full human experience of abandonment into that communion. He took Israel’s ancient cry of desolation upon His own lips. No believer who prays into darkness now prays alone. Christ has entered that darkness and made its cry His own.

The words begin, “My God, My God.” Even at the point of desolation, Jesus addresses the Father in the language of belonging. Psalm 22 later declares, “Nor has He hidden His face from Him; But when He cried to Him, He heard” (Psalm 22:24). The psalm holds felt forsakenness and the truth of being heard within one prayer. The Resurrection reveals that the Father’s hidden action ran deeper than the visible darkness of the Cross.

We should speak carefully here. Our suffering does not equal Christ’s Passion, and we do not explain every silence by calling it a private Good Friday. The Cross gives a firmer assurance: God has entered the place where He seems absent. Christ’s redeeming presence reaches even the person who cannot feel it.

The humanity of Jesus makes this assurance concrete. He did not observe abandonment from a protected distance. His human mind and heart passed through anguish, darkness, and death. The divine Son made these experiences His own without ceasing to be one with the Father. Christian faith therefore refuses two easy answers: that the suffering is unreal, or that God has withdrawn from it.

The Cross also reveals the form of God’s presence. Sometimes He is present as deliverance from suffering. At Calvary He is present within suffering, bearing it and transforming its final meaning. The Father does not cease loving the Son when the Son can no longer see any sign of victory. Resurrection vindicates a communion that darkness concealed and could never destroy.

This matters when prayer yields no felt response. Our assurance rests in Christ’s completed work rather than in our ability to reproduce His confidence. He has carried human prayer through apparent abandonment into the Father’s hands. We may join our thin prayer to His perfect one.

Years of silence have taught me to distinguish hiddenness from absence. Isaiah praises the mystery directly: “Truly You are God, who hide Yourself, O God of Israel, the Savior!” (Isaiah 45:15). God remains beyond our sight and is still the Savior. His hiddenness belongs partly to the distance between Creator and creature. We cannot reduce Him to an object we inspect or an experience we control.

A child understands one simple difference between hiding and leaving. A parent hidden nearby remains attentive even when unseen. The comparison soon reaches its limit because spiritual desolation is no game. Still, it helps us refuse the conclusion that invisibility equals abandonment.

God may withdraw felt consolation so that faith rests more fully in His revealed truth. He may also be working in ways that remain completely unknown to us. We should not pretend to identify His purpose in every silence. We know that He has given Himself in Christ and remains faithful to His covenant.

Consolation is a gift, and we should receive it gratefully. Yet consolation can become confused with God Himself. We may begin to trust the warmth of prayer, the clarity of insight, or the sense of being guided. When those gifts disappear, we discover whether faith was resting in God or in the experience He gave.

Spiritual desolation can purify this attachment. The purification is usually hidden from the person undergoing it. Faith becomes less able to admire itself. Prayer may shrink to attendance: showing up, keeping the appointed hour, or saying one psalm without satisfaction. Such fidelity appears poor, but it can express a love freed from immediate reward.

We must still avoid turning dryness into an accomplishment. No one should seek darkness or distrust joy. God gives consolation for our strengthening and permits desolation according to wisdom we rarely understand. The faithful response in either state is the same: receive the present grace and remain available to God.

Silence can slowly change the questions we bring. For years I demanded an explanation for suffering. Even when an explanation came, it rarely reached the ache beneath the question. Eventually “Why?” opened into “Who are You here?” I have received few complete explanations. I have repeatedly received God through Scripture, worship, the care of others, and the hidden endurance grace supplied. His self‑gift does not solve every mystery. It keeps mystery from becoming emptiness.

Some people meet silence and step away. Usually they drift into indifference or a competent self‑reliance that keeps the forms of faith while expecting nothing. God becomes a respected memory rather than Someone addressed as a friend. Some return years later because the hunger endured after every substitute failed.

If silence has made you fear that faith was an illusion, the wound itself tells us something. We do not ache for a voice that never mattered. Wounded faith is still living faith. Remain with Christ as you are able. When words fail, tears, tiredness, and the simple decision to stay can become prayer in the Spirit.

Mother Teresa offers a modern witness that great love and great interior darkness can occupy the same life. She spent decades caring for dying people with visible joy. Letters published after her death in Come Be My Light revealed long periods without a felt sense of God’s presence. She wrote, “I look and do not see, listen and do not hear.”

Some readers treated the letters as evidence of hypocrisy. I read them as evidence of costly fidelity. She continued serving, praying, and keeping her vows without emotional reward. Her love became an act rooted in God’s prior gift rather than in spiritual warmth.

Her experience should not become the standard for holiness. Consolation is also grace, and many faithful lives contain no prolonged darkness of this kind. Her witness makes a narrower point: the absence of feeling cannot prove the absence of love. Grace may bear visible fruit in a life whose interior weather remains dark.

Her service also shows that love can remain active when prayer feels empty. She continued to recognize Christ in people whom others had abandoned. The hidden God met her through concrete acts of mercy even when inward consolation failed. Fidelity moved outward through hands, schedules, promises, and daily duties.

This gives silence an ethical test. A darkness that turns us inward may need help, correction, or rest. Grace draws us, however slowly, toward love of God and neighbor. We may feel nothing heroic while making the next faithful act. Its fruit can become visible to others long before it becomes visible to us.

The story of Lazarus joins love and delay in a sentence that has steadied me. When Lazarus became sick, his sisters sent Jesus an urgent message: “Lord, behold, he whom You love is sick” (John 11:3). Then they waited. John describes what followed:

Now Jesus loved Martha and her sister and Lazarus. So, when He heard that he was sick, He stayed two more days in the place where He was. (John 11:5‑6)

John deliberately connects the love and the delay: Jesus loved them, so He stayed. The delay served a purpose the sisters could not see. We must not infer that every delay will receive such a clear explanation. This story reveals that Christ’s love can remain fully active when His timing wounds those who love Him.

When Jesus reached the tomb, He wept (John 11:35). His purpose did not make grief unnecessary. Divine providence and human sorrow met in His tears. The nurse in the hallway had the Gospel on her side: grief expresses love, and hope does not require a dry face.

Silence therefore cannot suspend the love God has revealed in Christ. The remaining question concerns what grace does during the two days, two years, or twenty years of waiting. We may not see the whole answer. We can begin by looking closer than the sky: at the quiet work taking place within the person who waits.
