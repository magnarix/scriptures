Part II · The Waiting

Chapter Five

The Fourth Answer

✦ ✦ ✦

≈ 13 min read

PRAYER SOMETIMES RECEIVES NO answer we can recognize, and this silence is different from a clear refusal or an evident delay. We ask, and nothing seems to return. Christian tradition has a name for it: spiritual desolation. Felt consolation disappears, and faith must cling to God without emotional support.

I was taught that God answers Yes, No, or Wait. The saying contains truth, and I repeated it for years. It also gives every experience a tidy shelf, and silence has no shelf. No door opens or closes. We search for a sin that may be blocking the answer; we fast, ask others to pray, try again with tears at midnight, and still nothing moves. Eventually a colder question appears.

Is anyone there?

Silence visits decisions large and small. I have asked which of two faithful roads to take and risen with no more certainty, and I have carried petitions for loved ones through years without recognizing a reply. Someone who prayed for a wandering child told me the quiet became harder to bear than the fear itself.

Some believers stop expecting anything from prayer at all and describe it as writing again and again to an address that never answers. I understand the fatigue. Speaking into a room where nothing moves has emptied hearts more effectively than many arguments against God ever have.

Silence also tempts us to interpret what has not been said. We write the message ourselves: God is angry; I asked wrongly; I am beneath His notice. Faithful people have handed me these conclusions in tears, believing they were reading God’s verdict when they held only a blank page and their own fear. I have done the same. We can live for years inside a condemnation God never pronounced.

Divine silence does not prove divine absence. Scripture often shows God hidden from human perception while He is still faithfully present, and sometimes that hiddenness draws faith beyond its dependence on feelings and explanations. We can refuse to let silence contradict what God has revealed about Himself in Christ.

Several experiences can feel like divine silence. Exhaustion, grief, depression, trauma, and physical illness can dull our awareness of everything, God included; sin can make us avoid His voice; a season of ordinary spiritual dryness may come without any clear cause. These conditions deserve different forms of care, and prayer should never be used to avoid medical help, honest repentance, rest, or the company of wise people.

The theological question begins after we have admitted those realities. God is never merely one more feeling inside us, and His presence does not rise and fall with our ability to perceive it. The covenant rests upon His action and promise. A clouded mind can lose the sensation of sunlight without moving the sun.

None of this makes the darkness painless. Human beings know God through the created faculties of thought, memory, affection, body, and community. When these cease to carry consolation, faith feels stripped, and we continue by holding to what God has made objectively known in Christ until feeling and understanding return, or until faith passes into sight.

The Bible speaks from within this experience. Habakkuk was a prophet, a man called to hear God’s word, yet his book begins with a complaint:

O Lord, how long shall I cry for help, and thou wilt not hear? Or cry to thee “Violence!” and thou wilt not save? (Habakkuk 1:2)

“How long” tells us the silence has outlasted ordinary patience. Yet Habakkuk still addresses the LORD, and the silence is unbearable to him because he believes God hears and saves. Scripture preserves his protest without making it more respectable, and God does not require us to claim an answer we have not received.

Lament keeps faith truthful. It remembers God’s character while naming the experience that seems to contradict it, and it holds that tension inside prayer. Habakkuk does not protect God with a false report, and he does not protect himself by walking away. He waits before the One whose silence he cannot explain. This waiting is an act of covenant faith. Habakkuk uses God’s name while asking why God appears unlike Himself, and the complaint depends upon a history of promise. A person who expected nothing from God would feel no scandal in His silence. Lament hurts because faith remembers that the LORD has heard, saved, and acted before.

Scripture gives the complaint a place within revelation itself. God allows human anguish to speak in the very book through which He speaks to us. This tells us something about inspiration and about prayer, for divine truth does not erase the human voice, and grace can carry protest without approving every conclusion the sufferer reaches.

Lament also keeps pain from becoming an accusation against the self, for the sufferer need not assume that silence proves personal failure. Habakkuk directs the unresolved question toward God and waits for God’s reply.

YEARS AGO I STOOD beside someone I loved moments after he died. In the corridor I had prepared myself to pray aloud and speak a true word, perhaps to quote Scripture; I had done these things at other bedsides. Then I entered the room. Death was closer than I had ever known it, the stillness felt unnatural, and every prepared sentence collapsed. I could only tremble.

I had expected to feel God’s distance. Instead the room felt occupied. The nearness was severe, more like a summons than a comfort, and the words I had planned were too small. I had entered meaning to close the story with prayer, and I just stood there.

Years later, reading the end of Mark’s Gospel, I recognized where I had been standing. Mark tells how the women came to the tomb at dawn with spices, carrying the last gestures of love in a world still governed by death. They found the stone rolled away, the body gone, and a messenger announcing news no one was prepared for. Mark sets down what happened next in a single astonishing sentence:

And they went out and fled from the tomb; for trembling and astonishment had come upon them; and they said nothing to any one, for they were afraid. (Mark 16:8)

The women’s fear once looked like failure to me, until the hospital room taught me to read it differently. They had met something their understanding could not take in. The empty tomb overturned everything they knew about death, and the trembling was what a body does in front of that.

The tomb looked like another absence. Christ’s body was gone, and the women at first had only a promise that He had gone ahead. Yet this absence announced the Resurrection. The empty tomb shows that apparent absence can conceal an action of God greater than anything we expected.

✦ ✦ ✦

THE SILENCE CAME AGAIN more recently, when someone I loved lay in a hospital approaching death and for days the machines made the loudest sounds in the room. I had spent years beside the dying and the mourning; I knew the Scripture and the prayers, the whole language used to accompany grief. When the dying person was mine to lose, that vocabulary shrank. I had the words. They did not help.

One afternoon a nurse found me in the hallway. She was doing nothing official; she had simply noticed, the way some people notice, that the man who was supposed to be the strong one was worn through. She looked at me and said the sentence I have carried ever since.

“It is okay to grieve, since grief is an expression of love.”

I had spoken similar words to many people, but they carried different weight when I received them. The nurse comforted the comforter without knowing she had done so, and her kindness bore grace as surely as many sermons.

Her sentence also spoke Christian truth. Grief grows from love, and Resurrection hope does not cancel tears, because hope does not erase the relationship death has interrupted. While heaven seemed silent over my petitions, mercy reached me through an ordinary person in a hospital corridor. God’s help had taken a form I was in danger of overlooking.

JESUS ENTERED THE DEPTHS of human desolation on the Cross. Matthew records that at the ninth hour He cried aloud with the opening words of Psalm 22:

My God, my God, why hast thou forsaken me? Why art thou so far from helping me, from the words of my groaning? (Psalm 22:1)

The eternal Son never left His unbroken communion with the Father. On the Cross He carried the full human experience of abandonment into that communion, taking Israel’s ancient cry of desolation upon His own lips. No believer who prays into darkness now prays alone, for Christ has entered that darkness and made its cry His own.

The words begin, “My God, My God.” Even at the point of desolation Jesus addresses the Father in the language of belonging, and Psalm 22 later declares, “he has not hid his face from him, but has heard, when he cried to him” (Psalm 22:24). The psalm holds felt forsakenness and the truth of being heard within one prayer. The Resurrection reveals that the Father’s hidden action ran deeper than the visible darkness of the Cross.

We should speak carefully here. Our suffering does not equal Christ’s Passion, and we do not explain every silence by calling it a private Good Friday. The Cross gives a firmer assurance. God has entered the place where He seems absent, and Christ’s redeeming presence reaches even the person who cannot feel it.

The humanity of Jesus makes this assurance concrete. He did not observe abandonment from a protected distance. His human mind and heart passed through anguish, darkness, and death, and the divine Son made these experiences His own without ceasing to be one with the Father. Christian faith therefore refuses two easy answers: that the suffering is unreal, or that God has withdrawn from it.

The Cross also reveals the form of God’s presence. Sometimes He delivers us out of suffering. At Calvary He remained within it, bearing it and transforming its final meaning. The Father does not cease loving the Son when no sign of victory can be seen, and the Resurrection vindicates a communion that darkness concealed and could never destroy.

When prayer yields no felt response, our assurance rests in Christ’s completed work rather than in our ability to reproduce His confidence. He has carried human prayer through apparent abandonment into the Father’s hands, and we may join our thin prayer to His perfect one.

Years of silence have taught me to distinguish hiddenness from absence. Isaiah praises the mystery directly: “Truly, thou art a God who hidest thyself, O God of Israel, the Savior” (Isaiah 45:15). God is beyond our sight and still the Savior. His hiddenness belongs partly to the distance between Creator and creature, for we cannot reduce Him to an object we inspect or an experience we control.

A child understands one simple difference between hiding and leaving. A parent hidden nearby is still attentive even when unseen. The comparison soon reaches its limit, because spiritual desolation is no game. Yet it helps us refuse the conclusion that invisibility equals abandonment.

God may withdraw felt consolation so that faith rests more fully in His revealed truth, or He may be working in ways completely unknown to us. What we know is that He has given Himself in Christ and keeps faith with His covenant.

Consolation is a gift, and we should receive it gratefully. Yet consolation can become confused with God Himself. We may begin to trust the warmth of prayer, the clarity of insight, or the sense that we are being guided. When those gifts disappear, we find out where our faith was resting. Was it in God, or in the experience He gave?

Spiritual desolation can purify this attachment, though the purification is usually hidden from the person undergoing it. Faith becomes less able to admire itself. Prayer may shrink to attendance: showing up, keeping the appointed hour, saying one psalm without satisfaction. Such fidelity appears poor, but it can express a love freed from immediate reward.

Dryness itself earns no merit, and darkness is nothing to seek. Joy, when it comes, can be welcomed. God gives consolation for our strengthening and permits desolation according to wisdom we rarely understand. The faithful response in either state is to receive the present grace and stay available to God.

Silence can slowly change the questions we bring. For years I demanded an explanation for suffering, and even when an explanation came, it rarely reached the ache beneath the question. Eventually “Why?” opened into “Who are You here?” I have received few complete explanations, yet I have received God again and again: through Scripture and worship, through the care of others, and through a hidden endurance that grace supplied. His self‑gift does not solve every mystery, but it keeps mystery from becoming emptiness.

Some people meet silence and step away. Usually they drift into indifference or a competent self‑reliance that keeps the forms of faith while expecting nothing. God becomes a respected memory. They no longer talk to Him as to a friend. Some return years later, because the hunger endured after every substitute failed.

If silence has made you fear that faith was an illusion, the wound itself tells us something. We do not ache for a voice that never mattered. Wounded faith is still living faith. Stay with Christ as you are able, and when words fail, your tears, your tiredness, and the simple decision to stay can become prayer in the Spirit.

✦ ✦ ✦

MOTHER TERESA OFFERS A modern witness that great love and great interior darkness can occupy the same life. She spent decades caring for dying people with visible joy, yet the letters published after her death in Come Be My Light revealed long periods without a felt sense of God’s presence. She wrote, “I look and do not see, listen and do not hear.”

Some readers treated the letters as evidence of hypocrisy. I read them as costly fidelity. She went on serving and praying and keeping her vows without emotional reward. Her love ran on God’s prior gift, not on spiritual warmth.

Consolation is also grace, and many faithful lives contain no prolonged darkness of this kind. Her witness makes a narrower point. A believer may stop feeling God’s presence without ceasing to love Him. Grace may bear visible fruit in a life whose interior weather stays dark.

Her service also shows that love can go on working when prayer feels empty. She continued to recognize Christ in people whom others had abandoned, and the hidden God met her through concrete acts of mercy even when inward consolation failed. Fidelity moved outward through hands, schedules, promises, and daily duties.

Silence also has a practical test. A darkness that turns us inward may need help, correction, or rest, but grace draws us, however slowly, toward love of God and neighbor. We may feel nothing heroic while making the next faithful act, and its fruit can become visible to others long before it becomes visible to us.

✦ ✦ ✦

THE STORY OF LAZARUS joins love and delay in a sentence that has steadied me. When Lazarus became sick, his sisters sent Jesus an urgent message: “Lord, he whom you love is ill” (John 11:3). Then they waited. John describes what followed:

Now Jesus loved Martha and her sister and Lazarus. So when he heard that he was ill, he stayed two days longer in the place where he was. (John 11:5‑6)

John deliberately connects the love and the delay. Jesus loved them, so He stayed. The delay served a purpose the sisters could not see. This story reveals that Christ’s love can be fully at work while His timing wounds those who love Him.

When Jesus reached the tomb, He wept (John 11:35). His purpose did not make grief unnecessary; divine providence and human sorrow met in His tears. The nurse in the hallway had the Gospel on her side. Grief expresses love, and hope does not require a dry face.

Silence therefore cannot suspend the love God has revealed in Christ. The question left to us is what grace does during the two days, or two years, or twenty years of waiting. We may not see the whole answer, but we can begin by looking closer than the sky, at the quiet work taking place within the person who waits.
