Appendix

A Simple Way to Pray

✦ ✦ ✦

≈ 4 min read

PEOPLE OFTEN ASK WHAT they should actually do when they pray tomorrow morning. No method can force an answer from God, and the following practice is not a rule. It is a simple pattern that has helped me remain honest and attentive. Use what helps, adapt it to your responsibilities, and leave the rest without guilt.

Begin with the truth. Tell God what you want in plain language. If you need money, name the need. If you fear a diagnosis, use the doctor’s word. If the desire embarrasses you, confess the embarrassment too. The Father already knows what you need, but naming it brings the desire into conscious relationship with Him. What remains hidden can govern us without examination.

Ask boldly in Jesus’ name. To pray in His name is more than adding a phrase to the end. It is to approach the Father through Christ and to ask as someone who belongs to Him. Bring the whole request without trying to protect yourself from disappointment by pretending not to care.

Then entrust the outcome to God. Ask that His name be honored and His Kingdom served. Surrender does not cancel the request. It places the request within confidence in the Father’s wisdom. Boldness without surrender can become demand. Surrender without honest asking can become emotional self‑protection. Christian petition includes both.

I also pray at regular times, usually morning and evening, and at midday when possible. Scripture gives many examples of ordered prayer:

Evening and morning and at noon
I will pray, and cry aloud,
And He shall hear my voice. (Psalm 55:17 NKJV)

Daniel prayed three times each day even when the practice placed him in danger. He “knelt down on his knees three times that day, and prayed and gave thanks before his God, as was his custom since early days” (Daniel 6:10). The custom existed before the crisis and supported him within it.

Regular times give prayer a home in the day. They settle a decision that mood should not control. Structure can become empty, but the absence of structure can make prayer depend entirely on inspiration. We need both form and attention. Go to prayer when feeling is strong and when feeling is absent. God remains worthy of address in either condition.

Repetition can also serve love. Jesus warns against “vain repetitions” (Matthew 6:7), words multiplied as though length could manipulate a reluctant deity. He does not forbid returning faithfully to the same prayer. Scripture itself contains repeated prayers, refrains, and petitions. A short sentence spoken sincerely each day may deepen as life gathers inside it.

The body matters too. If sitting makes you sleepy, walk. If illness requires you to lie down, pray there. Kneeling can express reverence, but no posture makes prayer valid. One of the simplest prayers can accompany ordinary work: “Jesus, I love You.”

At times, one request becomes obsessive. I may then entrust it to God and choose not to repeat it for a while. This is a personal discipline, not a biblical command and never a reason to neglect urgent action or another person’s safety. The pause concerns repetitive mental possession of an outcome. I may pray:

Lord, You have heard this one many times, and I have presented it to You. I am going to leave it with You for a while and stop bringing it up, so that my heart does not become obsessed with the outcome, or a servant of the result I hope for.

After days or weeks, I may take up the request again. Often the pause reveals whether I have been trusting God or trying to control the result. Persistent prayer and temporary rest can both be faithful when each arises from love rather than fear.

Prayer also belongs to the Church. Tell a trusted Christian what you are carrying and ask that person to pray with you. Jesus promises His presence where two or three gather in His name (Matthew 18:20). Shared prayer does not make God more willing, but it allows members of Christ’s body to bear one another’s burdens.

End within the prayer Jesus taught: “Your will be done” (Matthew 6:10). These words are not a waiver added to protect us from disappointment. They are the mature act of trust that follows honest petition. We hand God the outcome, timing, and interpretation while remaining free to lament what we do not understand.

Tomorrow morning, bring God the prayer you carried through this book. Use ordinary words. Ask for what you truly desire. Ask in the name of Jesus, give thanks for grace already received, and entrust the result to the Father. Return in the evening and again the next day. When attention fails, begin again without drama. Prayer is sustained less by perfect concentration than by the faithfulness of God, who hears.
