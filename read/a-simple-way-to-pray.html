Appendix

A Simple Way to Pray

✦ ✦ ✦

≈ 4 min read

PEOPLE OFTEN ASK WHAT they should actually do when they pray tomorrow morning. No method can force an answer from God. The practice that follows is only a pattern that has kept me honest and attentive, never a rule. Use what helps, fit it to your own life, and leave the rest without guilt.

I begin with the truth. I tell God what I want in plain language. If I need money, I name the need, and if I fear a diagnosis, I use the doctor’s word for it. When a desire embarrasses me, I confess the embarrassment too. The Father already knows what I need, but naming it brings the desire into conscious relationship with Him, and what stays hidden can govern us without examination.

Then I ask boldly in Jesus’ name. To pray in His name is more than adding a phrase at the end. It means approaching the Father through Christ and asking as someone who belongs to Him. I bring the whole request, without trying to protect myself from disappointment by pretending not to care.

Then I entrust the outcome to God, asking that His name be honored and His Kingdom served. Even so, the request still stands, now set within confidence in the Father’s wisdom. Boldness without surrender can harden into demand, and surrender without honest asking can be a way of protecting my own feelings. Christian petition includes both.

I also pray at regular times, usually morning and evening, and at midday when possible. Scripture gives many examples of ordered prayer:

Evening and morning and at noon
I utter my complaint and moan,
and he will hear my voice. (Psalm 55:17)

Daniel prayed three times each day even when the practice placed him in danger. He “got down upon his knees three times a day and prayed and gave thanks before his God, as he had done previously” (Daniel 6:10). The custom was in place before the crisis, and it held him steady inside it.

Regular times give prayer a home in the day. They settle in advance a decision that mood should not control. Structure can become empty, it is true, but prayer without any structure depends entirely on inspiration. We need both form and attention. Go to prayer when feeling is strong and go when it is not, for God is worthy of it either way.

Repetition can also serve love. Jesus warns against “empty phrases” (Matthew 6:7), words multiplied as though length could manipulate a reluctant deity, yet He does not forbid returning faithfully to the same prayer; Scripture itself contains repeated prayers, refrains, and petitions. A short sentence spoken sincerely each day may come to mean more as the years pass, because more of your life is in it.

The body matters too. When sitting makes me sleepy, I walk, and when illness has required it, I have prayed lying down. Kneeling can express reverence, but no posture makes prayer valid. One of the simplest prayers I know can accompany ordinary work: “Jesus, I love You.”

At times one request becomes obsessive, and then I may entrust it to God and choose not to repeat it for a while. This is a personal discipline, not a biblical command, and it never excuses me from acting when action is needed. The pause is about the way one request can take over my mind. I may pray:

Lord, You have heard this one many times, and I have presented it to You. I am going to leave it with You for a while and stop bringing it up, so that my heart does not become obsessed with the outcome, or a servant of the result I hope for.

After days or weeks I may take the request up again, and often the pause has shown me whether I was trusting God or trying to control the result. Persistent prayer can be faithful, and so can a temporary rest. The question is whether I am resting in God or merely afraid to ask again.

Prayer also belongs to the whole church, so I try not to carry a prayer entirely alone. I tell a trusted Christian what I am carrying and ask them to pray with me. Jesus promises His presence where two or three gather in His name (Matthew 18:20). Shared prayer does not make God more willing to hear, for He is willing already. It lets the members of Christ’s body bear one another’s burdens.

I end within the prayer Jesus taught: “Thy will be done” (Matthew 6:10). These words are the mature act of trust that follows honest petition. They are not an escape clause prepared in case the answer is no. We hand God the outcome, the timing, and the interpretation, and we stay free to lament what we do not understand.

That is the whole of it. In the morning I bring God the prayer I am carrying, in ordinary words. I ask for what I truly desire, ask it in the name of Jesus, give thanks for grace already received, and entrust the result to the Father. I return in the evening and again the next day, and when attention fails, I begin again without drama. Prayer is sustained less by perfect concentration than by the faithfulness of God, who hears. Start tomorrow morning and see.
