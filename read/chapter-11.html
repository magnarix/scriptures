Chapter Eleven

The Eleventh Hour

✦ ✦ ✦

≈ 13 min read

PERSISTENCE IN PRAYER MEANS refusing to give up. It does not wear God down, and it stores up no claim on God. It guarantees nothing about our timetable, but it keeps desire open before the Father. An answer that arrives late loses nothing of its eternal fullness.

I knew a woman who prayed thirty‑eight years for her husband’s salvation, beginning as a young wife and growing old carrying the same request. The answer came during the final week of his life. Thirty‑eight years is a long procession of ordinary mornings; she missed some of them, but she returned often enough for the prayer to shape a lifetime.

For most of those years there was nothing remarkable about her persistence. Most days brought no sign and no consolation, and she simply placed one name before God again. The greatness of such prayer lies in repeated fidelity more than in any intensity.

This matters because many of us cannot sustain dramatic fervor for decades. Christian perseverance asks us simply to keep returning. It does not require constant emotional force. A tired sentence offered again can carry more trust than a moment of spiritual excitement.

✦ ✦ ✦

LUKE TELLS US WHY Jesus gave the parable: “And he told them a parable, to the effect that they ought always to pray and not lose heart” (Luke 18:1). The story is His answer to losing heart:

“In a certain city there was a judge who neither feared God nor regarded man; and there was a widow in that city who kept coming to him and saying, ‘Vindicate me against my adversary.’ For a while he refused; but afterward he said to himself, ‘Though I neither fear God nor regard man, yet because this widow bothers me, I will vindicate her, or she will wear me out by her continual coming.’” (Luke 18:2‑5)

The judge fears neither God nor public opinion; conscience and shame cannot move him. The widow has no social standing or wealth with which to influence the court. She possesses only her claim, her feet, and her voice.

She keeps returning until the judge grants justice to make her stop. The phrase translated “wear me out” uses vivid language associated with being struck beneath the eye, and Jesus gives the scene its humor: a powerful man begins fearing the arrival of a socially powerless widow.

And the Lord said, “Hear what the unrighteous judge says. And will not God vindicate his elect, who cry to him day and night? Will he delay long over them? I tell you, he will vindicate them speedily. Nevertheless, when the Son of man comes, will he find faith on earth?” (Luke 18:6‑8)

This parable is easily misread, as though God were a slower version of the judge and persistence a siege against heaven. Jesus argues by contrast. If an unjust judge finally acts, how much more will the just and loving God hear His people?

The judge needs pressure because he lacks love and justice, and God lacks neither. Jesus acknowledges that His people cry “day and night” and experience delay, but the delay comes from One whose character is the opposite of the judge’s. That is why persistence should never rest on divine reluctance.

Here is the thing: if God is already willing, repetition cannot make Him more so. What it changes is the life of the person praying. Luke names loss of heart at the beginning, and Jesus asks at the end whether the Son of Man will find faith on earth. God’s justice is certain, and the only open question is whether our faith will persevere.

When we keep asking, we join a prayer Christ is already praying, and we cooperate with providence. The asking keeps desire open to grace, and it leaves intact the freedom of the person for whom we pray. Repetition works on our side of the relationship, though God also uses our prayer within purposes we cannot map.

Christ’s intercession gives ours its foundation. The risen Lord carries His people before the Father, and the Spirit helps their weakness. We do not keep prayer alive by private strength alone. Our persistence enters a prayer already being sustained within the life of the Trinity.

This makes intercession both humble and bold. We cannot enter another person’s soul or force repentance or arrange providence, but we can ask the Father to give light, conviction, protection, and every grace that assists freedom toward the good. Love asks without trying to possess.

God’s action and human freedom are never competitors. Grace awakens freedom, heals it, and strengthens it so that a real human Yes becomes possible, and persistent prayer trusts God to work that intimately without turning the person into an object.

Long prayer keeps a specific request alive and can keep the heart tender. Resignation often begins by making the petition vague, so that “Bring this person to faith” becomes “Be with him.” The safer words ask for nothing we could recognize and therefore risk no disappointment. Eventually even they disappear.

The widow refuses that anesthesia. She continues naming justice because injustice must not become the final word. There is no technique in this. Through the delay, her faith simply keeps telling the truth about what it wants.

I have buried prayers and called the burial surrender. Real surrender places the request in God’s hands without conditions or deadlines, while resignation withdraws the request to avoid pain, and the two can be hard to tell apart because fatigue can speak reverently.

Some prayers should change as wisdom and circumstances change; persistence is fidelity to God, and it does not require stubborn attachment to every first formulation. Yet the passing years alone should not persuade us that a good desire no longer belongs in His presence.

Discernment asks whether the desire still serves love, truth, justice, and the good of the person concerned. A prayer for reconciliation may mature into a prayer that includes repentance, restitution, and safe boundaries. The heart of the request stays while its understanding grows wiser.

This maturity differs from retreat, which removes specificity to escape disappointment. Mature persistence becomes more truthful about what genuine healing would require, and it may ask for a harder and holier answer than the one first imagined.

✦ ✦ ✦

MATTHEW GIVES A LIVING example in the Canaanite woman. She cried to Jesus for her tormented daughter, and “he did not answer her a word” (Matthew 15:23). She continued. Jesus replied, “I was sent only to the lost sheep of the house of Israel” (Matthew 15:24). She knelt and asked again.

He said that children’s bread should not be thrown to the little dogs. She answered within His image: “Yes, Lord, yet even the dogs eat the crumbs that fall from their masters’ table” (Matthew 15:27). Jesus replied, “O woman, great is your faith! Be it done for you as you desire” (Matthew 15:28).

This passage troubled me from childhood because I heard the image as exclusion. The woman heard that the bread was within reach, and she trusted that even a crumb of Christ’s mercy was sufficient for her daughter.

The exchange must be read within the Gospel’s movement from Israel toward the nations. Jesus reveals the woman’s faith publicly and grants the request. Her persistence draws out the expansive mercy already present in His mission and anticipates the welcome of the nations; there was no prejudice in Christ for it to overcome.

She stays before Jesus through silence and a hard saying, and her humble answer is also intelligent and bold. She does not argue that she deserves a place at the table. She simply trusts that the One who provides the bread has more than enough.

Her faith is not mechanical, because she is dealing with a person. She repeats no formula and presents no spiritual qualification, but responds to Jesus Himself and refuses to interpret His silence as the end of the encounter.

The mother’s love also gives persistence its urgency, for she is asking for another person’s liberation, with no advantage in it for herself. Intercession carries someone else’s need into the presence of Christ and keeps it there when the sufferer cannot pray.

Jesus’ praise reveals that persistence can survive misunderstanding. She does not possess a complete account of His mission, yet she recognizes mercy, and faith often knows the character of Christ before it understands the path He is taking.

Jesus taught persistence more directly after His disciples asked how to pray:

“And I tell you, Ask, and it will be given you; seek, and you will find; knock, and it will be opened to you. For every one who asks receives, and he who seeks finds, and to him who knocks it will be opened.” (Luke 11:9‑10)

The verbs carry continuing force, telling us to keep asking, seeking, and knocking. The images build as they go. Asking is a word, seeking is a search, and knocking means standing at a door that has not opened.

Jesus promises that the one who asks receives, though He leaves the timing unspecified and does not say the gift will match every imagined form. The surrounding teaching identifies the Father as the giver of good gifts and of the Holy Spirit. Persistence trusts His goodness while the door is still closed.

Christian history gives many witnesses. Monica saw her son’s conversion shortly before her death. George Müller is said to have died before seeing the final answers to prayers he had carried for decades.

These stories differ from Scripture in authority, since they illustrate doctrine without establishing it, and we receive them critically, aware that retelling can simplify chronology or sharpen details. Their value lies in embodied witness to truths already given in the Gospel.

They also prevent persistence from becoming abstract. A long prayer has to outlast moves, illness, disappointment, and ordinary duty; it survives only by being woven into a real human life.

✦ ✦ ✦

SIXTEEN CENTURIES AGO, MONICA watched her gifted son reject her faith; we know the story because Augustine later wrote it as a confession addressed to God. He pursued rhetoric, ambition, a long relationship outside marriage, and a sect that flattered his intellect. Monica continued praying after argument could no longer reach him.

Augustine remembered that she wept over his spiritual wandering with the grief of a bereaved mother. For nearly two decades, she carried his name before God while his success and confidence in another path increased.

Monica asked a bishop to argue with Augustine, and he declined, because the young man still loved novelty and debate; argument would give him another contest to win. The bishop advised her to continue praying and trust that Augustine’s reading would expose his error.

She kept pressing until the bishop dismissed her with the assurance traditionally rendered “the son of these tears” could not perish. Augustine says she received the words as though they came from heaven, though the sentence was pastoral encouragement rather than a universal promise about every praying parent.

The following years did not resemble fulfillment. Augustine sailed abroad for his career after deceiving his mother about his departure, and Monica spent the night near the shore, praying that the voyage be stopped. The ship sailed.

Looking back, Augustine saw that the refused request served the larger one, for the voyage carried him toward the place where his conversion would occur. Monica could not have known this while grieving on the shore. Persistent prayer often contains days when one form of the request fails and providence stays hidden.

Augustine’s conversion came while he wept in a garden and heard a childlike voice repeating, “take up and read.” He opened Paul’s letters and encountered a passage that ended his resistance. He told Monica. Within months, as she approached death, she said the one earthly hope that had held her was fulfilled.

The Confessions became a thanksgiving to God in which Monica’s tears hold an honored place. Her prayer did not cause conversion mechanically or override Augustine’s freedom. Grace worked through Scripture, friendship, preaching, thought, suffering, and his free consent, and her intercession was one part of that providential whole.

Monica’s persistence also underwent purification. She sometimes prayed for a particular means, such as stopping a voyage, while God served the deeper desire through another route. Long intercession teaches us to distinguish the person we love from the plan we have designed for that person.

Her tears were simply her love, laid open before God. The answer came from grace, which gathered her sorrow into a providence broader than she could see from any one day.

George Müller carried some prayers beyond what he could see. During his long work caring for orphans, he recorded needs and answers with unusual care. In an account written after thirty‑six years of prayer, he said that three of five people had been converted and two had not. He continued praying for them. Later biographical retellings agree that those two conversions came, but they differ about their precise timing in relation to his death.

The story should be received as transmitted testimony rather than a mathematical promise, but its central witness is strong. Müller continued interceding without requiring personal sight of the result.

That freedom from sight is a form of theological hope, which rests in God’s future and does not depend on what we can already see. Hope entrusts the good request to God and remains faithful even when no result is visible. It offers no guarantee that every named person will respond as desired.

Müller’s written records also show that memory serves persistence, because human beings forget both requests and answers. Writing can help us notice patterns of providence, give thanks, revise mistaken interpretations, and recognize petitions still open.

Prayer does not expire when the person praying dies, for God remembers the communion in which the petition was offered. Some answers may unfold in rooms we never enter and among people who never learn who prayed.

This gives us no control beyond death. We hand the request over to God, and it may enter a providence larger than one lifespan. Müller kept praying anyway.

Jesus told of laborers hired at the eleventh hour who received a full day’s wage. The parable chiefly reveals the generosity of God’s Kingdom, but it also helps us see that grace which comes late is not a lesser grace. A conversion at life’s end receives the fullness of Christ, though lost years may still carry real consequences.

Some promises and prayers unfold across generations. Abraham died after seeing one son of promise; the nation came later. Intercession belongs to the whole fellowship of God’s people and is not confined to one person’s visible lifetime.

Prayer is not a spiritual asset automatically transferred to descendants; each person stands free before grace. Yet a life of intercession leaves a real inheritance of desires shaped toward God, habits of mercy, remembered names, and petitions entrusted to divine faithfulness.

The fellowship of God’s people reaches across generations through worship, witness, teaching, and love. We receive prayers begun before us and add our own; no generation prays alone or begins the Christian life from nothing.

This shared intercession also guards against private possession, for the person for whom I pray belongs first to God, and others may carry the request after my strength ends. The church goes on praying long after my attention fails, and it will go on after I am gone.

✦ ✦ ✦

RETURN TO THE WOMAN who prayed thirty‑eight years. The answer came with one week left, and measured by eternity, that final week holds full room for salvation. The man crucified beside Jesus had only hours and received Christ’s promise of paradise.

I will not decorate the husband’s final week with details. Some I never knew, and the rest belong to his family. The important fact is that grace arrived fully enough for the prayer to be recognized as answered.

The repeated prayer also formed the wife. Naming her husband before God made complete indifference more difficult, and intercession held open a door within her through seasons when disappointment might have closed it.

This does not mean she never felt anger, fatigue, or hurt. Persistence can live alongside boundaries, honesty, and a refusal to enable harm, and prayer kept her asking for his eternal good without requiring her to pretend that every part of the marriage was well.

Her decades likely felt ordinary: meals, worship, disappointment, and familiar words before sleep. When the answer came, it entered a home where mercy had long been practiced by the person asking heaven to give it.

Persistence formed patience without making her passive. She could continue ordinary responsibilities while asking for a change she could not produce, and this is one of prayer’s quiet freedoms. It allows us to act where action is possible and entrust what lies beyond our reach.

The practice of mercy also prepared her to receive the answer as gift. Had resentment become her only relation to her husband, even his conversion might have arrived inside a closed house. Intercession kept some capacity for welcome alive.

Persistent prayer eventually questions the one who prays. Do we ask God to send help while standing carefully outside the need ourselves? Keep asking, and the petition begins to reveal acts of mercy, courage, generosity, or repair that we could offer.

This does not mean every intercessor is personally able or called to solve the problem, for limits, safety, vocation, and wisdom all matter. Yet sometimes God’s first visible movement is the grace that makes us willing to become part of the answer.
