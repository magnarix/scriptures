Part I · The Asking

Chapter One

God Is Not a Vending Machine

✦ ✦ ✦

≈ 14 min read

FOR YEARS I ASSUMED prayer was mainly a way to get things from God. I would have denied it if anyone asked, but my habits told the truth. I prayed when I needed something and judged the prayer by the result. A favorable answer proved that my faith was strong, and a refusal sent me looking for a fault in my conduct, my words, or my technique. Many believers live by the same assumption without ever stating it.

Prayer begins elsewhere. It is communion with God, and we ask for what we need inside that communion, as children speaking to their Father. When the request becomes the purpose and God becomes the means, prayer slowly collapses. We may keep saying religious words, but the relationship has been reduced to an exchange.

A vending machine shows the difference. You insert the right amount, press the right code, and expect the product to fall. No relationship is involved. You do not thank the machine or stay beside it for company, and when it takes your money and gives nothing back, you feel cheated. Some people slap the glass. This has never improved a machine, though each generation continues the experiment.

That machine was once my unspoken picture of God. Faith supplied the coins, Scripture supplied the codes, and God stood behind the glass, expected to deliver. I never thought I was treating Him impersonally. As far as I could see, I was taking His promises seriously.

✦ ✦ ✦

I FIRST TESTED THIS theology as a boy. One night I had unfinished schoolwork due the next morning; half was done, and the rest had defeated me. I placed a cross on top of the pages and prayed, “Jesus, I want you to finish my work. I’m going to shower now, and when I return, I should see the work completed.” Then I left Him alone to work. I even gave Him privacy.

I returned clean and hopeful, and the papers lay exactly as I had left them. I laugh at that boy, but I recognize him. His prayer was my later theology spoken without adult manners. He believed divine power created a debt. Because Jesus could finish the work, Jesus should finish it. He was demanding a gift, and a gift that has to be demanded is really a wage. God owed him nothing. Yet God used that foolish prayer to begin teaching him what grace means.

The boy grew older, and the same error became more respectable. For years I asked God to make me as rich as the wealthiest man alive, and I had already prepared a spiritual defense of the request. I would use the fortune for the Kingdom, wealth would bring influence, influence would draw crowds to the Gospel, and I could build God a beautiful church. I even worried that poverty among those who preached Christ might discourage people from believing.

The plan sounded noble from inside my own head. I had arranged the sequence for God: fortune, influence, church, conversions. The Almighty seemed to be waiting for my financial strategy. I have since heard versions of that prayer from many people, because ambition is hard to recognize once we have taught it to talk about serving God.

God did not rebuke me. He simply refused the plan, and year after year nothing happened. Scripture eventually showed me that His refusal was mercy.

✦ ✦ ✦

JESUS TOLD OF A rich man whose land produced more than he could store (Luke 12:16‑21). The man decided to build larger barns, then addressed his own soul and promised it ease for many years. Scripture uses “soul” here for the man’s whole life, and he treated that life as another possession he could store, secure, and spend.

God called him a fool. In Psalm 14, the fool lives as though God were absent, whatever his mouth may profess, and the rich man had planned for every year except eternity. That very night his soul was required of him, with the barns full and nothing in his hands. He had stored treasure for himself and had never become rich toward God.

The parable exposed my own barns. I planned to build mine for God, which made the delusion harder to see. Yet the measures were still worldly: influence counted in crowds, worth counted in money, and the Kingdom advanced by the same power that advances every earthly enterprise. I had written a business plan and asked God to sign it.

His refusal spared me from the man I wanted to become. God often works through what the world considers weak, so that no one mistakes the source of the power, and He works upon His servants while He works through them. I asked for resources that would make me effective, and all the while He was working to make me faithful. I had been created for glory, and that glory is to share, by grace, in the life of God (cf. 2 Peter 1:4). Gold decorates a life without ever giving it eternal weight. If God had granted my plan, the Kingdom might have received very little from me, and God Himself less.

My prayer for wealth was one form of a common error. We make the petition the center and judge God by what happens next. Disappointment then becomes spiritually dangerous, for enough refusals can make a person walk away from God when the only relationship he knew was the exchange.

Love has a different order. It keeps no accounts. It gives without asking what the gift will earn. Petition belongs within that love. I bring my needs to God every day, sometimes with great urgency, yet the requests live inside the relationship, and the relationship does not stand or fall on their being granted.

This distinction is also the difference between childishness and childhood. A small child may forget the giver as soon as he sees the wrapped gift, while a mature child receives the gift as an expression of the giver’s love. God calls us to be children before Him, bold enough to ask and secure enough to stay when the answer hurts. What He does not want is the childishness that cares only for what He places in our hands.

Communion means more than feeling near to God. By grace we come to share in the Son’s life with the Father through the Holy Spirit. We do not approach the Trinity as strangers trying to attract attention, for in Christ we receive the Spirit, who teaches our hearts to cry, “Abba, Father.” Prayer begins inside a gift we did not create and cannot buy. A child belongs to his father before he ever says a word.

This is why Christian prayer is always larger than the person praying. Jesus Christ is the eternal Son who receives everything from the Father and returns everything in love, and when we are joined to Him, His life with the Father becomes our own. We remain creatures, wholly dependent upon grace, yet we are welcomed into the Son’s communion. We pray to the Father because Christ has brought us near, and through the Son because His obedience carries ours. We pray in the Holy Spirit because even our first movement toward God is already the Spirit at work within us.

The Trinity is therefore the living shape of prayer. The Father is its source and end, the Son gives us access and teaches us the trust of children, and the Spirit dwells within us and makes our words more than a cry thrown into the distance. This is a far richer reality than answered requests. A request may be refused while this communion deepens. Circumstances may not move at all, and grace still draws the life further into the love of God. The prayer has then entered the very purpose for which we were created.

I have sat with people who walked away after years of disappointment. Their unbelief is often painfully personal. They say they no longer believe in God, then describe everything He failed to do: the healing He withheld, the prayer He seemed to ignore, the years they believe they wasted. No one feels betrayed by an empty room.

Often a false contract has died. Someone taught them that faith would produce an outcome, and when the outcome failed, they treated God as we treat a machine that keeps the money. Then the accuser supplied his explanation. God ignored you, lacked the power, or did not care. Transactional prayer leaves the wounded heart open to that lie because a broken exchange seems to allow no other explanation.

God never signed such a contract. I say this as a man who once lived by it. I have known faithful believers who denied themselves, carried their cross, and followed Christ without receiving anything they would call extraordinary. They had no visions or windfalls, no vivid dreams to report. Transactional religion labels them failures, and I have seen the shame of that judgment settle on some of the most faithful faces I know.

Christian salvation is larger than the recovery of health, wealth, or the life we lost. God restores His image in us and conforms us to Christ, and every other gift serves that work. The deepest blessing is the person grace makes of us in communion with Christ. Once we understand this, circumstances lose their power to decide whether God has been good.

Jesus addressed this error before teaching His disciples the words of the Lord’s Prayer. He first told them who was listening:

Do not be like them, for your Father knows what you need before you ask him. (Matthew 6:8)

This sentence removes the basis of every transaction. We do not pray to inform God, who already knows, and we do not pray to overcome His reluctance, for the Father knew our need before we felt it. Jesus leaves one reality standing after every technique has fallen away: “your Father.” Prayer begins with the Person who loved and knew us first.

Augustine answered the obvious question sixteen centuries ago, when a widow named Proba asked him how she should pray. If God already knows, why ask? Augustine explained that God does not need our information; He commands us to ask because prayer enlarges our desire, stretching the soul until it can receive more fully what God is preparing to give.

Prayer therefore begins its work within the person who prays. This does not reduce prayer to self‑improvement, for God Himself acts in the encounter. Grace awakens desire, purifies it, and makes room for the gift. Proba asked for a method, and Augustine pointed her toward communion; her asking drew her into the company of the One she sought.

Desire needs this work because we rarely want only one thing. My prayer for wealth contained a desire to serve God, a desire to be admired, a fear of insignificance, and a wish to control the future. I could not separate them. God could. His delay allowed the false desires to show themselves while preserving what was true. Prayer placed the whole confused heart before Him, and grace patiently sorted what I could not.

This helps explain why some answers take a form we did not request. God loves the person who asks, more deeply than that person understands himself, and He sees the eternal capacity hidden inside a temporary desire. He may give the object, withhold it, or make us wait; in every case His purpose reaches beyond the object toward the soul that will receive it. Augustine keeps us from two mistakes. Our desires are not worthless, and they are not yet pure. God honors desire enough to transform it.

The old writers called prayer the breath of the Christian, and the image is exact. A person can study breathing, discuss it, and admire a diagram of the lungs while still suffocating. Air gives life only when it is drawn in. In the same way, we can think and speak about God for years without speaking to Him.

I say this with some shame. A clergyman can handle holy things all day and never turn his heart toward God. Communion may sound like an achievement for mystics, but usually it is much plainer: being with God, staying with Him, returning when the mind has wandered. Some evenings it means sitting before Him with nothing to report and no request to file. We remain because He is there.

✦ ✦ ✦

YEARS INTO THIS CONVERSION from transaction to communion, a man who had known me a long time asked about my praying. He had seen me rise early and return to God through many seasons, and he knew that many things I had requested had never happened. His question carried no malice; he simply said aloud what others were too polite to ask.

“Why do you keep at all this?” he said. “Does it work? Do you get your prayers answered?”

“No,” I said. “I just want to commune with my God.”

He looked as though I had answered a different question, but I had answered the one that mattered. Somewhere through the years, prayer’s success rate had stopped being my concern, and I had not noticed the change until I heard myself speak.

The young man who asked to be as rich as the wealthiest man alive could never have given that answer; he would have produced testimonies, defended prayer’s effectiveness, and explained every delay. I still bring God my petitions. I have never stopped. Yet the one gift present in every faithful prayer is God Himself. His will may comfort me or cut across mine, but His presence does not depend upon the result. A granted request and a painful refusal stand within the same love of Christ.

There is a verse in the Psalms that shows this conversion from the inside:

Thou hast said, “Seek ye my face.” My heart says to thee, “Thy face, Lord, do I seek.” (Psalm 27:8)

God speaks first. David’s seeking answers an invitation already given, and grace always has this priority. Even our desire for God begins with God drawing us.

David seeks God’s face. Bread answers hunger, and rain answers the need of the fields, but we seek a face for the sake of the person whose face it is. For years I watched God’s hand and waited to see what it would give; a man can live that way for decades and never look toward the face above the hand. In the same psalm, David asks to dwell in the house of the LORD and behold His beauty. He has learned to desire God for God’s own sake.

Communion does not require us to clean our desires before speaking. C.S. Lewis wrote that we must lay before God “what is in us, not what ought to be in us.” My foolish prayer for great wealth did exactly that. The desire was real, so I spoke it. A respectable prayer might have concealed the ambition and allowed it to govern me from the dark.

God received the prayer without granting its terms and separated a good longing from the false form I had given it. I wanted my life to serve His Kingdom, and God has honored that desire in a currency I did not know. He prospers His children in wisdom, love, holiness, courage, and compassion. These riches survive death. The fortune I requested could not.

Transactional prayer often rests its weight on one promise of Jesus. I understand why. Its closing words sound like a divine contract:

If you abide in me, and my words abide in you, ask whatever you will, and it shall be done for you. (John 15:7)

The promise begins with abiding. Jesus describes the shared life of a branch within a vine. The branch keeps no ledger and makes no independent life for itself; sap and fruit come through union with the vine.

“Abide in Me,” Jesus says. Let My words dwell in you. Then ask what you desire. A desire that abides in Christ does not come through untouched. His word purifies it and gives it His shape. The promise is complete and trustworthy. It belongs to branches receiving the life of the vine, and it becomes a blank check only after we tear its final clause away from its beginning.

Abiding also changes what we mean by an answer, for the life of the vine is already entering the branch before the fruit appears, and hidden communion precedes the visible result. We often look only for fruit because it can be counted, displayed, and compared, but Christ begins with union. A life rooted in Him may appear unproductive for a season while grace is doing its deepest work beyond our sight.

The branch does not disappear into the vine. It remains itself, alive and bearing fruit. In the same way, communion with Christ heals human desire and freedom instead of erasing them. Prayer becomes more personal as it becomes more surrendered, because grace restores the person God intended. We begin by asking Christ to support our plans, and we mature as His life within us teaches us to desire the Father’s will as our good.

Communion does not make petition unnecessary. For a time, I tried to want nothing so that nothing could be refused. I told myself this was maturity, but I was only protecting myself from another refusal.

Scripture would not permit it. Jesus said that the Father knows our needs before we ask, and then He taught us to ask for bread, forgiveness, and deliverance, the plain needs of daily life. The Father knows, and the Father still commands us to ask. Our requests allow us to live consciously as children who receive, and they reveal a tenderness in God. He delights in hearing the children He already knows and loves.
