Prologue

The Vow

✦ ✦ ✦

≈ 6 min read

THIS BOOK BEGAN AS a promise I made to God in the worst year of my life.

In July 2008, during the Great Recession, I lost my job. I expected to find another one quickly. I did not. Soon the phone was ringing every five minutes. Creditors called. Collection agencies called. The mortgage company warned of foreclosure, and credit card companies threatened to sue. At night I lay awake doing sums that never came out right. In the morning I prayed. By breakfast the phone had begun again.

My prayers in those days were desperate, but desperation was not their deepest problem. I treated prayer as a mechanism. I had been taught that a problem would move if I spoke the name of Jesus with enough faith. So I claimed, declared, and rebuked. The next morning the mountain still stood where I had left it.

That failure exposed the god I had imagined. He was less a Father than the keeper of a spiritual system. If I supplied enough faith, correct words, and moral effort, he would supply the result. I had turned filial trust into leverage and providence into delivery. When the system failed, I blamed myself. My faith must be too weak. My effort must be too small. My sins must be too many. This theology always leaves the wounded person in the same place: before a bookkeeper with a severe face, forever behind on the payments.

Then we lost the house. I had designed it myself, from the ground up, every wall and every window. We signed the papers under duress and drove away. After a year of pleading with God, His answer seemed to be a moving truck.

I saw only what had been taken. God was already teaching me to see how faithfully I was being held.

The disasters I rehearsed at night never came. No one sued us. We were never homeless. We did not go hungry. Near the bottom, I found a can of chicken noodle soup marked down to ninety‑nine cents. I bought it, and God fed me that day. I have eaten finer meals since and forgotten almost all of them. I remember that can.

Daily bread means what Jesus taught us to say: bread for this day. We prefer enough for the year because stored provision gives us the feeling of control. God sometimes gives one day at a time because trust can live only in the present. People who hear this story often ask what happened the next day. A woman from the church brought what she could find in her refrigerator: a can of soup, a potato, and more. I had thanked God for what I feared would be my last meal. Morning came, and I did not have to beg. “I have been young, and now am old,” the psalm says, “Yet I have not seen the righteous forsaken, Nor his descendants begging bread” (Psalm 37:25).

God was also using the loss to uncover what I loved. One question finally broke through my self‑pity: why did the house matter so much when God was feeding me every day? I needed years to face the answer. I had trusted the house, the salary, and the image of myself as a man who could manage his own life. Much of my praying asked God to protect my grip on those things. I called it faith. Often it was fear asking for religious permission to remain in control.

God’s providence went deeper than preserving my circumstances. He was freeing me from the lie that my circumstances could preserve me. He opened my clenched hand one finger at a time. Each opening felt like the refusal of a prayer. Only later did I understand it as mercy.

During that dark year, I made God a promise. One question would not leave me alone: why does God allow those who trust Him to suffer? I promised that if He carried me through, I would write what the darkness taught me about answered prayer. I could not write a victory story. The job and the house were gone. I could only testify that God had met me where victory, as I understood it, had failed.

This book keeps that vow, many years late. God has been patient in collecting it.

I have no method that makes God act, and I distrust anyone who offers one. In many years as a clergyman, I have sat with people whom such methods quietly crushed. They come ashamed. They assume that a prayer failed because their faith was thin, their effort poor, or their life unworthy. If you carry that shame, hear me plainly: prayer is not a test you failed. The weakness you confess may be real, but it does not explain why God has refused to be controlled.

I will not try to prove that prayer “works.” That language is too small. Machines work. Techniques work. Prayer is the life of a child with the Father, through the Son, in the Holy Spirit. Petition belongs inside that communion. Once we remove it from communion, prayer becomes an attempt to manage God with sacred language.

What I can offer is what a clergyman has: Scripture, stories, and scars. I still do not receive everything I ask for. I do not always know God’s will. Anyone who claims constant certainty and predictable results is describing a fantasy of Christian life. Yet for more than three decades I have watched God answer prayer. Sometimes He gives exactly what was asked. Often He gives what no one knew to ask for. His answer always serves a purpose deeper than the request, because His purpose is to bring us into the life He gives. This book will make that case one chapter at a time.

Under that conviction sits one verse that has carried me longer than any other:

This poor man cried out, and the LORD heard him, And saved him out of all his troubles. (Psalm 34:6)

The order matters. A poor man cries because he has nothing left with which to bargain. That was my condition in 2008. It is also the true condition of everyone who prays, whether we recognize it or not. Then God hears. Then He saves. The psalm says nothing about the manner or the timing of the rescue. It tells us that God saved the man; it never promises that salvation looked like rescue while it was happening. I have been that poor man. Often the hand that saves us is clear only after it has carried us out.

Bring your own prayer into these pages. You probably knew which one I meant before finishing that sentence: the healing that has not come, the marriage that has not mended, the child who has not come home, the door that remains closed. Do not hide it out of politeness toward God. We will begin with asking, because Christian surrender never makes desire dishonest. Then we will enter the waiting: silence, delay, disappointment, and the thorn that stays. We will look at the answers God gives, including those we recognize only after they have changed us. Finally, we will look toward the Giver Himself. Every gift of grace comes from Him and leads us further into His life.

“The LORD gave, and the LORD has taken away; Blessed be the name of the LORD” (Job 1:21). Job could speak both halves in one breath because the same God remained present in both. I want these pages to help you know that presence when your own prayer seems to have reached only silence.

We begin with the confusion that shaped half my life: I knew how to ask God for things, but I had never seriously asked what prayer was for.
