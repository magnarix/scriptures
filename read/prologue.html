Prologue

The Vow

✦ ✦ ✦

≈ 6 min read

THIS BOOK BEGAN AS a promise I made to God in the worst year of my life.

In July 2008, during the Great Recession, I lost my job. I expected to find another one quickly. I did not. Soon the phone was ringing every five minutes: creditors, collection agencies, the mortgage company warning of foreclosure, credit card companies threatening to sue. At night I lay awake running numbers that never came out right. In the morning I prayed, and by breakfast the phone had begun again.

My prayers in those days were desperate, but that was not the worst of it. I treated prayer as a mechanism. I had been taught that a problem would move if I spoke the name of Jesus with enough faith, so I claimed, declared, and rebuked. The next morning the mountain still stood where I had left it.

That failure exposed the god I had imagined, less a Father than the keeper of a spiritual system. If I supplied enough faith, correct words, and moral effort, he would supply the result. I had stopped trusting my Father and started bargaining with Him. When the system failed, I blamed myself. My faith must be too weak, my effort too small, or there was some sin I hadn’t dealt with. That theology turned God into a bookkeeper, and I could never catch up on the payments.

Then we lost the house. I had designed it myself, from the ground up, every wall and every window. We signed the papers under duress and drove away. After a year of pleading with God, His answer seemed to be a moving truck.

At the time all I could see was what we had lost. It took me years to see how we were being carried.

The disasters I rehearsed at night never came. No one sued us, we were never homeless, and we did not go hungry. Near the bottom, I found a can of chicken noodle soup marked down to ninety‑nine cents. I bought it, and God fed me that day. I have eaten finer meals since and forgotten almost all of them. I remember that can.

Daily bread means what Jesus taught us to say, bread for this day. We would rather have enough for the year, since having it stored away feels like control, but God sometimes gives one day at a time, because trust can live only in the present. People who hear this story often ask what happened the next day. A woman from the church brought what she could find in her refrigerator: a can of soup, a potato, and more. I had thanked God for what I feared would be my last meal. Morning came, and I did not have to beg. “I have been young, and now am old,” the psalm says, “yet I have not seen the righteous forsaken or his children begging bread” (Psalm 37:25). I read that verse as my testimony, not as a promise that the righteous never go hungry. Lazarus begged at a rich man’s gate, and God had not forsaken him.

God was also using the loss to uncover what I loved. One question finally broke through my self‑pity. Why did the house matter so much when God was feeding me every day? I needed years to face the answer. I had trusted the house, the salary, and the image of myself as a man who could manage his own life, and much of my praying asked God to protect my grip on those things. I called it faith. Looking back, most of it was fear dressed up in religious language, trying to keep control.

God was doing more than keeping us afloat. He was teaching me that the things I leaned on could not hold me up. He pried my fingers loose one at a time, and every one of those losses felt like a no. Now I believe it was mercy.

During that dark year, I made God a promise. A question would not leave me alone. Why does God allow those who trust Him to suffer? I promised that if He carried me through, I would write what the darkness taught me about answered prayer. I could not write a victory story, because the job and the house were gone. I could only testify that God had met me where victory, as I understood it, had failed.

This book keeps that vow, many years late. God has been patient in collecting it.

I have no method that makes God act, and I distrust anyone who offers one. In many years as a clergyman, I have sat with people those methods quietly crushed. They come ashamed. They assume that a prayer failed because their faith was thin, their effort poor, or their life unworthy. If you carry that shame, hear me plainly. Prayer is not a test you failed. The weakness you confess may be real, but it does not explain why God has refused to be controlled.

I will not try to prove that prayer “works.” That language is too small. Machines work, and so do techniques. Prayer is the life of a child with the Father, through the Son, in the Holy Spirit, and petition belongs inside that communion. Once we remove it, prayer becomes an attempt to manage God with sacred language.

What I can offer is Scripture and my own scars. I still do not receive everything I ask for, and I do not always know God’s will. Anyone who claims constant certainty and predictable results is promising more than Christ ever promised. Yet for more than three decades I have watched God answer prayer. Sometimes He gives exactly what was asked, and often He gives what no one knew to ask for. His answer always serves a purpose deeper than the request, because His purpose is to bring us into the life He gives. This book will make that case one chapter at a time.

Under that conviction sits one verse that has carried me longer than any other:

This poor man cried, and the Lord heard him, and saved him out of all his troubles. (Psalm 34:6)

The order matters. A poor man cries because he has nothing left to bargain with. That was my condition in 2008, and it is the true condition of everyone who prays, whether we recognize it or not. Then God hears, and then He saves. The psalm says nothing about the manner or the timing of the rescue. It tells us that God saved the man. It never promises that salvation looked like rescue while it was happening. I have been that poor man. Often the hand that saves us is clear only after it has carried us out.

Bring your own prayer into these pages. You already know which one it is. Maybe it is the healing that has not come, the marriage, the child who still is not home. Do not hide it out of politeness toward God. We will begin with asking, because surrendering to God does not mean pretending we do not want what we ask for. Then we will enter the waiting, the silence and delay, the disappointment, the thorn that stays. We will look at the answers God gives, including those we recognize only after they have changed us. And finally we will look toward the Giver Himself, for every gift of grace comes from Him and leads us further into His life.

“The Lord gave, and the Lord has taken away; blessed be the name of the Lord” (Job 1:21). Job could speak both halves in one breath because the same God was present in both. I want these pages to help you know that presence when your own prayer seems to have reached only silence.

We begin with the confusion that shaped half my life. I knew how to ask God for things, but I had never seriously asked what prayer was for.
