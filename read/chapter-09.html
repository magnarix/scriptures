Part III · The Answer

Chapter Nine

The Prayer at the Center

✦ ✦ ✦

≈ 13 min read

THIS BOOK RESTS ON two facts. In Gethsemane Jesus asked the Father to let the cup pass, and Scripture says that He was heard, though the cup did not pass. Here is the center of Christian prayer. A refused request can be fully heard within unbroken communion. I know how much that costs to believe beside a dying person.

Gethsemane means “oil press.” The garden lay on the lower slope of the Mount of Olives across the valley from Jerusalem, and olives were crushed there until they released their oil. Jesus went after the Passover meal because prayer in that place was His habit; Judas knew where to find Him. Eight disciples stayed near the entrance, and three went farther and fell asleep. Jesus continued alone.

Matthew writes, “And going a little farther he fell on his face and prayed, ‘My Father, if it be possible, let this cup pass from me; nevertheless, not as I will, but as thou wilt’” (Matthew 26:39). The eternal Son prayed with a fully human mind, heart, and will. He fell on His face and asked that the cup pass. Within hours soldiers entered the garden, and by the next morning He was crucified.

Measured only by the immediate request, the prayer appears unanswered. The sinless Son prayed with perfect trust and pure desire, and He persisted. Every attempt to blame an ungranted prayer on moral or spiritual failure ends here.

The Gospels preserve the scene at the turning point of redemption, and Christian worship has repeated it for centuries. Prayer cannot be defined only by obtaining the requested result, because Christ’s own prayer would expose that definition as false. Any account of answered prayer that cannot survive this garden is wrong.

✦ ✦ ✦

GETHSEMANE BECAME MORE THAN a passage when the man who had been like a father to me died. Family members prayed across time zones; friends prayed, congregations prayed, and strangers fasted. Messages arrived each day. His condition worsened, and he died.

Years as a clergyman gave me language for the pain without reducing it, but that week I still had to open Scripture before a congregation. I told them that we had prayed and that I was grieving, and I admitted what many believers fear saying aloud, that God seemed not to have answered.

One truth kept me standing. Jesus never taught us to pray that our will be done. He taught, “Thy will be done” (Matthew 6:10), and then prayed those words when obedience carried an unimaginable cost. During that week my heart had mostly prayed, “Let my will be done.” I resented the difference for years before I began to receive surrender as mercy.

Surrender does not make human desire sinful or unreal. Christ’s desire to be spared suffering belonged to His sinless human will, and obedience brought that desire into complete filial consent to the Father’s saving will. Christian surrender follows His pattern without pretending that our motives possess His purity.

The distinction between Christ’s human will and His divine will matters. Jesus is one divine Person, the eternal Son, who assumed a complete human nature. That humanity includes a real will capable of choosing human goods such as life, friendship, bodily integrity, and freedom from torture. His desire to avoid the cup reveals the truth of His humanity.

His human will never rebels against God; it experiences the cost of obedience and freely consents, and the prayer allows us to see that consent taking place within anguish. Salvation is accomplished through a human obedience perfectly united to the divine will, an obedience offered on behalf of humanity from within our condition.

This gives Christian surrender its dignity. Grace does not destroy the human will and put divine force in its place. Grace heals freedom so that a person can offer himself in love. Our surrender remains imperfect, mixed, and dependent upon mercy, yet Christ has opened the path of truly human obedience.

✦ ✦ ✦

WHEN I WAS YOUNG, a preacher asked whether we would continue following Jesus if our prayers were never answered as expected. I was immersed in prosperity teaching at the time, and I answered inwardly, “Why follow a god who cannot answer prayer?” Many believers carry a quieter version of the same question.

We can construct a Messiah who keeps the healings, the multiplied bread, the calmed storm, and the raised child while losing the road to Jerusalem. Such a figure bears the name Jesus but differs from the Christ of the Gospels, giving victory without discipleship and resurrection without the Cross.

Jesus walked toward Jerusalem knowingly; Mark says those following were amazed and afraid, and two disciples still asked for places of honor. Jesus answered, “You do not know what you are asking. Are you able to drink the cup that I drink, or to be baptized with the baptism with which I am baptized?” (Mark 10:38).

Discipleship follows Christ and does not direct Him. We go behind the One who passes through suffering into glory. This does not mean God positively wills every harm His people endure, but it does mean no disciple can define divine faithfulness in a way that excludes the path Christ Himself walked.

In the prophets and the psalms the cup can signify judgment and suffering (Isaiah 51:17; Psalm 75:8), and Jesus knew the scriptural weight of His words. He entered the garden knowing what awaited Him and asked the Father, with the trust of the Son, whether another way was possible.

✦ ✦ ✦

LUKE PRESERVES THE BODILY details of the night:

And he withdrew from them about a stone’s throw, and knelt down and prayed, “Father, if thou art willing, remove this cup from me; nevertheless not my will, but thine, be done.” And there appeared to him an angel from heaven, strengthening him. And being in an agony he prayed more earnestly; and his sweat became like great drops of blood falling down upon the ground. (Luke 22:41‑44)

Jesus withdrew only a stone’s throw, knelt, and named the desire directly, asking the Father to remove the cup. The asking and the surrender belong to one prayer; honest desire does not compete with obedience. Mark adds the address “Abba” (Mark 14:36). The most terrible petition in Scripture begins with filial intimacy.

An angel came from heaven and strengthened Him. Heaven answered, though not by moving the cup. The strength enabled Christ, in His humanity, to continue freely along the path of obedience without making the suffering any less real.

The angel’s presence shows that assistance and deliverance are different gifts. We usually ask for the second, and God may give the first while leaving the feared event in place. Assistance honors human weakness by sustaining it rather than pretending it has no limits.

Christ needed no correction or moral improvement. The strengthening served His human endurance within the saving mission He freely embraced, and it reveals the Father’s care at the very moment when obedience did not take the form of rescue.

This pattern helps us recognize grace without lowering our prayer. We may still ask for the cup to pass, and if strength arrives instead, receiving it does not mean the original desire was faithless. The gift answers the person even when it leaves the circumstance unchanged.

I have seen a lesser reflection of this grace in hospitals and at gravesides. The feared event still comes, yet strength appears that no one thought to ask for. A family is still standing. A widow completes the burial. If the only test is whether the suffering was removed, such grace goes unnoticed.

Christ’s agony deepened, and His prayer became more earnest. Anguish does not prove a lack of faith. Jesus trusted the Father perfectly while experiencing dread in His human soul and body, and serenity is not the only emotional form of trust.

Anxiety can tempt us to reclaim control and distrust God’s care, yet anguish brought to the Father stays within faith. Gethsemane makes room for believers who kneel frightened and physically overwhelmed; they need not imitate a calm they do not possess.

Hebrews tells us directly whether Christ was heard:

In the days of his flesh, Jesus offered up prayers and supplications, with loud cries and tears, to him who was able to save him from death, and he was heard for his godly fear. Although he was a Son, he learned obedience through what he suffered. (Hebrews 5:7‑8)

Christ was heard, and the cup remained. Hebrews keeps both truths together. The Father saved the Son from death through death, and the answer passed through crucifixion, burial, and the sealed tomb into Resurrection.

This is the mystery at the heart of the gospel. The Son freely offered Himself, entered death, and rose into indestructible life. The Father did not will betrayal, torture, or murder as moral evils; human agents freely committed those sins. God willed salvation through the Son’s obedient love and brought the greatest good from humanity’s worst act.

The Resurrection is the Father’s vindication of the crucified Son and the beginning of renewed creation, not a compensation for suffering. Death is overcome from within, because the One who entered it could not be held by it (cf. Acts 2:24). The answer exceeds the request without making the request dishonest.

If the cup had passed, Christ would have been spared the Passion. Because He freely drank it, humanity was reconciled to God and death was conquered. Risen life entered history through that obedience. We say this with trembling because the good achieved never makes betrayal and murder good. Redemption displays God’s power to defeat evil without becoming its author.

Answered prayer must therefore be read within the whole movement from cross to resurrection. On Thursday night the prayer looks refused. Friday is catastrophe, and Saturday shows nothing at all, a sealed tomb. Only from Sunday can you see that the answer was already underway through the hours when nothing looked answered.

Hebrews says that the Son “learned obedience” through suffering. The eternal Son did not move from disobedience to obedience or from ignorance to moral knowledge; in His humanity He lived obedience under anguish, suffering, and death, conditions He had not previously experienced. What He eternally shared with the Father became a fully human act within history.

The Father publicly named Jesus His beloved Son (Matthew 3:17), and that perfect love did not spare the Beloved from suffering. It held unbroken through the Passion and brought Him through death. At a bedside we are tempted to treat refusal as evidence that we are loved less, and Gethsemane will not let us do that arithmetic. Different outcome does not equal different love.

This truth does not tell us why a particular prayer is refused, but it tells us who is refusing it. The same Father who loved the Son through Gethsemane receives those joined to the Son within that same filial communion.

Union with Christ gives believers more than an analogy. Through grace they share in His relation to the Father, and the Spirit of adoption teaches them to cry “Abba” within their own fear. Their prayer is taken into the Son’s prayer and carried by His perfect obedience.

We must preserve the difference. Christ is the Son by nature; we become children by grace. His Passion redeems ours, and ours adds nothing lacking to His sacrifice. Yet He truly permits us to share in His filial trust and, in our own suffering, to know the fellowship of His sufferings (Philippians 3:10).

This confidence is not something we deduce from how our circumstances are going. God has revealed it. The Cross and Resurrection give divine love its definitive form, a self‑gift stronger than sin and death.

✦ ✦ ✦

SOONER OR LATER THE cup we are praying about does not move. What do we do then? I have long described three responses, which I call fistful, fateful, and faithful surrender.

Fistful surrender yields to power while accusing God’s character. It says, “You could have acted differently, so I will obey like a prisoner and withhold my heart.” This response still believes God exists, but the relationship has hardened into resentment.

The fist often begins as pain that never found a place to speak, and we should meet it first with compassion. Anger can be brought into lament and reopened to God; the danger comes when anger settles into a permanent interpretation that refuses every evidence of mercy.

Repentance from fistful surrender releases the final accusation and asks God to judge what the sufferer cannot; it does not require denying the wound. The clenched hand may open slowly, and grace is patient with that movement.

The Psalms contain fierce protest, and honest anger is still prayer while it addresses God and waits for Him. Fistful surrender closes the case. A person may continue attending, serving, and singing while inwardly refusing further trust.

Fateful surrender gives the outcome to impersonal necessity. It may look peaceful and say, “Whatever will be, will be.” Beneath the calm lies despair. The fatalist stops asking because no personal providence is left to receive the request. Fate never hears a request, so it can neither refuse it nor love the one who makes it.

Providence differs from fate because providence belongs to the living God, who holds creation in wisdom and directs history toward His Kingdom without reducing persons to pieces in a machine. Events are still contingent, creatures are still free, and evil is still evil.

Faithful surrender can therefore grieve and act. We seek treatment, resist injustice, protect the vulnerable, and ask for change. Fate says action is meaningless, but providence gives action a place within God’s care even when success is uncertain.

Faithful surrender keeps desire, anguish, and the Father. “Abba” begins the prayer, “Yours be done” completes it, and the whole struggle stays inside communion. Christ’s human will, neither erased nor coerced, freely consents in filial obedience to the divine saving will He shares eternally with the Father and the Holy Spirit.

Jesus entrusts the outcome and rises from prayer. Surrender becomes obedience you can watch, not an inward feeling or a fine sentence.

Faithful surrender also receives its strength from Christ, for we do not reproduce Gethsemane through willpower. The Spirit joins us to the obedient Son and forms His trust within us. Our consent may be small, and it may have to be given again in fear.

This explains why surrender often needs to be renewed. A decision made in prayer meets new facts, bodily exhaustion, and waves of grief. Each return places the will again before the Father. Repeating the consent is not failure; it is faithfulness.

The goal is communion. Emotional resignation falls silent, but faithful surrender keeps speaking to God. It still listens to Scripture and acts in love, and it hands over control while remaining personally engaged with the One who receives the gift.

Jesus woke His friends and walked toward the torches. Faithful surrender can move forward without a forecast, because it trusts a Person. The centurion at Capernaum described faith through life under authority, and Christian obedience likewise acts without possessing the whole plan.

The military image has limits, for the Father does not treat His children as expendable troops, and Christ Himself is the Son who leads by giving His life. Our obedience is filial before it is martial. We move under the authority of the One whose commands arise from holy love.

During the week my loved one died, I moved through all three responses, sometimes within one hour. Some prayers held a hidden fist. At other times I became numb and muttered, “Whatever happens, happens.” Then grace gave moments of faithful surrender that I could not manufacture.

At the funeral I stood as a clergyman and said the familiar Christian words with conviction, and I still regard that as a quiet grace. Jesus returned to prayer a second and third time, and we also return because consent must be renewed within changing pain.

For two days the disciples understood the garden as the beginning of catastrophe; after the Resurrection they reread the same events within God’s victory. The betrayal and death were still evil, but their place in the story changed, because resurrection revealed what divine love had accomplished through Christ’s obedience.

I have slowly reread the week my loved one died. The room is still painful, but I now see graces that grief first hid. People kept faith with us from far away. Those who loved him gathered. Truth was spoken through tears, and there was enough strength to get through the funeral. These do not turn death into the answer we asked for.

They show that God was acting within the refusal. Some answers are recognized only after resurrection light changes our reading, and that recognition must never be forced upon the newly grieving. It comes, when it comes, as grace.
