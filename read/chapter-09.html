Part III · The Answer

Chapter Nine

The Prayer at the Center

✦ ✦ ✦

≈ 12 min read

THIS BOOK RESTS UPON two facts. In Gethsemane, Jesus asked the Father to let the cup pass. The cup remained, and Scripture says that He was heard. These truths reveal the center of Christian prayer: a refused request can remain fully heard within unbroken communion. I know how much that costs to believe beside a dying person.

Gethsemane means “oil press.” The garden lay on the lower slope of the Mount of Olives across the valley from Jerusalem. Olives were crushed there until they released their oil. Jesus went after the Passover meal because prayer in that place was His habit. Judas knew where to find Him. Eight disciples remained near the entrance. Three went farther and fell asleep. Jesus continued alone.

Matthew writes, “He went a little farther and fell on His face, and prayed, saying, ‘O My Father, if it is possible, let this cup pass from Me; nevertheless, not as I will, but as You will’” (Matthew 26:39 NKJV). The eternal Son prayed with a fully human mind, heart, and will. He fell upon His face and asked that the cup pass. Within hours, soldiers entered the garden. By the next morning, He was crucified.

Measured only by the immediate request, the prayer appears unanswered. The sinless Son prayed with perfect faith, pure desire, and complete persistence. Every attempt to blame an ungranted prayer on moral or spiritual failure ends here.

The Gospels preserve the scene at the turning point of redemption. Christian worship has repeated it for centuries. Prayer cannot be defined only by obtaining the requested result because Christ’s own prayer would expose the definition as false. Every theology of answered prayer must stand among these trees.

Gethsemane became more than a passage when the man who had been like a father to me died. Family members across time zones prayed. Friends prayed, congregations prayed, and strangers fasted. Messages arrived each day. His condition worsened, and he died.

Years as a clergyman gave me language for the pain without reducing it. That week I still had to open Scripture before a congregation. I told them that we had prayed and that I was grieving. I also admitted what many believers fear saying aloud: God seemed not to have answered.

One truth kept me standing. Jesus never taught us to pray that our will be done. He taught, “Your will be done” (Matthew 6:10), then prayed those words when obedience carried an unimaginable cost. During that week, my heart had mostly prayed, “Let my will be done.” I resented the difference for years before I began to receive surrender as mercy.

Surrender does not make human desire sinful or unreal. Christ’s desire to be spared suffering belonged to His sinless human will. Obedience brought that desire into complete filial consent to the Father’s saving will. Christian surrender follows His pattern without pretending that our motives possess His purity.

The distinction between Christ’s human will and divine will matters. Jesus is one divine Person, the eternal Son, who assumed a complete human nature. That humanity includes a real will capable of choosing human goods. Life, friendship, bodily integrity, and freedom from torture are genuine goods. His desire to avoid the cup therefore reveals the truth of His humanity.

His human will never rebels against God. It experiences the cost of obedience and freely consents. The prayer allows us to see that consent taking place within anguish. Salvation is accomplished through a human obedience perfectly united to the divine will, an obedience offered on behalf of humanity from within our condition.

This gives Christian surrender dignity. Grace does not destroy the human will and substitute divine force. It heals freedom so that a person can offer himself in love. Our surrender remains imperfect, mixed, and dependent upon mercy, yet Christ has opened the path of truly human obedience.

When I was young, a preacher asked whether we would continue following Jesus if our prayers were never answered as expected. Immersed in prosperity teaching, I answered inwardly, “Why follow a god who cannot answer prayer?” Many believers carry a quieter version of the same question.

We can construct a Messiah who keeps the healings, multiplied bread, calmed storm, and raised child while losing the road to Jerusalem. Such a figure bears the name Jesus but differs from the Christ of the Gospels. He gives victory without discipleship and resurrection without the Cross.

Jesus walked toward Jerusalem knowingly. Mark says those following were amazed and afraid. Two disciples still asked for places of honor. Jesus answered, “You do not know what you ask. Are you able to drink the cup that I drink, and be baptized with the baptism that I am baptized with?” (Mark 10:38).

Discipleship follows Christ rather than directing Him. We go behind the One who passes through suffering into glory. This does not mean God positively wills every harm His people endure. It means no disciple can define divine faithfulness in a way that excludes the path Christ Himself walked.

In the prophets, the cup can signify judgment and suffering (Isaiah 51:17; Psalm 75:8). Jesus knew the scriptural weight of His words. He entered the garden knowing what awaited Him and asked the Father, with the trust of the Son, whether another way was possible.

✦ ✦ ✦

LUKE PRESERVES THE BODILY details of the night:

And He was withdrawn from them about a stone’s throw, and He knelt down and prayed, saying, “Father, if it is Your will, take this cup away from Me; nevertheless not My will, but Yours, be done.” Then an angel appeared to Him from heaven, strengthening Him. And being in agony, He prayed more earnestly. Then His sweat became like great drops of blood falling down to the ground. (Luke 22:41‑44)

Jesus withdrew only a stone’s throw. He knelt and named the desire directly: “take this cup away from Me.” The asking and surrender belong to one prayer. Honest desire does not compete with obedience. Mark adds the address “Abba” (Mark 14:36). The most terrible petition in Scripture begins with filial intimacy.

An angel came from heaven and strengthened Him. Heaven answered before the cup moved, and the cup never moved. Strength did not make suffering unreal. It enabled Christ’s human nature to continue freely along the path of obedience.

The angel’s presence shows that assistance and deliverance are different gifts. We usually ask for the second. God may give the first while the feared event remains. Assistance honors human weakness by sustaining it rather than pretending it has no limits.

Christ did not need correction or moral improvement. The strengthening served His human endurance within the saving mission He freely embraced. It also reveals the Father’s care at the very moment when obedience did not take the form of rescue.

This pattern helps us recognize grace without lowering our prayer. We may still ask for the cup to pass. If strength arrives instead, receiving it does not mean the original desire was faithless. The gift answers the person even when it leaves the circumstance unchanged.

I have seen a lesser reflection of this grace in hospitals and at gravesides. The event feared still comes, yet strength appears that no one thought to request. A family remains standing. A widow completes the burial. Measured only by removal of suffering, such grace goes unnoticed.

Christ’s agony deepened, and His prayer became more earnest. Anguish does not prove a lack of faith. Jesus trusted the Father perfectly while experiencing dread in His human soul and body. Serenity is not the only emotional form of trust.

Anxiety can tempt us to reclaim control and distrust God’s care. Yet anguish brought to the Father remains within faith. Gethsemane makes room for believers who kneel frightened and physically overwhelmed. They need not imitate calm they do not possess.

Hebrews tells us directly whether Christ was heard:

who, in the days of His flesh, when He had offered up prayers and supplications, with vehement cries and tears to Him who was able to save Him from death, and was heard because of His godly fear, though He was a Son, yet He learned obedience by the things which He suffered. (Hebrews 5:7‑8)

Christ was heard, and the cup remained. Hebrews keeps both truths together. The Father saved the Son from death through death. The answer passed through crucifixion, burial, and the sealed tomb into Resurrection.

This is the Paschal mystery. The Son freely offered Himself, entered death, and rose into indestructible life. The Father did not will betrayal, torture, or murder as moral evils. Human agents freely committed those sins. God willed salvation through the Son’s obedient love and brought the greatest good from humanity’s worst act.

The Resurrection is more than compensation after suffering. It is the Father’s vindication of the crucified Son and the beginning of renewed creation. Death is overcome from within because the One who entered it could not be held by it (cf. Acts 2:24). The answer exceeds the request without making the request dishonest.

If the cup had passed, Christ would have been spared the Passion. Because He freely drank it, humanity received reconciliation, death was conquered, and risen life entered history. We say this with trembling because the good achieved never makes betrayal and murder good. Redemption displays God’s power to defeat evil without becoming its author.

Answered prayer must therefore be read within the whole Paschal movement. Thursday night alone looks like refusal. Friday looks like catastrophe. Saturday offers only a sealed tomb. Sunday reveals an answer already being accomplished through the hours when nothing looked answered.

Hebrews says that the Son “learned obedience” through suffering. The eternal Son did not move from disobedience to obedience or ignorance to moral knowledge. In His humanity, He lived obedience under conditions He had not previously experienced: anguish, suffering, and death. What He eternally shared with the Father became a fully human act within history.

The Father publicly named Jesus His beloved Son (Matthew 3:17). Perfect love did not spare the Beloved from suffering. It remained unbroken through the suffering and brought Him through death. At a bedside, we are tempted to treat refusal as evidence that we are loved less. Gethsemane forbids that calculation. Different outcome does not equal different love.

This truth does not explain why a particular prayer is refused. It gives us the character of the relationship within which the mystery remains. The Father who loved the Son through Gethsemane receives those joined to the Son within that same filial communion.

Union with Christ gives believers more than an analogy. Through grace, they share in His relation to the Father. The Spirit of adoption teaches them to cry “Abba” within their own fear. Their prayer is taken into the Son’s prayer and carried by His perfect obedience.

We must preserve the difference. Christ is the Son by nature; we become children by grace. His Passion redeems ours; ours adds nothing lacking to His sacrifice. Yet He truly permits us to participate in His filial trust and to unite unavoidable suffering to His self‑offering.

This truth rests in revelation rather than inference from changing circumstances. The Cross and Resurrection give divine love its definitive form: self‑gift stronger than sin and death.

✦ ✦ ✦

EVERY BELIEVER EVENTUALLY FACES a cup that does not move. I have long described three possible responses: fistful, fateful, and faithful surrender.

Fistful surrender yields to power while accusing God’s character. It says, “You could have acted differently, so I will obey like a prisoner and withhold my heart.” This response still believes God exists, but relationship has hardened into resentment.

The fist often begins as pain that received no safe place to speak. We should meet it first with compassion. Anger can be brought into lament and reopened to God. The danger comes when anger becomes a permanent interpretation that refuses every evidence of mercy.

Repentance from fistful surrender does not require denying the wound. It releases the final accusation and asks God to judge what the sufferer cannot. The clenched hand may open slowly. Grace is patient with that movement.

The Psalms contain fierce protest. Honest anger remains prayer while it still addresses God and waits for Him. Fistful surrender closes the case. A person may continue attending, serving, and singing while inwardly refusing further trust.

Fateful surrender gives the outcome to impersonal necessity. It may look peaceful and say, “Whatever will be, will be.” Beneath the calm lies despair. The fatalist stops asking because no personal providence remains to receive the request. Fate cannot break a heart through refusal, and it cannot love.

Providence differs from fate because providence belongs to the living God. Events remain contingent, creatures remain free, and evil remains evil. God holds creation in wisdom and directs history toward His Kingdom without reducing persons to pieces in a machine.

Faithful acceptance can therefore grieve and act. We seek treatment, resist injustice, protect the vulnerable, and ask for change. Fate says action is meaningless. Providence gives action a place within God’s care even when success is uncertain.

Faithful surrender keeps desire, anguish, and the Father. “Abba” begins the prayer, and “Yours be done” completes it. The entire struggle remains inside communion. Christ’s human will is neither erased nor coerced. It freely consents in filial obedience to the divine saving will He shares eternally with the Father and the Holy Spirit.

Jesus entrusts the outcome and rises from prayer. Surrender becomes embodied obedience. It does not remain an inward feeling or a sentence admired for its beauty.

Faithful surrender also receives its strength from Christ. We do not reproduce Gethsemane through willpower. The Spirit joins us to the obedient Son and forms His trust within us. Our consent may be small, repeated, and accompanied by fear.

This explains why surrender often needs to be renewed. A decision made in prayer meets new facts, bodily exhaustion, and waves of grief. Each return places the will again before the Father. Repetition is fidelity working through time.

The goal is communion rather than emotional resignation. Faithful surrender keeps speaking to God, listening to Scripture, and acting in love. It hands over control while remaining personally engaged with the One who receives the gift.

Jesus woke His friends and walked toward the torches. Faithful surrender can move forward because it trusts a Person rather than a prediction. The centurion at Capernaum described faith through life under authority. Christian obedience likewise acts without possessing the whole plan.

The military image has limits. The Father does not treat His children as expendable troops. Christ Himself is the Son who leads by giving His life. Our obedience is filial before it is martial: we move under the authority of the One whose commands arise from holy love.

During the week of the death, I moved through all three responses, sometimes within one hour. Some prayers held a hidden fist. At other times I became numb and muttered, “Whatever happens, happens.” Then grace gave moments of faithful surrender that I could not manufacture.

At the funeral, I stood as a clergyman and said the familiar Christian words with conviction. I still regard that as a quiet grace. Surrender often needs repetition. Jesus returned to prayer a second and third time. We also return because consent must be renewed within changing pain.

For two days, the disciples understood the garden as the beginning of catastrophe. After the Resurrection, they reread the same events within God’s victory. The betrayal and death remained evil. Their place in the story changed because resurrection revealed what divine love had accomplished through Christ’s obedience.

I have slowly reread the week my loved one died. The room remains painful, but I now see graces that grief initially hid: faith shared across distance, people gathered in love, truth spoken through tears, and strength given for the funeral. These do not turn death into the answer we requested.

They show that God was acting within the refusal. Some answers are recognized only after resurrection light changes our reading. That recognition must never be forced upon the newly grieving. It comes, when it comes, as grace.
