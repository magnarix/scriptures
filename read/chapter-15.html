Chapter Fifteen

What Difference Has Prayer Actually Made?

✦ ✦ ✦

≈ 9 min read

A FRIEND SOMETIMES ASKS what difference prayer has made in my life. He does not pray, and the question contains no hostility. That makes it harder to dismiss. We have both worked, endured lean years, waited in hospitals, and buried people we loved. Our bread required the same labor. Our bodies are subject to the same weakness. If the road looks so similar, what has prayer changed?

Believers ask this too, usually in quieter voices. Some have prayed faithfully for decades and feel guilty for raising the question. They should not. Honest faith does not fear honest examination.

The question begins with a truth. Prayer does not remove us from creaturely life. We still earn bread, make uncertain decisions, age, and die. A praying farmer and an unpraying farmer wait beneath the same sky. A believer’s diagnosis is printed in the same clinical language as anyone else’s. Jesus never promised exemption from labor, uncertainty, or loss. Teaching otherwise leaves people with a faith that collapses as soon as ordinary suffering arrives.

In college, I once prayed aloud for rain. The town had a dry season and a wet season, but that year’s dry season had become a drought. Water was scarce. Young and eager to test my faith, I stood beside the pipe that supplied the town and prayed, “In the Name of the Lord Jesus Christ, let there be rain!”

Nothing happened immediately. About thirty minutes later, rain fell for five minutes.

That evening my friends offered three explanations. One thought an airplane had seeded the clouds. Another called it coincidence. A third allowed that my prayer might have mattered. The same rain supported three interpretations, and no one’s mind changed.

I thanked God. The shower provided enough water for the dormitory to wash before church that Sunday. I did not need to prove a mechanism before giving thanks. Providence is often known personally without becoming available as a laboratory demonstration. Gratitude is a confession of trust, not a scientific claim that every causal question has been settled.

I take intellectual objections seriously because shallow answers once brought me near the loss of faith. In college, I asked who wrote the Letter to the Hebrews. No one around me could tell me. Since I had been taught that the Bible is God’s Word, I wanted to understand how an anonymous work could carry divine authority.

Instead of an answer, I received slogans: “Just believe.” “Do not lean on your own understanding.” “Questions like that come from the devil.” Scripture was used to end inquiry instead of guide it. The response insulted both intelligence and conscience while claiming God’s authority.

I stood over a trash bin with my Bible in my hand. I had concluded that a faith unable to survive an honest question had no claim on my honest life. Yet I did not let the Bible fall. Some part of me suspected that the failure belonged to the answerers, not to the Book.

That suspicion returned the Bible to the shelf. Returning it to my heart took much longer. I searched for people who allowed questions, and I discovered that many of mine had ancient homes. The Church had wrestled with authorship, inspiration, canon, and authority long before I asked. The authority of Hebrews does not depend on our ability to name its human author. It rests in God’s inspiration and in the book’s reception within the apostolic canon handed down by the Church.

I also learned that “I do not know” can be a faithful answer. Reverence does not require false certainty. Some questions receive substantial answers. Others remain open beneath the greater truths God has made clear.

Along that road, inherited faith became personal faith. As a boy, I had often recited prayer as a formality. After nearly losing faith, I spoke to God as Someone whose presence I had almost ceased to recognize. Prayer mattered differently because I understood what it meant to have Someone to address.

The slogans that failed me and the easy answers my friend distrusts share one weakness. They leave no room for a living questioner before the living God. Prayer makes its difference by opening a life that has become sealed within itself.

Without prayer, effort can become an attempt at self‑creation. Every success must prove our worth. Every failure threatens our identity. Work must accomplish its ordinary purpose and answer the deeper question of whether we deserve to exist. Success becomes too important to enjoy, and failure becomes too personal to absorb.

When everything rests on what we can secure, anxiety follows logically. We must become our own foundation, verdict, and protection. Psalm 127 describes the exhaustion of that life:

Unless the LORD builds the house,
They labor in vain who build it;
Unless the LORD guards the city,
The watchman stays awake in vain.
It is vain for you to rise up early,
To sit up late,
To eat the bread of sorrows;
For so He gives His beloved sleep. (Psalm 127:1‑2 NKJV)

The psalm does not condemn work. The builders keep building, and the watchman remains at his post. What becomes vain is work required to guarantee what only God can secure. Human responsibility is real, but it is never sovereignty.

The final gift is sleep. We can compel ourselves to keep working, but we cannot force ourselves to sleep. Sleep requires surrender. Each night the body confesses that the world continues under God’s care while we are unconscious.

Prayer restores this creaturely order. It removes desperation from responsibility. The person who prays still works, plans, risks, and builds. Prayer removes the hidden demand that every task establish personal worth. In Christ, identity precedes achievement. We work as people already received by grace.

That change does not weaken effort. It turns work into offering. The field is still plowed, but it is plowed by a child of God rather than a defendant trying to earn a favorable verdict.

Some years ago, I gave sustained effort to work I believed God had asked me to do. The results were meager. I began to reason from the result to the work and from the work to myself: poor results meant poor work, and poor work meant a worthless worker.

One morning I prayed, “Lord, I did what I could. You saw the hours. I cannot make it succeed, and I do not believe You will judge me for what was never in my hands. It is Yours. It was always Yours.”

The visible result did not change. The work remained meager. What changed was my relation to it. I could offer the labor to God without forcing it to vindicate me. That night I slept. The miracle was inward, but it was real.

Paul can therefore speak of intense effort and complete dependence in the same sentence:

But by the grace of God I am what I am, and His grace toward me was not in vain; but I labored more abundantly than they all, yet not I, but the grace of God which was with me. (1 Corinthians 15:10)

Paul truly labored. He traveled, suffered, made tents, preached, and wrote. Grace did not replace his agency. It awakened, sustained, and directed it. Christian tradition calls this cooperation with grace. God acts in us in such a way that our action becomes fully alive, not less human.

Paul refuses to treat his labor as self‑originating. “Yet not I,” he says, and gives glory to the grace that worked with him. Both claims are true: Paul labored, and grace made the labor fruitful. Dependence does not produce passivity. It frees energy that would otherwise be spent defending the self.

The same distinction helps with anxiety. Scripture says, “The effective, fervent prayer of a righteous man avails much” (James 5:16). The psalmist cries to God in distress (Psalm 18:6). Jesus Himself prayed in agony, with sweat like drops of blood (Luke 22:44). Faith is not emotional numbness. Yet Paul also writes:

Be anxious for nothing, but in everything by prayer and supplication, with thanksgiving, let your requests be made known to God; and the peace of God, which surpasses all understanding, will guard your hearts and minds through Christ Jesus. (Philippians 4:6‑7)

The first movement of anxiety can be involuntary. Bodies and minds may sound an alarm before the will has chosen anything. Such distress is not itself a sin or proof of weak faith. The question is what we do with it.

Fervent prayer carries distress toward God. Worry, in the sense at issue here, repeatedly tries to recover control that belongs to God. The two may look equally intense, but they move in different directions. Prayer entrusts; worry attempts to govern.

I struggle deeply with Paul’s command. My working life has included layoffs, long gaps, and uncertain seasons. Anxiety often speaks to me in the language of someday: when work is secure, I will rest; when debts are paid, I will be generous; when the calendar clears, I will live.

Someday remains one crisis away. This is a false theology of time. It makes present faithfulness wait for a future we do not control. Worry forces us to suffer tomorrow before tomorrow arrives, without the grace God will give if its troubles actually come. A person may be fed, sheltered, and loved while living inwardly like a city under siege.

I may hand a matter to God in the morning and take it back by afternoon. When I notice, I name the act: I am trying to govern my life again. Then I surrender it again. Some days this happens five times. God has never met the fifth return with less patience than the first.

Paul promises that God’s peace will guard our hearts and minds. Guard is a military word. The peace is not something we manufacture by mastering our emotions. It is God’s own provision, standing watch over a life entrusted to Him.

I can now answer my friend. Prayer may leave many outward features of life unchanged. The person who prays still rises for work, faces uncertain weather, and stands at gravesides. Yet beneath that surface, prayer changes the structure of endurance. It places labor and suffering within communion with God.

The deepest weight of striving is often the suspicion that we carry it alone. Prayer answers that loneliness before it changes a circumstance. Jesus speaks directly to burdened people:

Come to Me, all you who labor and are heavy laden, and I will give you rest. Take My yoke upon you and learn from Me, for I am gentle and lowly in heart, and you will find rest for your souls. For My yoke is easy and My burden is light. (Matthew 11:28‑30)

Jesus offers rest, then gives a yoke. A yoke is an instrument of work. His rest is therefore deeper than inactivity. It is life and labor shared with Him. The field remains, but we no longer enter it alone.

We should not press the image beyond Jesus’ words or turn it into a sentimental promise that suffering will feel light. Some burdens remain crushing. The promise is that Christ is gentle and lowly, that He receives the burdened, and that discipleship under His rule leads to rest for the soul. His presence changes suffering because love now bears us within it.

Grief still wounds, but it is brought into communion. Success can be enjoyed without becoming our foundation. The future remains uncertain, but it is no longer empty. God is present there before we arrive.

None of this can be photographed. From outside, the road may look unchanged. From within, it is the difference between solitude and communion, self‑creation and grace, fear‑driven work and faithful offering. A road walked with Christ is a different road even when every stone remains in place.

This leads to the largest question in the book. If prayer can do its deepest work before any circumstance changes, what do we mean when we call a prayer answered? The final chapter turns to that question.
